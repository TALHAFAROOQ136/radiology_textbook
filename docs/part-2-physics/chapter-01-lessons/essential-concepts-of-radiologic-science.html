Core Concepts of Radiologic Science

Radiologic science is the discipline that applies the principles of physics, biology, and technology to the production and interpretation of medical images. It is the foundation upon which all diagnostic imaging is built — from a simple chest X-ray to a complex PET-CT scan.

  • Define radiologic science and describe its major branches
  • Distinguish between ionising and non-ionising radiation
  • Identify the key units of measurement used in radiologic practice
  • Explain the ALARA principle and the three pillars of radiation protection
  • Understand the inverse square law and its clinical relevance
  • Describe the fundamental determinants of radiographic image quality
  • Recognise the roles of professionals within the radiologic team

What is Radiologic Science?​

Radiologic science is a multi-disciplinary field that combines the principles of physics, human biology, and engineering technology to produce and interpret medical images. It encompasses all aspects of diagnostic imaging, radiation therapy, and radiation protection.

The discipline rests on three inseparable foundations — collectively known as the Radiologic Science Triad:

  1. Physics — the behaviour of radiation and its interaction with matter
  2. Biology — how living tissue responds to radiation exposure
  3. Technology — the equipment, detectors, and image-processing systems used in imaging

Branches of Radiologic Science​

BranchCore FunctionClinical Examples
Diagnostic RadiologyImage-based disease detection and diagnosisX-ray, CT, MRI, Ultrasound
Interventional RadiologyImage-guided therapeutic proceduresAngioplasty, biopsy, drainage
Nuclear MedicineRadioactive tracers for diagnosis and treatmentPET, SPECT, thyroid scan

Radiologic Science is the applied science concerned with the use of ionising and non-ionising radiation for the diagnosis and treatment of disease, combined with the protection of patients, staff, and the public from unnecessary radiation exposure.

A Brief History of Radiologic Science​

The field of radiologic science has evolved remarkably over 130 years — from the accidental discovery of X-rays to AI-guided imaging.

Key Milestones​

  • 1895 — Wilhelm Conrad Röntgen discovers X-rays at the University of Würzburg, Germany. He images his wife's hand — the first medical X-ray. Awarded the first Nobel Prize in Physics (1901).
  • 1896 — X-rays applied clinically within weeks: fracture detection, foreign body localisation.
  • 1898 — Marie and Pierre Curie discover polonium and radium; introduce the term radioactivity. Marie Curie is the only person to win Nobel Prizes in two sciences (Physics 1903, Chemistry 1911).
  • 1913 — William Coolidge invents the hot-cathode X-ray tube — the direct ancestor of the modern X-ray tube.
  • 1920s — Contrast agents (barium sulphate, iodine) introduced for gastrointestinal and vascular imaging.
  • 1950s — Nuclear medicine emerges with radioactive iodine for thyroid disease.
  • 1971 — Sir Godfrey Hounsfield develops Computed Tomography (CT). Nobel Prize awarded 1979.
  • 1973 — Paul Lauterbur demonstrates the first MRI image.
  • 1980s — Digital radiography begins to replace film-screen systems.
  • 1990s — Spiral CT, PET scanning, and PACS (Picture Archiving and Communication Systems) emerge.
  • 2000s — Multi-detector CT (64+ slice), 3T MRI, and digital mammography become standard.
  • 2010s–present — Artificial intelligence, photon-counting CT, and ultra-high-field MRI (7T).

Marie Curie not only discovered two elements (polonium and radium) but also established mobile X-ray units during World War I — known as petites Curies — bringing imaging directly to the battlefield. Her extraordinary contribution to medicine came at a personal cost: she died of aplastic anaemia in 1934, almost certainly caused by decades of unprotected radiation exposure.

Types of Radiation​

Ionising Radiation​

Ionising radiation carries sufficient energy to remove electrons from atoms, producing ion pairs. This process can damage biological molecules — particularly DNA — and is the basis of both the diagnostic utility and the biological risk of radiation.

Classification of ionising radiation:

Beta (β)β⁻ or β⁺High-speed electron or positronLow–moderatePlastic, glass, thin aluminium
Gamma (γ)γHigh-energy electromagnetic photonHighLead, dense concrete
X-ray—Electromagnetic photonHighLead, dense concrete
NeutronnNeutral particleVery highWater, polyethylene, borated materials

Non-Ionising Radiation​

Non-ionising radiation lacks sufficient energy to remove electrons. It is used in several important imaging modalities without producing ion pairs.

TypeMedical UseModality
Radiofrequency (RF)Excites hydrogen protonsMRI
UltrasoundMechanical pressure waves — pulse-echo imagingDiagnostic ultrasound
Visible lightIllumination and detectionEndoscopy, microscopy
InfraredTemperature mappingThermography

X-rays and gamma rays are physically identical — both are electromagnetic photons. They differ only in their origin: X-rays are produced by electron transitions (in X-ray tubes), while gamma rays originate from nuclear disintegration (radioactive decay). This distinction is important in radiation protection and nuclear medicine.

The Electromagnetic Spectrum​

Electromagnetic (EM) radiation consists of oscillating electric and magnetic fields propagating through space at the speed of light (c = 3 × 10⁸ m/s).

Fundamental relationships:

  • Wave equation: c = f × λ
  • Photon energy: E = hf = hc/λ
    • h = Planck's constant = 6.626 × 10⁻³⁴ J·s
    • f = frequency (Hz)
    • λ = wavelength (m)

As frequency increases, wavelength decreases and photon energy increases.

RegionWavelengthPhoton EnergyMedical Application
Radiofrequency> 1 mm< 1 meVMRI — proton excitation
Microwave1 mm – 1 m~meVDiathermy
Infrared700 nm – 1 mm0.001 – 2 eVThermography
Visible light400 – 700 nm1.8 – 3.1 eVEndoscopy
Ultraviolet10 – 400 nm3 – 120 eVPhototherapy
X-rays0.001 – 10 nm0.1 – 120 keVRadiography, CT, fluoroscopy
Gamma rays< 0.01 nm> 100 keVNuclear medicine, radiotherapy

Mnemonic for the EM spectrum (low to high energy): Radio → Microwave → Infrared → Visible → Ultraviolet → X-rays → Gamma

"Real Men In Vegas Use X-ray Glasses"

Ionising radiation begins at the ultraviolet end of the spectrum and includes X-rays and gamma rays.

Effective dose (mSv) is the most clinically useful quantity — it allows comparison of radiation risk between different types of examination (e.g., chest X-ray vs CT chest) and against natural background radiation. It takes into account both the type of radiation and the sensitivity of the tissues irradiated.

Ionisation and Excitation​

When radiation interacts with matter, two fundamentally different processes can occur:

Ionisation​

Radiation transfers sufficient energy to completely eject an electron from an atom:

  • The atom becomes a positive ion (lost an electron)
  • The ejected electron may be captured by a neutral atom to form a negative ion
  • The resulting ion pair (positive ion + free electron) is chemically reactive
  • Ion pairs can break chemical bonds and damage DNA

Excitation​

Radiation transfers energy to an electron, raising it to a higher energy shell without ejection:

  • The atom enters an excited state
  • Returns to the ground state by emitting a lower-energy photon (fluorescence)
  • Less biologically damaging than ionisation
  • Exploited in intensifying screens and scintillator detectors

Ionisation is the process by which an atom or molecule acquires a positive or negative charge by gaining or losing electrons. In the context of radiobiology, ionisation is the primary mechanism by which radiation damages biological molecules, including DNA.

The ALARA Principle and Radiation Protection​

ALARA — As Low As Reasonably Achievable​

ALARA is the guiding principle of radiation protection across all areas of radiologic practice. It requires that radiation doses be kept as low as reasonably achievable, taking into account social and economic factors.

The Three Pillars of Radiation Protection​

PrincipleMechanismPractical Application
TimeReduce duration of exposureEfficient technique; minimise fluoroscopy time; pre-plan procedures
DistanceIncrease distance from radiation sourceStep back during exposure; stand behind protective screens
ShieldingPlace attenuating material between source and personLead aprons, thyroid shields, lead glass, room shielding

The Inverse Square Law​

The intensity of radiation decreases with the square of the distance from the source:

Intensity ∝ 1 / Distance²

Distance from sourceRelative Dose
1 m100%
2 m25% (reduced by 75%)
3 m11% (reduced by 89%)
4 m6.25% (reduced by 94%)

The inverse square law has profound practical implications. A radiographer standing just 2 metres from a fluoroscopy patient receives only 25% of the dose compared to standing at 1 metre. During cardiac catheterisation, the operator's dose can be dramatically reduced simply by stepping back during cine acquisition.

Justification and Optimisation​

Every radiation exposure must satisfy two key requirements:

Justification: The clinical benefit must outweigh the radiation risk.

  • Is this examination clinically indicated?
  • Will the result change patient management?
  • Could a non-ionising alternative (MRI, ultrasound) provide equivalent diagnostic information?

Optimisation: The minimum dose should be used to produce a diagnostically acceptable image.

  • Appropriate kVp and mAs selection
  • Automatic Exposure Control (AEC)
  • Collimation to restrict the beam to the area of interest
  • Use of iterative or AI-based reconstruction in CT

A chest X-ray delivers approximately 0.02 mSv — equivalent to the radiation received naturally over just three days. By contrast, a CT of the abdomen and pelvis delivers approximately 10 mSv — equivalent to 3.5 years of natural background radiation. This difference must be factored into clinical decision-making and patient communication.

Image Quality — The Five Fundamental Parameters​

The diagnostic value of any radiographic image depends on five key parameters:

1. Contrast​

The difference in optical density or signal intensity between adjacent structures. Without contrast, different tissues appear indistinguishable.

  • Subject contrast: Inherent attenuation difference between tissues
  • Receptor/image contrast: The system's ability to reproduce those differences
  • High contrast: bone vs air (easy to distinguish)
  • Low contrast: liver vs adjacent soft tissue (requires optimisation)

Factors that affect contrast: kVp, scatter radiation, collimation, contrast agents, detector type.

2. Spatial Resolution​

The ability to resolve two closely adjacent structures as distinct and separate.

  • Measured in line pairs per millimetre (lp/mm)
  • Affected by: focal spot size, detector pixel size, geometric magnification, motion
ModalityTypical Spatial Resolution
Screen-film radiography5 – 10 lp/mm
Digital radiography (DR)3 – 5 lp/mm
Computed Tomography (CT)0.5 – 1.5 lp/mm
MRI0.5 – 2 lp/mm
Mammography (digital)10 – 20 lp/mm

3. Noise​

Random variation in image signal that appears as graininess or "quantum mottle."

  • Quantum noise: Too few photons reaching the detector (insufficient mAs)
  • Electronic noise: Amplifier and detector electronics
  • Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR): Higher SNR = better image quality
  • Noise increases at low doses — managed by iterative reconstruction in CT

4. Geometric Distortion​

Change in size or shape of the imaged structure compared to true anatomy.

  • Caused by: divergent X-ray beam (magnification), patient positioning, curved image receptors
  • Magnification factor = Source-to-Image Distance (SID) ÷ Source-to-Object Distance (SOD)

5. Artefacts​

Structures visible in the image that do not represent real anatomy.

  • Metal streak artefacts (CT, MRI)
  • Motion blur
  • Grid lines and moiré patterns
  • Beam hardening artefacts (CT)

Radiation Biology — An Introduction​

Understanding how radiation affects living tissue is fundamental to radiologic science.

Direct and Indirect Action​

Radiation can damage biological molecules by two mechanisms:

Direct action:

  • Radiation directly strikes a critical molecule (most often DNA)
  • Accounts for approximately 30% of X-ray biological damage
  • More important with densely ionising radiation (alpha particles, neutrons)

Indirect action:

  • Radiation ionises water molecules → highly reactive free radicals
  • Free radicals (particularly the hydroxyl radical, OH·) attack DNA
  • Accounts for approximately 70% of X-ray biological damage

Free radical formation:

H₂O + radiation → H₂O⁺ + e⁻ → H· + OH· (hydroxyl radical)

DNA Damage Types​

Damage TypeDescriptionRepairability
Single-strand break (SSB)One strand of the DNA double helix is brokenUsually repaired accurately using opposite strand as template
Double-strand break (DSB)Both strands broken at approximately the same locationPotentially irreparable; most critical lesion
Base damageChemical alteration of DNA basesOften repaired by base excision repair
Cross-linksDNA–DNA or DNA–protein cross-linksVariable repairability

Double-strand breaks (DSBs) are considered the most biologically significant form of DNA damage caused by ionising radiation. Unlike single-strand breaks — which can be repaired using the intact complementary strand as a template — DSBs may result in misrepair, chromosome aberrations, or cell death. The probability of DSB formation increases with dose and with linear energy transfer (LET).

Cell Responses to Radiation Damage​

ResponseDescriptionOutcome
Successful repairDNA repair enzymes restore integrityCell survives normally
Misrepair / mutationIncorrect repair of damageAltered cell — potential carcinogenesis
ApoptosisProgrammed cell death triggeredCell eliminated safely
Mitotic deathCell dies when attempting to divideParticularly common in rapidly dividing cells
SenescencePermanent arrest of cell divisionNon-dividing but metabolically active

Ethics and Professional Practice​

Core Ethical Principles in Radiology​

PrincipleApplication in Radiologic Practice
BeneficenceOnly perform examinations with a clear clinical indication; act in the patient's best interest
Non-maleficenceMinimise radiation dose; avoid unnecessary procedures; apply ALARA rigorously

Patient Safety Standards​

A safe radiologic examination requires:

  • Two-point patient identification before every examination
  • Screening for contraindications: pregnancy, renal impairment (for contrast), metal implants (for MRI), contrast allergy
  • Radiation justification — questioning every referral when clinical indication is unclear
  • ALARA application — optimising every exposure
  • Accurate dose documentation and incident reporting
  • A clean, safe, and respectful examination environment
  • Radiologic science unites physics, biology, and technology to produce and interpret medical images across six major branches.
  • Ionising radiation (X-rays, gamma rays, alpha, beta) carries sufficient energy to remove electrons and can damage DNA. Non-ionising radiation (RF, ultrasound) does not.
  • The key dosimetric units are: Gray (Gy) for absorbed dose, Sievert (Sv) for equivalent and effective dose, and Becquerel (Bq) for activity.
  • ALARA (As Low As Reasonably Achievable) is the governing principle of radiation protection, implemented through time, distance, and shielding.
  • The inverse square law states that radiation intensity decreases with the square of distance — doubling the distance reduces dose to one quarter.
  • Radiographic image quality is determined by five parameters: contrast, spatial resolution, noise, distortion, and artefacts.
  • X-rays cause approximately 70% of their biological damage indirectly through free radical (OH·) formation from water radiolysis.
  • Every imaging examination must be justified (net clinical benefit) and optimised (minimum dose for diagnostic quality).
