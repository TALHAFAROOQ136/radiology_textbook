Deterministic Effects of Radiation

Deterministic effects (also called tissue reactions) are radiation-induced injuries that occur above a threshold dose and whose severity increases with increasing dose. They result from the killing of large numbers of cells in a tissue or organ, exceeding the capacity for repair and repopulation.

Characteristics of Deterministic Effects​

FeatureDescription
Threshold doseMust be exceeded before effect occurs
SeverityIncreases with dose above threshold
CauseSubstantial cell killing in a tissue
PredictabilityPredictable in an irradiated individual
ExamplesErythema, epilation, cataract, infertility, ARS

Threshold Doses for Key Deterministic Effects​

Skin​

EffectThreshold DoseTime of Onset
Erythema (temporary redness)~2 GyHours to days
Epilation (hair loss)~3–5 Gy2–3 weeks
Dry desquamation~10 Gy4–6 weeks
Moist desquamation~15–20 Gy4–6 weeks
Necrosis / ulceration>25 GyMonths
Late telangiectasia>30 Gy (fractionated)Months–years

Skin effects are the most common deterministic injuries from fluoroscopy-guided interventional procedures.

Lens of the Eye​

EffectThresholdOnset
Detectable lens opacity~0.5 GyMonths–years
Visual impairment (cataract)~5 Gy (acute)Years

ICRP 2011 revised threshold: 0.5 Gy for lens opacity (reduced from previous 2 Gy), driving reduced occupational dose limit for the eye (20 mSv/year average).

Gonads​

EffectMale ThresholdFemale Threshold
Temporary infertility~0.15 Gy~0.65 Gy
Permanent sterility~3.5–6 Gy~2.5–6 Gy

Bone Marrow​

EffectThresholdOnset
Depression of blood counts~0.5 Gy2–4 weeks
Aplasia (severe)~2–3 Gy whole body2–4 weeks
LD₅₀/₆₀ (50% mortality without treatment)~3–4 Gy30–60 days

Acute Radiation Syndrome (ARS)​

ARS occurs after whole-body or large partial-body exposure to high doses, typically from radiation accidents or nuclear events (not diagnostic radiology).

ARS Phases​

  1. Prodromal phase (hours): Nausea, vomiting, diarrhoea, fatigue
  2. Latent phase (days–weeks): Apparent recovery
  3. Manifest illness (weeks): Organ system failure
  4. Recovery or death

ARS Syndromes by Dose​

SyndromeDose RangeDominant EffectOutcome
Haematopoietic1–6 GyBone marrow failureSurvivable with treatment
Gastrointestinal6–15 GyGI mucosal lossDeath within 2 weeks without treatment
Cerebrovascular>15–20 GyCNS/vascular failureDeath within hours–days

Organ-Specific Deterministic Effects in Radiotherapy​

In radiotherapy, high doses are deliberately delivered to tumours but nearby organs must be protected:

Lung (Radiation Pneumonitis)​

  • Threshold: ~20 Gy to significant lung volume
  • Acute (1–3 months): pneumonitis (cough, dyspnoea, fever)
  • Late (6+ months): fibrosis

Heart​

  • Pericarditis: ~40 Gy
  • Coronary artery disease: Risk increases after mediastinal irradiation
  • Cardiomyopathy: After high-dose cardiac irradiation

Kidney​

  • Radiation nephropathy: TD₅/₅ (5% complication in 5 years) ≈ 20 Gy
  • Hypertension, proteinuria, renal failure

Spinal Cord​

  • Radiation myelopathy (paralysis): TD₅/₅ ≈ 45 Gy (conventionally fractionated)
  • Critical structure limiting radiotherapy dose

Liver​

  • Radiation-induced liver disease (RILD): ~30–35 Gy mean liver dose

Deterministic Effects in Diagnostic Radiology​

Deterministic effects are not expected from routine diagnostic imaging because doses are well below thresholds. However, they can occur in:

Fluoroscopy-Guided Procedures​

High-dose interventional procedures can exceed skin dose thresholds:

ProcedureTypical Skin DoseRisk
Simple cardiac catheterisation~0.5 GyVery low
Complex PTCA1–2 GyLow
TIPS procedure2–5 GyErythema possible
Complex IR procedures>3–5 GyErythema; epilation risk

ICRP guidance: If peak skin dose may exceed 3 Gy, patient follow-up recommended to identify and manage skin injuries.

Neonatal and Paediatric CT​

  • Multiple CT examinations in critically ill neonates may approach lens dose thresholds
  • Active dose management essential

Management of Radiation Injuries​

Acute Skin Reactions​

  • Mild erythema: moisturising cream, avoid irritants
  • Moist desquamation: wound care, antimicrobials
  • Severe necrosis: surgical consultation, possible skin grafting

ARS Management​

  • Haematopoietic syndrome: supportive care, growth factors (G-CSF), bone marrow transplant
  • GI syndrome: fluid replacement, antimicrobials, bowel rest
  • Cytokine therapies and radioprotectors under investigation
