Health Physics

Health physics is the science concerned with the recognition, evaluation, and control of health hazards from ionising radiation. It provides the scientific and practical foundation for all radiation protection programmes in medical, industrial, and research settings.

Scope of Health Physics​

Health physics encompasses:

  • Radiation measurement and dosimetry
  • Biological effects of radiation
  • Radiation protection standards and regulations
  • Radioactive material handling and waste management
  • Emergency response to radiation incidents
  • Occupational and public dose management

Quantities and Units in Health Physics​

Absorbed Dose​

  • Energy deposited per unit mass of tissue
  • Unit: Gray (Gy) = 1 joule per kilogram
  • Old unit: rad (1 Gy = 100 rad)

Equivalent Dose​

Accounts for the biological effectiveness of different radiation types:

H = D × W_R

Radiation TypeWeighting Factor (W_R)
X-rays, gamma rays, electrons1
Protons2
Alpha particles20
Neutrons5–20 (energy dependent)
  • Unit: Sievert (Sv)
  • Old unit: rem (1 Sv = 100 rem)

Effective Dose​

Accounts for the sensitivity of different organs:

E = Σ (H_T × W_T)

TissueWeighting Factor (W_T)
Bone marrow, breast, colon, lung, stomach0.12 each
Gonads0.08
Bladder, liver, oesophagus, thyroid0.04 each
Bone surface, brain, salivary glands, skin0.01 each
  • Unit: Sievert (Sv)
  • Used for risk estimation and dose limit compliance

Exposure​

  • Ionisation produced in air by X/gamma radiation
  • Unit: Coulombs per kilogram (C/kg); old unit: Roentgen (R)
  • 1 R ≈ 0.00877 Gy in air

Activity​

  • Rate of radioactive decay
  • Unit: Becquerel (Bq) = 1 disintegration per second
  • Old unit: Curie (Ci); 1 Ci = 3.7 × 10¹⁰ Bq

Radiation Detection Instruments​

Ionisation Chamber​

  • Gas-filled detector; measures ionisation current
  • Accurate, stable — used for calibration and dose measurement
  • Survey meters: Cutie Pie, Farmer chamber
  • Dose area product (DAP) meter: Fixed to X-ray tube for patient dose monitoring

Geiger-Müller (GM) Counter​

  • Gas-filled tube; detects individual ionising events
  • Used for contamination surveys (nuclear medicine areas)
  • Not suitable for dose rate measurement at high intensities
  • Characteristic audible click per detected event

Scintillation Detector​

  • Crystal (NaI, CsI) converts radiation to light
  • Light detected by photomultiplier tube
  • Used for: gamma spectroscopy, thyroid uptake probes, gamma cameras
  • High sensitivity — detects very low activities

Semiconductor Detector​

  • Silicon or germanium crystal; direct charge production
  • Excellent energy resolution (gamma spectroscopy)
  • Requires cooling (germanium)

Thermoluminescent Dosimeter (TLD)​

  • Crystal (LiF, CaF₂) stores energy from radiation
  • Heated to release stored energy as light
  • Light proportional to dose received
  • Used for: personal dosimetry, patient dose measurement

Optically Stimulated Luminescence (OSL)​

  • Similar to TLD but read by laser light
  • More sensitive; can be re-read multiple times
  • Currently most common personal dosimeter

Radiation Survey and Monitoring​

Area Monitoring​

  • Regular surveys of controlled and supervised areas
  • Identify unexpected dose rate hotspots
  • Verify adequacy of shielding
  • Document compliance with dose rate limits

Contamination Monitoring (Nuclear Medicine)​

  • Wipe tests: paper wiped over surface, counted in scintillation counter
  • Direct surveys with GM counter
  • Required after handling unsealed radioactive sources

Environmental Monitoring​

  • Monitor dose rates at facility boundary
  • Ensure public dose limits are not exceeded
  • Required by regulatory licence conditions

Radiation Protection Regulations​

International Framework​

  • ICRP: Provides scientific recommendations (not legally binding)
  • IAEA: Develops Basic Safety Standards (BSS); adopted by member states
  • WHO: Health guidance on radiation protection

National Regulations (Examples)​

USANRC Regulations (10 CFR); FDA (21 CFR) for medical devices

Key Regulatory Requirements​

  • Radiation protection programme documentation
  • Appointment of Radiation Protection Adviser (RPA) / Medical Physicist
  • Personal dosimetry for classified workers
  • Equipment registration and periodic inspection
  • Incident reporting thresholds
  • Training requirements for radiation workers

Radiation Protection Organisation​

Radiation Protection Adviser (RPA)​

  • Qualified expert (medical physicist or health physicist)
  • Advises employer on compliance with regulations
  • Reviews shielding designs, procedures, and incidents

Radiation Protection Supervisor (RPS)​

  • Local supervisor in each X-ray department
  • Ensures local rules are followed
  • First point of contact for radiation protection queries

Local Rules​

Written procedures that every radiation worker must follow:

  • Identifies controlled and supervised areas
  • Defines contingency arrangements
  • States dose investigation levels
  • Lists key contacts (RPS, RPA)

Radioactive Waste Management​

In nuclear medicine departments:

  • Segregate waste by radionuclide and half-life
  • Store to allow decay (decay-in-storage for short-lived isotopes)
  • Dispose according to regulatory authorisation
  • Keep records of all radioactive waste disposals
