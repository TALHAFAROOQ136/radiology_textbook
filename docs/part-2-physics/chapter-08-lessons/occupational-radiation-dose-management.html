Occupational Radiation Dose Management

Occupational radiation dose management protects radiation workers — radiographers, radiologists, interventional cardiologists, nurses, and others — from excessive radiation exposure during their professional activities. It combines regulatory compliance, physical protection measures, personal monitoring, and training.

Who is at Risk?​

Radiation Workers in Medical Imaging​

OccupationPrimary Exposure Source
RadiographerScattered radiation from patients
Radiologist (diagnostic)Minimal — outside room during exposures
Interventional radiologist / cardiologistScatter during prolonged fluoroscopy
Nuclear medicine technologistUnsealed radioactive sources
Radiation oncology staffTherapeutic radiation sources
Theatre staffC-arm fluoroscopy during surgery
Nursing staff (IR/cath lab)Scatter during procedures

Dose Limits for Radiation Workers​

Based on ICRP Publication 103 (2007):

Body RegionAnnual Dose Limit
Effective dose20 mSv/year (averaged over 5 consecutive years)
Effective dose (single year)50 mSv maximum in any one year
Eye lens20 mSv/year (ICRP 2011 revision)
Skin500 mSv/year (localised area)
Extremities (hands, feet)500 mSv/year

Classification of Workers​

ClassDose CriterionRequirements
Classified workerLikely to exceed 6 mSv/year effective doseIndividual dosimetry; medical surveillance
Non-classified workerUnlikely to exceed 6 mSv/yearWorkplace monitoring sufficient

Personal Protective Equipment (PPE)​

Lead Apron​

  • Primary protection for trunk during fluoroscopy
  • 0.25 mm Pb: ~88% attenuation at 75 kVp scatter
  • 0.35 mm Pb: ~93% attenuation — recommended minimum
  • 0.5 mm Pb: ~97% attenuation — preferred for high-workload operators
  • Wrap-around design protects back from scatter
  • Must be inspected regularly for cracks (fluoroscopy of apron itself)

Thyroid Collar​

  • Protects thyroid gland (radiosensitive; tissue W_T = 0.04)
  • 0.5 mm Pb equivalent
  • Should be worn by all staff in fluoroscopy rooms

Lead Glasses​

  • Protects lens of eye from scatter radiation
  • Particularly important for interventional operators
  • Side shields recommended
  • Leaded glass: 0.5–0.75 mm Pb equivalent
  • Required given revised eye lens dose limit (20 mSv/year)

Lead Gloves​

  • Thin lead rubber (~0.25 mm Pb)
  • Reduces hand dose during fluoroscopy
  • Not suitable for use in primary beam (insufficient protection)

Ceiling-Suspended Shield​

  • Transparent lead acrylic panel
  • Positioned between patient scatter source and operator's head/neck
  • Highly effective — reduces head dose by 60–90%

Table-Side Curtain (Lead Skirt)​

  • Lead rubber curtain hanging from table side
  • Attenuates scatter from below the table
  • Reduces dose to lower body and gonads

Physical Protection Principles in Practice​

Distance​

  • Scatter intensity follows inverse square law
  • At 1 m from patient: scatter ~0.1% of primary beam
  • At 2 m: scatter only 25% of dose at 1 m
  • Step back during fluoroscopy whenever possible

Time​

  • Minimise fluoroscopy time
  • Pulsed fluoroscopy reduces dose rate proportionally
  • Use last image hold; plan acquisitions carefully
  • Rotate staff for prolonged procedures

Shielding​

  • Use all available PPE
  • Position ceiling-suspended shield and table curtain before starting
  • Use structural shielding (control booth, lead screens) whenever possible

Personal Dosimetry​

Types of Dosimeters​

DosimeterReading IntervalAdvantages
Film badgeMonthlyLow cost; historical standard
TLDMonthly/quarterlyAccurate; tissue-equivalent
OSLMonthlyMost common; re-readable

Wearing Position​

DosimeterPositionEstimates
Whole-body badgeCollar level, outside apronHead, neck, thyroid dose
Second badgeWaist level, under apronTrunk dose (with correction factor)
Ring/wrist dosimeterNon-dominant handHand dose (fluoroscopy operators)
Eye dosimeterAt eye levelLens dose (IR operators)

Two-Dosimeter Protocol​

For workers wearing lead aprons, a correction formula estimates effective dose:

E ≈ 0.5 H_under + 0.025 H_over (Niklason formula)

Or more conservatively: use the collar dosimeter reading as a conservative overestimate.

Dose Investigation and Escalation​

Investigation Levels​

If a worker's dose exceeds a defined investigation level:

  1. Confirm dosimeter was worn correctly and not lost
  2. Review work practices during the period
  3. Check for equipment malfunction or unusual procedures
  4. Implement corrective measures if dose was genuinely elevated

Typical Investigation Levels​

PeriodInvestigation Level
Monthly>2 mSv
Quarterly>5 mSv
Annual>15 mSv (classified workers)

Occupational Dose in Interventional Radiology​

Interventional radiologists and cardiologists receive the highest occupational doses in radiology:

Risk Factors for High Dose​

  • Long fluoroscopy times (complex procedures)
  • High DAP procedures (TIPS, complex PCI)
  • Poor beam geometry (steep angulations)
  • Inadequate use of PPE and ceiling shields
  • Standing close to the patient

Dose Reduction Strategies for IR Operators​

StrategyDose Reduction
Ceiling-suspended shield60–90% head/neck reduction
Table-side curtain60–80% lower body reduction
Step back when not manipulating75% at 2 m vs 1 m
Pulsed fluoroscopy (7.5 fps vs 30 fps)75% reduction
FPD close to patientReduces scatter intensity
Steep angulations avoidedLess scatter toward operator
Wrap-around 0.5 mm apron>95% trunk attenuation

Special Situations​

Pregnant Radiation Workers​

  • Must declare pregnancy to employer
  • Additional monthly dosimeter at abdomen
  • Foetal dose limit: 1 mSv for remainder of pregnancy
  • May need temporary reassignment away from fluoroscopy/nuclear medicine
  • Reassignment not always necessary — most diagnostic radiology poses minimal foetal risk

Nuclear Medicine Workers​

  • Exposure from: patient handling, radiopharmaceutical preparation, waste disposal
  • Hand dose often dominant (syringe handling)
  • Use syringe shields; minimise handling time
  • Contamination monitoring essential

Radiographers​

  • Typically low occupational dose (<1–2 mSv/year)
  • Must stand behind protective barrier during exposures
  • Fluoroscopy-assisting radiographers require lead aprons

Training and Supervision​

All radiation workers must receive:

  • Induction training on radiation risks and protection measures
  • Instruction in use of PPE and dosimeters
  • Regular refresher training (typically every 3–5 years)
  • Awareness of local rules and emergency procedures
