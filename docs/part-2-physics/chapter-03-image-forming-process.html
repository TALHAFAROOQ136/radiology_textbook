The Radiographic Image

The image forming process in radiology involves the differential attenuation of X-rays as they pass through the patient, followed by detection and display of the resulting pattern.

Steps in Image Formation​

  1. X-ray generation — X-ray tube produces a beam of photons
  2. Beam collimation — Restricts beam to the region of interest
  3. Interaction with patient — Differential attenuation creates the X-ray shadow
  4. Detection — Detector captures transmitted photons
  5. Processing — Signal converted to a viewable image
  6. Display — Image shown on monitor or film

Differential Attenuation​

Different tissues attenuate X-rays to different degrees, creating subject contrast:

TissueAttenuationAppearance on X-ray
Air / LungVery lowBlack
FatLowDark grey
Soft tissue / WaterModerateGrey
BoneHighLight grey/white
Metal / ContrastVery highBright white

Digital Radiography (DR)​

Computed Radiography (CR)​

  • Uses photostimulable phosphor (PSP) imaging plates
  • Plate stores latent image; laser readout releases stored energy as light
  • Light converted to digital signal

Direct Digital Radiography (DDR)​

  • Indirect DR: Scintillator converts X-rays to light → photodiode array
  • Direct DR: Photoconductor (e.g., amorphous selenium) converts X-rays directly to electrical charge
  • Immediate image display; no cassette needed

Fluoroscopy​

Real-time X-ray imaging using:

  • Continuous or pulsed X-ray beam
  • Image intensifier or flat-panel detector
  • Used for dynamic studies (barium swallow, angiography, catheter placement)

CT Image Formation​

  1. X-ray tube rotates around patient
  2. Multiple projections acquired at different angles
  3. Back-projection reconstruction (filtered back projection or iterative algorithms)
  4. Produces cross-sectional (axial) images
  5. Data displayed as Hounsfield Units (HU):
TissueHU Range
Air−1000
Fat−100 to −50
Water0
Soft tissue+20 to +80
Bone+400 to +1000

Image Display and Windowing​
