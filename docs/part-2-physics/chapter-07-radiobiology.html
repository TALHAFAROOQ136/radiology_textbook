Radiobiology

Radiobiology is the study of the effects of ionising radiation on living organisms. Understanding radiobiological principles is essential for optimising patient care, minimising radiation risk, and providing accurate information to patients and referring clinicians.

What is Ionising Radiation?​

Ionising radiation carries sufficient energy to remove electrons from atoms, creating ion pairs. This ionisation can damage biological molecules, particularly DNA.

Types of ionising radiation used in medicine:

TypeNatureRange in TissueUse
X-raysElectromagneticHigh penetrationRadiography, CT, fluoroscopy
Gamma raysElectromagneticHigh penetrationNuclear medicine, radiotherapy
Alpha particlesHelium nucleusVery short (~mm)Targeted radionuclide therapy
Beta particlesElectronsShort (~cm)Nuclear medicine therapy
ProtonsCharged particlesDefined rangeProton beam therapy

Interaction of Radiation with Cells​

Direct and Indirect Action​

Direct action (~30–40% of damage):

  • Radiation directly strikes and damages a critical target molecule (DNA)
  • More significant with high-LET radiation (alpha, neutrons)

Indirect action (~60–70% of damage):

  • Radiation ionises water molecules → free radicals (OH·)
  • Free radicals chemically damage DNA
  • Most significant mechanism for X-rays and gamma rays

Free Radicals​

  • Highly reactive molecules with unpaired electrons
  • OH· (hydroxyl radical) is the most damaging
  • Can be scavenged by antioxidants (oxygen effect is relevant — hypoxic cells are more radioresistant)

DNA Damage​

DNA is the primary critical target of radiation:

Types of DNA Damage​

Damage TypeDescriptionRepair
Single-strand break (SSB)One strand of DNA helix brokenUsually repaired accurately
Double-strand break (DSB)Both strands broken at same siteMore difficult; may be misrepaired
Base damageChemical modification of DNA basesUsually repaired
Cross-linksDNA-DNA or DNA-protein linksVariable repair

Double-strand breaks are the most biologically significant — misrepair can lead to mutations or cell death.

DNA Repair Mechanisms​

  • Base excision repair (BER): Single base damage
  • Nucleotide excision repair (NER): Larger lesions
  • Homologous recombination (HR): Accurate DSB repair (S/G2 phase)
  • Non-homologous end joining (NHEJ): Fast but error-prone DSB repair

Cell Response to Radiation​

Cell Death​

Radiation can kill cells through:

  • Mitotic (reproductive) death: Cell unable to divide; dies at next mitosis
  • Apoptosis (programmed cell death): Rapid death without division attempt
  • Necrosis: At very high doses

Cell Survival Curves​

Graphical representation of cell survival vs radiation dose:

  • Shoulder region: At low doses, sublethal damage can be repaired
  • Linear region: At higher doses, survival decreases exponentially
  • D₀: Dose reducing survival to 37% — measure of radiosensitivity

The 4 Rs of Radiobiology​

RDescriptionSignificance
RepairRepair of sublethal damage between fractionsAllows normal tissue recovery
ReassortmentCells redistribute through cell cycleIncreases tumour cell killing
RepopulationCell proliferation between fractionsCan reduce tumour response
ReoxygenationHypoxic cells become oxygenatedIncreases radiosensitivity of tumour

Radiosensitivity​

Law of Bergonié and Tribondeau​

Cells are more radiosensitive when they are:

  • Rapidly dividing (high mitotic rate)
  • Undifferentiated (stem cells)
  • Will undergo many future divisions

Most radiosensitive tissues:

  • Bone marrow (haematopoietic cells)
  • Gonads (spermatogonia, oocytes)
  • Intestinal epithelium
  • Lymphocytes
  • Embryo/fetus

Most radioresistant tissues:

  • Muscle
  • Bone
  • Nervous tissue
  • Cartilage

Cell Cycle Sensitivity​

PhaseRadiosensitivity
M (mitosis)Most sensitive
G2Sensitive
G1Intermediate
S (late)Most resistant

Deterministic and Stochastic Effects​

Deterministic Effects (Tissue Reactions)​

  • Occur above a threshold dose
  • Severity increases with dose above threshold
  • Examples: erythema, cataracts, radiation sickness, sterility
EffectThreshold Dose
Temporary erythema (skin redness)~2 Gy
Permanent sterility (male)~3.5 Gy
Cataract (lens opacity)~0.5 Gy (fractionated)
Acute radiation syndrome~1 Gy whole body
LD₅₀/₆₀ (50% mortality)~3–4 Gy whole body

Stochastic Effects​

  • No threshold — any dose carries a risk (however small)
  • Probability (not severity) increases with dose
  • Examples: cancer induction, hereditary effects

Linear No-Threshold (LNT) model:

  • Assumes risk is proportional to dose at all levels
  • Used for radiation protection purposes
  • Controversial at very low doses

Radiation-Induced Cancer Risk​

TissueRelative Sensitivity
Bone marrow (leukaemia)Very high
BreastHigh
LungHigh
ThyroidHigh (especially in children)
ColonModerate
BladderModerate
LiverModerate

Nominal risk coefficient (ICRP 2007):

  • General population: ~5% per Sv (stochastic cancer risk)
  • Workers: ~4% per Sv

Special Considerations​

Foetal Irradiation​

The fetus is highly radiosensitive, especially in first trimester:

Gestational StagePrimary Risk
Pre-implantation (0–9 days)Death of embryo (all-or-nothing)
Organogenesis (2–8 weeks)Malformation (teratogenesis)
Fetal period (8+ weeks)CNS damage, growth retardation
All stagesChildhood cancer risk

Threshold for deterministic effects: ~100 mGy (below typical diagnostic imaging doses)

Children​

  • More radiosensitive than adults
  • Longer life expectancy → more time for stochastic effects to manifest
  • Paediatric dose optimisation essential (ALARA)

Radiation Hormesis​

Controversial hypothesis that very low doses of radiation may be beneficial (stimulate DNA repair mechanisms). Not accepted as basis for radiation protection policy.

Summary​

ConceptKey Point
Primary targetDNA double-strand breaks
Main mechanismIndirect (free radicals from water)
Deterministic effectsAbove threshold; severity dose-dependent
Stochastic effectsNo threshold; probability dose-dependent
Most sensitive cellsRapidly dividing, undifferentiated
Foetal riskGreatest in first trimester
Protection principleALARA — justify, optimise, limit
