Mammography

Mammography is a specialised low-energy X-ray technique designed to image the soft tissue structures of the breast with high contrast and spatial resolution. It is the primary imaging tool for breast cancer screening and diagnosis.

Why Mammography Requires Special Equipment​

The breast consists almost entirely of soft tissues with very similar X-ray attenuation coefficients. Standard radiographic equipment cannot differentiate these tissues adequately. Mammography overcomes this by:

  • Using low kVp (25–35 kVp) to maximise photoelectric effect differences
  • Using specialised target/filter combinations to produce appropriate beam quality
  • Applying compression to reduce thickness and scatter

Mammographic X-ray Tube​

Target Materials​

TargetK-edge EnergyUse
Molybdenum (Mo)20 keVStandard screen-film and digital mammography
Rhodium (Rh)23 keVThicker, denser breasts
Tungsten (W)70 keVDigital mammography (with appropriate filtration)

Filter Materials​

Filters remove photons outside the useful energy range:

FilterUse
Mo (0.03 mm)With Mo target — standard
Rh (0.025 mm)With Mo or Rh target — denser breasts
Ag (0.05 mm)With W target — digital systems

Small Focal Spot​

  • Standard focal spot: 0.3 mm
  • Magnification focal spot: 0.1 mm
  • Small focal spot essential for high spatial resolution

Compression​

Breast compression is a critical step in mammography:

Benefits:

  • Reduces breast thickness → less scatter → improved contrast
  • Separates overlapping structures
  • Reduces geometric blur (brings tissue closer to detector)
  • Reduces radiation dose
  • Immobilises breast → reduces motion blur

Compression force: Typically 100–200 N (automatic compression with manual fine-adjustment)

Mammographic Views​

Standard Views​

ViewAbbreviationDescription
CraniocaudalCCX-ray beam directed superior to inferior
Mediolateral obliqueMLOBeam angled ~45°; includes axillary tail

Additional Views​

  • Lateral (ML or LM): True lateral for lesion localisation
  • Spot compression: Focal compression of a specific area
  • Magnification: Increased SID with small focal spot for fine detail
  • Rolled views: Separate overlapping tissue

Screen-Film Mammography​

Historically the standard; now largely replaced by digital:

  • Single-emulsion film with single screen
  • Processing at higher temperature for enhanced contrast
  • Dedicated mammography viewboxes (high luminance)

Digital Mammography (Full-Field Digital Mammography — FFDM)​

Detector Types​

TypeMaterialConversion
Direct conversionAmorphous selenium (a-Se)Direct
Indirect conversionCsI + a-Si TFTIndirect
Photon-countingSilicon or CdTeDirect (counts individual photons)

Advantages of Digital Mammography​

  • Wider dynamic range (no over/underexposure issues)
  • Post-processing (window/level adjustment)
  • CAD (computer-aided detection) integration
  • PACS storage and teleradiology
  • Lower dose in some patient groups

Digital Breast Tomosynthesis (DBT)​

3D mammography — major advance in breast imaging:

Acquisition:

  • X-ray tube sweeps through an arc (~15°–50°)
  • Multiple low-dose projections acquired
  • Reconstructed into thin (1 mm) slices through the breast

Advantages over 2D mammography:

  • Reduces tissue superimposition (main cause of missed cancers and false positives)
  • Improved cancer detection rate
  • Reduced recall rate (~30–40% reduction)
  • Better characterisation of lesion margins

Dose: Approximately equal to or slightly higher than 2D digital mammography

Contrast-Enhanced Mammography (CEM)​

  • Iodinated contrast agent injected IV
  • Dual-energy acquisition (low and high energy)
  • Subtraction reveals enhancing lesions (neovascularisation)
  • Comparable sensitivity to breast MRI at lower cost

Radiation Dose in Mammography​

ExaminationMean Glandular Dose (MGD)
2-view digital mammography~3–4 mGy
DBT (per view)~1.5–2 mGy
Screen-film mammography~3–5 mGy

MGD (mean glandular dose) is the dose metric used in mammography — it measures dose to the radiosensitive glandular tissue.
