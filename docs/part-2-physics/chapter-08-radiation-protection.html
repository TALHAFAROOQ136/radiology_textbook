Radiation Protection

Radiation protection is the science and practice of protecting people and the environment from the harmful effects of ionising radiation while allowing beneficial uses of radiation in medicine, industry, and research. In diagnostic radiology, it encompasses the protection of patients, staff, and the public.

Why Radiation Protection Matters​

  • Ionising radiation can cause both deterministic effects (above threshold) and stochastic effects (cancer, no threshold)
  • Medical imaging is the largest man-made source of radiation exposure to the population
  • Approximately 3.6 billion radiological examinations performed worldwide annually
  • Benefit of diagnosis must outweigh radiation risk — but risk must be minimised

The ICRP System of Radiological Protection​

The International Commission on Radiological Protection (ICRP) provides the scientific basis for radiation protection worldwide.

Three Fundamental Principles​

1. Justification​

Every exposure to radiation must produce a net benefit:

  • Benefit to the individual or society must outweigh the detriment
  • Applies to: introduction of new practices, individual patient exposures
  • Referring clinician is responsible for justifying each examination
  • Radiologist provides clinical guidance on appropriateness

2. Optimisation (ALARA)​

Radiation doses must be kept As Low As Reasonably Achievable:

  • Balance between image quality (diagnostic benefit) and dose
  • Economic and social factors considered
  • Achieved through: technique optimisation, equipment QC, training
  • Diagnostic Reference Levels (DRLs): Dose benchmarks for standard examinations

3. Dose Limitation​

Annual dose limits apply to workers and the public:

Note: Dose limits do not apply to patients receiving medical exposures.

Patient Radiation Protection​

Justification of Examinations​

  • Refer to evidence-based referral guidelines (e.g., iRefer, ACR Appropriateness Criteria)
  • Avoid duplicate or unnecessary examinations
  • Consider alternatives (ultrasound, MRI) when appropriate

Optimisation of Patient Dose​

  • Use technique charts appropriate for digital systems
  • Monitor exposure indices (EI/DI)
  • Apply dose modulation (CT tube current modulation)
  • Appropriate collimation — restrict beam to area of interest
  • Use grids only when scatter is significant
  • Use the minimum number of CT phases necessary
  • Prefer low-dose protocols for follow-up imaging

Gonadal and Foetal Protection​

  • Use gonadal shielding when gonads are in or near the primary beam (where it does not compromise diagnosis)
  • For pregnant patients: assess gestational age and foetal dose
  • Foetal dose from most diagnostic procedures is well below deterministic thresholds
  • Reassure pregnant patients — risk is very small

Special Populations​

  • Children: Higher radiosensitivity; use paediatric-specific protocols (Image Gently campaign)
  • Pregnant staff: Declare pregnancy; additional monitoring; restrict high-dose procedures
  • Elderly: Lower long-term cancer risk; less restrictive dose management

Occupational Radiation Protection​

The Three Principles of Physical Protection​

1. Distance​

Intensity follows the inverse square law: I ∝ 1/d²

  • Double the distance → quarter the dose rate
  • Most effective and simple protection measure
  • Critical during fluoroscopy — stand as far as practical

2. Shielding​

Absorbing material between source and person:

MaterialUseTypical Thickness
Lead (Pb)Aprons, gloves, screens, walls0.25–0.5 mm Pb apron
ConcreteRoom walls15–30 cm
Lead glassViewing windows2–3 mm Pb equivalent
Barium plasterWallsVariable

Lead apron: Attenuates ~90–95% of scattered radiation at diagnostic energies.

3. Time​

Minimise time spent near radiation sources:

  • Prepare equipment and patient before activating beam
  • Use fluoroscopy intermittently (pulsed; last image hold)
  • Rotate staff for prolonged procedures

Radiation Monitoring​

Personal Dosimeters​

DosimeterTypeCharacteristics
Film badgePassiveMonthly; historical standard
TLD (thermoluminescent)PassiveMonthly/quarterly; accurate
OSL (optically stimulated luminescence)PassiveMonthly; most common currently
EPD (electronic personal dosimeter)ActiveReal-time readout; audible alarm

Wearing position:

  • Outside lead apron at collar level (estimates head/thyroid dose)
  • Under apron at waist level (estimates trunk dose)
  • Wrist/ring dosimeter for hand dose (fluoroscopy operators)
  • Eye dosimeter for interventional radiologists

Dose Investigation Levels​

If personal dose exceeds investigation level:

  1. Review work practices
  2. Check dosimeter was worn correctly
  3. Investigate potential incidents
  4. Implement corrective measures

Structural Radiation Shielding​

Room Design Principles​

  • Controlled area: X-ray room — only authorised personnel with dosimeters
  • Supervised area: Adjacent areas where some dose may occur
  • Primary barrier: Wall, floor, or ceiling in primary beam direction
  • Secondary barrier: Walls receiving only scattered or leakage radiation

Shielding Calculations​

Based on:

  • Workload (mA·min/week)
  • Use factor (U): fraction of time beam directed at that barrier
  • Occupancy factor (T): fraction of time an area is occupied
  • Distance from source to barrier

Diagnostic Reference Levels (DRLs)​

DRLs are dose benchmarks for standard examinations:

  • Set at approximately the 75th percentile of dose distributions
  • Not dose limits — trigger investigation if consistently exceeded
  • Established nationally and locally
ExaminationUK DRL
Chest PA0.1 mGy (ESD)
Abdomen AP4 mGy (ESD)
CT head50 mGy (CTDIvol)
CT chest10 mGy (CTDIvol)
CT abdomen/pelvis15 mGy (CTDIvol)

Radiation Accidents and Incidents​

Types​

  • Equipment malfunction: Erroneous high dose
  • Wrong patient/wrong examination: Unjustified exposure
  • Unintended foetal exposure: Failure to identify pregnancy
  • High-dose fluoroscopy skin injury: Prolonged interventional procedure

Reporting​

  • Significant radiation incidents must be reported to the regulator
  • Thresholds vary by jurisdiction
  • Root cause analysis and corrective action required

Summary​
