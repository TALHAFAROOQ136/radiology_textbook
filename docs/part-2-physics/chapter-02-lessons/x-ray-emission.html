X-ray Emission

X-ray emission refers to the characteristics of the X-ray beam as it exits the tube — its quantity, quality, and spatial distribution. Understanding emission properties is essential for optimising exposure technique and image quality.

X-ray Beam Quantity​

Quantity refers to the number of X-ray photons in the beam.

Measured by:​

  • mAs (milliampere-seconds): primary control of quantity
  • Exposure (mR or mGy at a given distance)
  • Air kerma (kinetic energy released per unit mass in air)

Factors affecting quantity:​

FactorEffect on Quantity
mAsDirectly proportional
kVpApproximately proportional to kVp²
Distance (SID)Inverse square law
FiltrationReduces (removes low-energy photons)
Target material (Z)Higher Z → more efficient production

X-ray Beam Quality​

Quality refers to the penetrating power of the beam — determined by photon energy distribution.

Measured by:​

  • kVp — primary control of quality
  • Half-value layer (HVL): thickness of material reducing beam intensity by half
    • Higher HVL = higher quality (more penetrating) beam
    • Typical HVL for diagnostic X-rays: 3–5 mm Al

Effective energy:​

The single energy of a monoenergetic beam with the same penetrating power as the actual polyenergetic beam. Approximately one-third to one-half of peak kVp.

X-ray Beam Filtration​

Filtration removes low-energy photons that increase patient dose without contributing usefully to the image.

Types of filtration:​

Inherent filtration

  • Glass envelope of the X-ray tube
  • Insulating oil
  • Tube window (beryllium in mammography tubes)
  • Equivalent: ~0.5–1.0 mm Al

Added filtration

  • Aluminium sheets placed in the beam path
  • Copper filters (K-edge filtration) in some applications

Total filtration (required minimum):

  • General radiography: 2.5 mm Al equivalent
  • Mammography: 0.5 mm Mo or 0.025 mm Rh

Effect of filtration on spectrum:​

  • Removes low-energy ("soft") photons
  • Hardens the beam (raises mean energy)
  • Reduces patient entrance dose
  • Slightly reduces overall intensity

Beam Geometry and Divergence​

X-rays are emitted in all directions from the focal spot, but the useful beam is collimated:

  • Divergent beam: intensity decreases with distance (inverse square law)
  • Heel effect: intensity variation across the beam due to anode geometry
    • Cathode side: higher intensity
    • Anode side: lower intensity
    • Clinical use: place thicker body part toward cathode side

Leakage and Scatter Radiation​

Leakage radiation​

  • X-rays emitted through tube housing (not through the window)
  • Must not exceed 1 mGy/hr at 1 m (regulatory limit)
  • Controlled by lead-lined tube housing

Scatter radiation​

  • X-rays that have changed direction after interacting with the patient (Compton scatter)
  • Primary source of occupational exposure to radiographers
  • Reduced by: collimation, grids, distance, shielding

Intensity and the Inverse Square Law​

Beam intensity decreases with the square of distance:

I₁/I₂ = (d₂)²/(d₁)²
