Chapter 12: Trauma and Mobile Radiography

Learning Objectives​

  • Describe trauma radiography protocols for major body regions
  • Explain the cross-table lateral technique and horizontal beam radiography
  • Understand mobile (portable) radiography principles and limitations
  • Apply C-spine clearance imaging principles

12.1 Trauma Radiography Protocols​

Primary Trauma Survey Imaging​

In major trauma, imaging follows the ATLS (Advanced Trauma Life Support) protocol:

Initial trauma series (X-rays in the trauma bay):

  1. AP Chest (portable)
  2. AP Pelvis
  3. Lateral C-spine (if not going directly to CT)

These three X-rays can be obtained rapidly without moving the patient.

CT in Major Trauma​

Modern trauma centers often proceed directly to CT rather than plain X-rays for major trauma patients:

Trauma CT ("pan-scan" or "whole-body CT"):

  • CT Head (non-contrast)
  • CT Cervical Spine
  • CT Chest (with contrast)
  • CT Abdomen and Pelvis (with contrast)
  • Can be completed in <5 minutes

Advantages over X-ray:

  • More sensitive for injuries
  • No patient movement required (except onto CT table)
  • Three-dimensional reconstruction possible

Disadvantages:

  • Higher radiation dose
  • Patient must be transported to CT scanner
  • Requires haemodynamic stability

Trauma X-Ray Principles​

  • Never move the patient into uncomfortable or dangerous positions
  • Radiation safety staff should clear the area before exposure
  • Use horizontal beam where possible to minimize patient movement
  • Document any limitations (e.g., "patient could not be fully positioned due to cervical collar")

12.2 Cross-Table Lateral Technique​

Principle​

The cross-table lateral technique uses a horizontal X-ray beam directed across the patient while they remain supine. This allows lateral images without rolling the patient.

Key applications:

  • Lateral cervical spine in trauma
  • Lateral hip in suspected hip fracture
  • Lateral knee when patient cannot flex
  • Hip and pelvis assessment

Cross-Table Lateral Cervical Spine​

Positioning:

  1. Patient supine with suspected C-spine injury (in cervical collar)
  2. DO NOT remove collar or move neck
  3. Horizontal beam directed from lateral side of neck
  4. Cassette/detector on opposite side of neck, held vertically
  5. Pull shoulders down (not the neck) to visualize C7-T1

Interpretation:

  • All 7 cervical vertebrae must be visible (including C7-T1)
  • If C7-T1 not seen: swimmer's view or CT

Limitations:

  • Cannot replace full C-spine series
  • X-ray alone insufficient for C-spine clearance in obtunded patients

Cross-Table Lateral Hip​

Indications: Suspected hip fracture (cannot do frog-leg lateral)

Positioning:

  1. Patient supine, injured hip in neutral position
  2. Unaffected hip and knee flexed and elevated (supported)
  3. Horizontal beam from medial to lateral
  4. Cassette held vertically against lateral aspect of injured hip

Shows: Femoral neck in profile; detects femoral neck fracture angulation and displacement

12.3 Horizontal Beam Radiography​

Uses​

  • Detection of fluid levels (bowel obstruction, joints)
  • Detection of free air (pneumoperitoneum on erect or decubitus)
  • Air-fluid levels in sinuses (Water's view with horizontal beam)
  • Lateral views without patient movement

Technique​

  • X-ray beam is directed horizontally (parallel to floor)
  • Film/detector is placed vertically beside the patient
  • Gravity causes air to rise and fluid to fall — creating diagnostic air-fluid levels
  • Works best when patient is upright or in decubitus position for ≥5 minutes

Sinus Views with Horizontal Beam​

Indication: Air-fluid levels in sinuses (acute sinusitis, trauma)

  • Standard Water's view (upright) uses horizontal beam
  • Fluid levels in maxillary sinuses visible
  • CT has largely replaced this for sinus pathology

12.4 Mobile (Portable) X-Ray​

When Mobile Radiography is Used​

  • Patients too unstable for transport to radiology
  • Intensive care unit (ICU) patients on ventilators
  • Post-operative patients in recovery
  • Emergency department trauma bays
  • Neonatal intensive care unit (NICU)
  • Operating theatres

Equipment​

Mobile X-ray machines:

  • Battery-powered or plug-in portable units
  • Digital (CR or DR) detectors for immediate image review
  • C-arm fluoroscopy for operative guidance

Limitations of Portable Radiography​

LimitationImpact
AP projection onlyDifferent anatomy angles vs. PA
Short focal-film distanceImage magnification
Less penetrationMay not penetrate large patients adequately
No Bucky grid alwaysIncreased scatter, reduced contrast
Patient position variableRotation, lordosis

AP vs. PA Chest X-Ray​

Standard (PA): Patient stands at cassette; tube behind patient

  • True PA projection
  • Less cardiac magnification
  • Better quality

Portable (AP): Tube in front; patient lying/sitting

  • Cardiac size appears larger (magnification)
  • Clavicles appear higher
  • Mediastinum wider
  • Must note "AP portable" on request form

Clinical implication: Do NOT diagnose cardiomegaly on AP portable CXR without comparison to PA CXR

ICU Chest X-Ray Review​

Systematic approach to portable ICU CXR:

  1. Technical quality: Rotation? Adequate inspiration?
  2. Tubes and lines:
    • Endotracheal tube: 5 cm above carina (neutral position)
    • Central venous lines: tip in SVC, above right atrium
    • Nasogastric tube: below diaphragm in stomach
    • Chest tubes: position relative to pneumothorax or effusion
    • PICC line: tip in SVC/lower
  3. Lungs: Consolidation, atelectasis, pneumothorax, effusion
  4. Heart: Size (limited on AP)
  5. Mediastinum: Width
  6. Bones: Rib fractures, clavicle

12.5 C-Spine Clearance Imaging​

Goal​

To exclude clinically significant cervical spine injury in trauma patients.

Methods​

Clinical Clearance (No Imaging Required if ALL Met)​

NEXUS Criteria (validated):

  1. No midline cervical tenderness
  2. No focal neurological deficit
  3. Normal level of consciousness
  4. No intoxication
  5. No distracting injury

If all 5 criteria met → C-spine injury very unlikely → can clinically clear without imaging

Canadian C-Spine Rule (high-risk and low-risk factors):

  • More complex; used to decide if X-ray or CT needed
  • More specific than NEXUS for certain populations

X-ray C-Spine Series (3-View)​

  1. Lateral (must include C7-T1)
  2. AP
  3. Open-mouth odontoid

Sensitivity: 85–90% (NOT adequate to clear C-spine in obtunded patients)

Must see all 7 vertebrae: If C7-T1 not seen → swimmer's view or CT

CT Cervical Spine​

Indications:

  • Obtunded patient (unable to cooperate with clinical exam)
  • High-energy mechanism (MVA, fall from height)
  • X-ray inadequate (cannot see C7-T1)
  • Abnormal or equivocal X-ray
  • High clinical suspicion despite normal X-ray

Sensitivity: >99% for cervical fractures

Limitations: May miss pure ligamentous injuries (no fracture, but instability)

MRI Cervical Spine​

Indications:

  • Neurological deficit with negative CT
  • High clinical suspicion of ligamentous injury with negative CT
  • Suspected cord contusion/hematoma
  • Pre-operative planning

Key Points Summary​

  1. Trauma survey X-rays: AP Chest, AP Pelvis, and Lateral C-spine — performed rapidly without moving patient significantly

  2. Cross-table lateral uses horizontal beam to image patient in supine position — essential for C-spine and hip assessment in trauma

  3. Portable AP chest appears different from PA — cardiac size magnified, mediastinum wider; do not over-call findings

  4. C-spine clearance: NEXUS criteria can safely exclude injury clinically in cooperative patients; CT needed for obtunded patients

  5. CT is more sensitive than X-ray for cervical fractures (>99% vs. ~85–90%)

  6. MRI is needed for ligamentous injury assessment when CT is negative but clinical suspicion remains
