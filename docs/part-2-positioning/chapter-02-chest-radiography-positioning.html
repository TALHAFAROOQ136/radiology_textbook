Chest Radiography Positioning

Introduction​

Chest radiography positioning is a fundamental aspect of diagnostic radiology that significantly impacts image quality and diagnostic accuracy. Proper positioning ensures optimal visualization of thoracic structures and reduces the need for repeat examinations. This chapter covers the essential techniques and considerations for chest radiography positioning.

Standard Chest Radiography Projections​

Posteroanterior (PA) Chest Projection​

The posteroanterior (PA) chest projection is the standard for routine chest radiography and serves as the primary screening examination for thoracic pathology.

Patient Positioning:​

  • Patient stands upright facing the image receptor
  • Anterior chest placed firmly against the image receptor
  • Shoulders rotated forward to move scapulae laterally out of the lung fields
  • Arms positioned behind the patient or raised slightly to avoid shoulder overlap
  • Patient's height should align the mid-sternum with the center of the image receptor
  • Chin elevated to remove the mandible from the upper lung fields
  • Patient should be instructed to take a deep inspiration and hold their breath during exposure

Technical Considerations:​

  • Central ray perpendicular to the image receptor
  • Centered at the level of T7 vertebra (inferior angle of the scapula)
  • Recommended SID: 72 inches (183 cm) to minimize magnification of the heart
  • Exposure parameters adjusted for patient habitus and inspiration depth

Image Criteria:​

  • Sharp, clear visualization of pulmonary vessels to the lateral chest wall
  • Both lung apices included in the image
  • Lateral borders of the ribs visible
  • Diaphragmatic domes clearly seen
  • Heart shadow sharp without motion blur
  • Minimal rotation (symmetrical appearance of sternoclavicular joints)

Lateral Chest Projection​

The lateral chest projection provides complementary information to the PA projection, particularly for assessing lesions hidden in the anterior or posterior mediastinum, cardiac silhouette, and posterior lung segments.

Patient Positioning:​

  • Patient stands sideways to the image receptor
  • Arms raised and placed on the head or held behind the head
  • Posterior chest wall placed firmly against the image receptor
  • Patient leans slightly forward to separate the clavicles from the upper lobes
  • Feet positioned to maintain proper balance and prevent patient movement
  • Patient instructed to hold breath after full inspiration

Technical Considerations:​

  • Central ray perpendicular to the image receptor
  • Centered at the level of T7 vertebra at the mid-coronal plane
  • Recommended SID: 72 inches (183 cm)
  • Exposure parameters similar to PA projection

Image Criteria:​

  • Vertebral bodies clearly visualized through the heart shadow
  • Posterior costophrenic angles visible
  • Clear separation of anterior and posterior aspects of the chest
  • Minimal rotation (posterior ribs overlapping)
  • Sharp cardiac silhouette without motion blur

Specialized Chest Projections​

Lordotic Chest Projection​

The lordotic projection is used to visualize the lung apices when they are obscured by the clavicles in the standard PA projection.

Indications:​

  • Suspected apical pathology
  • Follow-up of known apical lesions
  • Assessment of upper lobe infiltrates

Patient Positioning:​

  • Patient positioned as for PA chest
  • Patient tilted backward at an angle of 15-20 degrees
  • Chin elevated further than in standard PA
  • Shoulders positioned to move scapulae laterally

Apical Lordotic Projection​

A more exaggerated lordotic position used specifically to evaluate the lung apices.

Technical Parameters:​

  • Increased tube angulation (15-20 degrees cephalad)
  • Higher kVp to compensate for increased tissue penetration
  • Care taken to avoid excessive magnification of the heart

Positioning Considerations for Different Patient Populations​

Pediatric Patients​

Special Considerations:​

  • Immobilization techniques to prevent motion blur
  • Age-appropriate communication and positioning aids
  • Adjusted exposure parameters based on smaller body habitus
  • Parent/caregiver presence during examination when appropriate

Geriatric Patients​

Special Considerations:​

  • Accommodation for limited mobility or joint stiffness
  • Additional support to maintain proper positioning
  • Assessment of respiratory capacity for breath-holding
  • Consideration of comorbidities affecting positioning

Bedridden Patients​

Portable Chest Radiography:​

  • Anterior-posterior (AP) projection typically used
  • Supine or semi-upright positioning
  • Attention to patient safety and comfort
  • Alternative projections based on clinical indication

AP Portable Chest​

Used when patients cannot stand for standard PA radiography.

Positioning Techniques:​

  • Patient positioned supine or semi-upright in bed
  • Image receptor placed posterior to patient
  • Tube positioned anterior to patient
  • Shorter SID may result in increased heart magnification
  • Careful attention to central ray alignment

Common Positioning Errors and Artifacts​

Rotation​

Identification:​

  • Asymmetry of sternoclavicular joints
  • Unequal distance of spinous processes to vertebral bodies
  • Different apparent sizes of hemithoraces

Correction:​

  • Ensure patient is facing directly toward image receptor
  • Check shoulder positioning
  • Verify central ray alignment

Poor Inspiration​

Identification:​

  • Elevated diaphragms
  • Crowded pulmonary vessels
  • Small cardiac silhouette
  • Flattened diaphragmatic domes

Correction:​

  • Adequate patient instruction
  • Proper breathing technique demonstration
  • Allow sufficient time for patient preparation

Motion Blur​

Identification:​

  • Blurred edges of structures
  • Loss of fine detail
  • Unsharp cardiac and diaphragmatic borders

Prevention:​

  • Clear breath-holding instructions
  • Synchronization of exposure with patient's breath-hold
  • Minimize examination time

Quality Assurance in Chest Positioning​

Pre-examination Checklist:​

  • Verify patient identity and clinical indication
  • Assess patient's ability to cooperate with positioning requirements
  • Select appropriate technical parameters based on patient habitus
  • Ensure equipment is properly calibrated

Post-examination Review:​

  • Evaluate image quality against established criteria
  • Identify positioning errors for correction in future examinations
  • Document any special circumstances or modifications used
  • Ensure diagnostic quality images are obtained before patient departure

Clinical Correlation​

Proper chest radiography positioning directly impacts diagnostic confidence and patient care. Suboptimal positioning may lead to:

  • Misinterpretation of normal variants as pathology
  • Missed lesions due to poor visualization
  • Repeat examinations increasing radiation exposure
  • Delayed diagnosis and treatment

Understanding and implementing proper chest radiography positioning techniques ensures consistent, high-quality diagnostic images that serve as the foundation for accurate interpretation and optimal patient care.

Summary​

Chest radiography positioning requires attention to detail and understanding of the relationship between patient positioning and resulting image quality. Mastery of standard and specialized projections, along with awareness of common positioning errors, enables radiologic technologists to consistently produce diagnostic-quality chest radiographs. Proper positioning techniques contribute significantly to the accuracy of chest radiography interpretation and ultimately to improved patient outcomes.
