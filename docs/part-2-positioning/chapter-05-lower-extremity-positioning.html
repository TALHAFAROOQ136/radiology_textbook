Chapter 10: Skeletal Positioning — Lower Extremities

Learning Objectives​

  • Position patients correctly for foot, ankle, tibia, knee, and femur radiographs
  • Identify standard and supplementary views for lower extremity imaging
  • Explain the clinical indications for each position
  • Recognize key anatomical landmarks on lower extremity radiographs

10.1 Foot and Toe Positioning​

AP (Dorsoplantar) Foot​

Indications: Foot fractures, foreign bodies, arthritis, foot deformity

Positioning:

  1. Patient seated on table with knee flexed
  2. Foot flat on cassette
  3. Central ray angled 10° posteriorly (toward heel), centered at 3rd metatarsal base
  4. This angulation reduces foreshortening of metatarsals

Shows: All metatarsals, phalanges, tarsal bones (partially), TMT joints

Oblique Foot​

Positioning:

  • Foot rotated medially 30–45° (medial side elevated)
  • Medial oblique is standard
  • Central ray perpendicular at 3rd TMT joint

Shows: 3rd-5th metatarsal bases (Jones fracture at 5th MT base), cuboid, lateral tarsal area without overlap

Lateral Foot​

Positioning:

  • Patient in lateral position (lateral side of foot on cassette)
  • Foot in true lateral
  • Central ray perpendicular at mid-foot level

Shows: Calcaneus, talus in profile; midfoot, forefoot in lateral projection; calcaneal pitch angle

Calcaneus Views​

Axial (Harris-Beath view):

  • Foot dorsiflexed (patient pulls toes back with a strap)
  • Heel on cassette
  • Beam angled 40° from vertical (along long axis of foot)
  • Shows: Calcaneal body and subtalar joint from below

Lateral calcaneus:

  • True lateral view of calcaneus
  • Böhler's angle can be measured (normal 20–40°; reduced in calcaneal fracture)

10.2 Ankle Positioning​

AP Ankle​

Indications: Ankle fractures (Ottawa Ankle Rules), ligament injury, arthritis

Positioning:

  1. Patient supine or seated with leg extended
  2. Ankle in dorsiflexion (90°)
  3. Midsagittal plane of leg perpendicular to cassette
  4. Central ray perpendicular, centered at tibiotalar joint (midway between malleoli)

Shows: Tibiotalar joint, both malleoli, distal fibula

Note: True AP with some external rotation shows the ankle mortise differently from the mortise view

Mortise View (Oblique AP)​

Indication: Best view for ankle fracture assessment; shows mortise (bony ring of ankle)

Positioning:

  • Same as AP ankle but foot internally rotated 15–20°
  • This brings fibular malleolus out of overlapping position
  • Entire mortise (medial, superior, and lateral joint space) is visible

Interpretation:

  • Medial clear space: should be <4 mm (widening suggests deltoid ligament rupture)
  • Superior clear space: should be uniform
  • Tibiofibular overlap: reduced if syndesmosis disrupted

Lateral Ankle​

Positioning:

  • Patient in lateral position (lateral side of ankle on cassette)
  • True lateral (medial and lateral malleoli superimposed)
  • Central ray perpendicular at medial malleolus

Shows: Lateral view of talus, tibiotalar joint, Achilles tendon shadow, calcaneus

10.3 Tibia and Fibula​

AP Tibia/Fibula​

Positioning:

  1. Patient supine, leg extended
  2. Foot slightly dorsiflexed and internally rotated slightly
  3. Central ray perpendicular, centered midway along tibia/fibula
  4. Include both ankle and knee joints

Lateral Tibia/Fibula​

Positioning:

  • Knee and hip slightly flexed
  • Lateral side of leg on cassette
  • Central ray at midpoint of leg

Shows: Overlapping tibia and fibula in profile; medullary canal

10.4 Knee Positioning​

AP Knee​

Indications: Knee fractures, arthritis, effusion, patella assessment

Positioning:

  1. Patient supine, leg extended
  2. Slight internal rotation (3–5°) to correct for normal knee anatomy
  3. Central ray perpendicular at tibial plateau (1 cm below apex of patella)
  4. May be taken erect for joint space measurement

Shows: Distal femur, proximal tibia and fibula, patella, medial and lateral joint spaces

Lateral Knee​

Positioning:

  • Patient in lateral position (lateral side down)
  • Knee flexed 20–30° (or as patient tolerates)
  • Central ray perpendicular at medial tibial plateau

Shows: Femoral condyles, patellofemoral joint, tibial plateau in profile, fat pad

Fat pad sign: Hoffa's fat pad and prefemoral fat pad visible; displacement suggests effusion

Suprapatellar joint space: Normally should be congruent; effusion shows as soft tissue density

Patella Views​

AP/PA Tangential (Sunrise/Merchant View):

Sunrise View:

  • Patient seated, knee flexed over end of table
  • Cassette held below knee
  • Beam directed from above through patellofemoral joint
  • Shows patellofemoral joint; patellar maltracking; fractures

Merchant View (more reproducible):

  • Patient supine, knees bent 45° over table end
  • Bilateral tangential view of both patellofemoral joints
  • Shows: Q-angle, patellar tilt, subluxation

Tunnel View (Intercondylar Notch View)​

Indication: Detect loose bodies in intercondylar notch; osteochondral defects

Positioning:

  • Patient prone, knee flexed 40°
  • Or PA with knee on cassette
  • Beam angled 40° caudally
  • Shows intercondylar (intercondyloid) fossa without overlap

Also called: Notch view, Camp-Coventry method

Erect Knee (Weight-Bearing)​

Indication: True joint space measurement for osteoarthritis severity

Positioning:

  • Patient erect, weight bearing on both legs
  • AP view
  • Bilateral comparison

Shows: True articular cartilage loss (narrowing under load); better than non-weight bearing

10.5 Femur Positioning​

AP Femur​

Positioning:

  1. Patient supine
  2. Leg extended and internally rotated 15–20° (same as hip)
  3. Must include hip joint above and knee joint below
  4. Two cassettes may be needed for tall patients

Lateral Femur​

Positioning:

  • Patient in lateral position
  • Knee slightly flexed
  • Lateral side of thigh on cassette
  • Central ray midway along femoral shaft

10.6 Special Views​

Long Leg (Full Leg) View​

Indication: Limb length discrepancy; lower limb alignment for surgical planning

Positioning:

  • Patient erect, weight-bearing
  • Both lower limbs included on one long film
  • Includes hips, knees, and ankles
  • Digital X-ray allows stitching of multiple images

Measurements:

  • Mechanical axis (femoral head to center of ankle) — should pass through center of knee
  • Varus/valgus alignment

Whole Leg Radiograph​

Used pre-operatively for:

  • Tibial and femoral osteotomies
  • Total knee replacement planning
  • Assessment of tibial/femoral bowing

Key Points Summary​

  1. Mortise view (15–20° internal rotation) is the key ankle view for assessing the ankle mortise and detecting syndesmotic injury

  2. Medial clear space on mortise view should be <4 mm; widening indicates deltoid ligament rupture or medial mortise injury

  3. Böhler's angle on lateral calcaneus (normal 20–40°) is reduced in calcaneal fracture

  4. Merchant/Sunrise view of the patellofemoral joint is essential for evaluating patellar maltracking and subluxation

  5. Weight-bearing knee radiographs are required for accurate joint space measurement in osteoarthritis

  6. Ottawa Ankle Rules guide the decision to perform ankle and foot radiographs after trauma
