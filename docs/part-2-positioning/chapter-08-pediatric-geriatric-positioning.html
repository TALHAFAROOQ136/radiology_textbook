Chapter 13: Pediatric and Geriatric Positioning

Learning Objectives​

  • Identify unique positioning challenges for pediatric patients
  • Describe immobilization techniques and devices for children
  • Apply dose reduction principles specifically for pediatric imaging
  • Recognize geriatric-specific positioning adaptations

13.1 Pediatric Positioning Considerations​

Why Children Are Different​

Pediatric patients are NOT small adults. Key differences include:

Anatomical differences:

  • Proportionally larger head relative to body (affects positioning for chest/abdomen)
  • Growth plates present (open physes — potential for injury with inappropriate positioning)
  • Thymus visible in chest (can mimic mediastinal mass)
  • Larger radiosensitivity (rapidly dividing cells, longer life expectancy)

Behavioral differences:

  • Cannot follow instructions (infants, toddlers)
  • Movement artifact is common
  • Fear and anxiety affect cooperation
  • Parental presence may be essential

Technical implications:

  • Shorter exposure time needed (motion blur)
  • Smaller field sizes
  • Adjusted technique for smaller patient size

Age-Specific Approaches​

Age GroupKey ChallengesApproach
Newborn / Neonate (0-28 days)Fragile; hypothermia riskWarm blankets; NICU portable
Infant (1-12 months)Cannot cooperateImmobilization devices; fast exposure
Toddler (1-3 years)Most difficult; resistsParental holding; immobilization
Preschool (3-5 years)Can understand simple instructionsGames; distraction
School-age (5-12 years)Can cooperate well with reassuranceExplanation and reassurance
Adolescent (12+)Approach as adult; consider privacyPrivacy; consent

13.2 Immobilization Techniques​

Purpose​

Immobilization allows imaging without sedation in many cases:

  • Reduces motion artifact
  • Reduces repeat exposures (lower radiation)
  • Safer than sedation

Manual Restraint by Parents/Carers​

Permissible when:

  • Parent understands and consents
  • Parent wears lead apron
  • Parent is not pregnant
  • Parent has no recent significant radiation exposure

Technique:

  • Parent holds child's arms above head (for chest X-ray)
  • Parent holds head steady (for skull or facial views)
  • Gentle but firm hold

Note: Staff should not hold unless necessary and wearing appropriate protection.

Papoose Board (Pigg-O-Stat)​

  • Rigid board with velcro straps
  • Child secured with arms extended above head
  • Used for chest and abdominal views in infants
  • Allows PA chest view (child stands inside device)

Pigg-O-Stat​

  • Specialized device for infant chest radiography
  • Clear Perspex tray with head support
  • Child stands upright with arms elevated by the device
  • Provides true PA chest view
  • Most commonly used for infants 1-18 months

Head Holders​

  • Foam or Velcro straps to stabilize skull
  • Used for skull, facial, and sinus views

Sandbags​

  • Heavy sandbags placed on limbs to reduce movement
  • Not directly on the patient (risk of injury); on adjacent area to provide resistance

13.3 Radiation Dose Reduction in Pediatrics​

Why It Matters More in Children​

  • Children are 2-5x more radiosensitive than adults
  • Longer life expectancy = more time for cancer to develop
  • Gonads and bone marrow particularly sensitive

Key Principle: Child-Sized Techniques​

Image Gently Campaign principles:

  1. Child-size the exposure (reduce mA and kVp to match child's size)
  2. Shield gonads when not in primary beam
  3. One shot — optimize positioning to avoid repeats
  4. Size matters — know the child's weight and adjust protocols

Technique Adjustment by Size​

Instead of adult technique:

  • Reduce mAs significantly for smaller children
  • Automated exposure control (AEC) should be used
  • Pediatric protocols programmed separately from adults
  • DR/CR detectors allow lower dose than film-screen

Specific Dose Reduction Measures​

kVp optimization:

  • Chest X-ray children: ~65-70 kVp (lower than adult 120 kVp)
  • Lower kVp increases contrast but increases dose — balance carefully

Collimation:

  • Strict collimation to region of interest
  • Never use adult field size on a child

Gonadal shielding:

  • Ovarian shielding (girls): when ovaries are near primary beam (pelvis, abdomen)
  • Testicular shielding (boys): especially for hip/pelvis/abdomen
  • NOTE: Current ACR guidance is evolving — shielding can sometimes obscure anatomy; clinical judgment required

CT pediatric protocols:

  • Automatic tube current modulation
  • Reduced kVp for smaller patients (80-100 kVp vs. 120-140 kVp in adults)
  • Iterative reconstruction
  • Image Gently CT protocols

Alternatives to Ionizing Radiation​

  • Ultrasound: First choice for abdominal, renal, hip, and soft tissue pathology
  • MRI: Preferred for brain, spine, and musculoskeletal in children (no radiation) — sedation may be needed
  • Fluoroscopy: Minimize fluoroscopy time; use pulsed fluoroscopy

13.4 Common Pediatric Radiographic Studies​

Pediatric Chest X-Ray​

Normal variants in children:

  • Thymus: anterior mediastinal opacity; "sail sign" or "wave sign" — normal up to age 2-3
  • Rounded lung opacities may represent atelectasis (not pneumonia)
  • Respiratory motion common

Positioning challenges:

  • Infant: Pigg-O-Stat or supine AP (PA preferred if possible)
  • Toddler: AP supine or upright if can be positioned
  • School-age: Same as adult (PA erect)

Pediatric Hip Radiograph​

Developmental Dysplasia of the Hip (DDH):

  • AP pelvis + frog-leg lateral
  • Hilgenreiner line, Perkin line, acetabular index measured
  • Ultrasound preferred in infants <6 months (cartilaginous femoral head)

Perthes Disease (avascular necrosis of femoral head):

  • AP and frog-leg lateral hip views
  • Fragmentation, sclerosis, collapse of femoral head

Scoliosis Studies in Children​

  • Full spine PA (standing) + lateral
  • Monitor radiation dose — patients have repeated X-rays over years
  • EOS system: full-body imaging at very low dose (1/10th conventional X-ray)
  • Use PA rather than AP (reduces breast radiation dose significantly)

13.5 Geriatric Positioning Challenges​

Unique Considerations in Elderly Patients​

Anatomical changes:

  • Osteoporosis: fragile bones — handle gently; avoid forceful positioning
  • Kyphosis: difficult to achieve neutral spine position
  • Joint contractures: limited range of motion; cannot fully extend limbs
  • Skin fragility: pressure from positioning devices causes bruising or tears

Cognitive changes:

  • Dementia: cannot follow instructions; anxiety; agitation
  • Need simple, clear, repeated instructions
  • Familiar person present may help calm patient

Mobility:

  • Many use walkers, wheelchairs, or are bed-bound
  • Transfer to X-ray table may be difficult
  • Consider portable X-ray if transfer is dangerous

Modified Techniques for Elderly​

Chest X-ray:

  • PA erect if patient can stand
  • If kyphotic: beam may need tilting
  • Portable AP if patient cannot stand safely

Hip X-ray:

  • May need modified AP view if contracture prevents positioning
  • Cross-table lateral if frog-leg position impossible (arthritic/contracted hip)
  • Document why standard positioning was not possible

Lumbar spine:

  • Severe kyphosis: cannot achieve true AP/lateral
  • Osteoporotic compression fractures common — document findings carefully
  • Modified lateral with patient in wheelchair if necessary

Extremity X-rays:

  • Joint contractures: cannot extend elbow or wrist fully
  • Take best achievable position and document
  • Two views at 90° to each other still the goal

Osteoporosis Assessment​

DEXA Scan (Dual-Energy X-ray Absorptiometry):

  • Not a conventional radiograph — measures bone density
  • Two sites: lumbar spine (PA) and hip
  • T-score: compared to young normal adults
    • T-score >-1.0: Normal
    • T-score -1.0 to -2.5: Osteopenia
    • T-score <-2.5: Osteoporosis

VFA (Vertebral Fracture Assessment):

  • Can be done on DEXA machine
  • Lateral images of spine at low dose
  • Detects vertebral compression fractures

Key Points Summary​

  1. Children are NOT small adults — different anatomy, radiation sensitivity, and behavioral considerations

  2. Pigg-O-Stat is the standard device for infant chest radiography, allowing true PA positioning

  3. Image Gently principles: child-size technique, collimate carefully, shield when appropriate, avoid repeats

  4. Ultrasound and MRI are preferred over X-ray in children wherever clinically appropriate

  5. Elderly patients present with osteoporosis, contractures, and dementia — modified positioning and gentle handling required

  6. DEXA scan is the gold standard for bone density measurement and osteoporosis diagnosis
