Chapter 38: Soft Tissue and Sports Injuries

Learning Objectives​

  • Describe MRI features of ACL, PCL, and meniscal tears
  • Recognize rotator cuff tears on MRI and ultrasound
  • Identify Achilles tendon pathology on imaging
  • Describe the imaging approach to stress fractures
  • Classify muscle and tendon injuries

38.1 Knee Ligament and Meniscal Injuries​

ACL (Anterior Cruciate Ligament) Tear​

Mechanism: Non-contact pivoting/twisting; deceleration; valgus force

"Unhappy Triad" (O'Donoghue's):

  • ACL tear + MCL tear + medial meniscus tear
  • Mechanism: valgus force with rotation

MRI features of ACL tear:

Primary signs:

  • Complete disruption: Non-visualization of ACL or discontinuous fibers
  • Abnormal signal: High T2/PD signal within or replacing ACL fibers
  • "Empty intercondylar notch" sign
  • ACL fibers fail to run from posterior lateral femoral condyle to anterior tibia

Secondary signs (associated findings):

  • Bone bruise: Kissing contusions at lateral femoral condyle and posterior lateral tibial plateau ("pivot shift" bone bruise)
  • Segond fracture: Lateral tibial plateau rim avulsion — highly specific (pathognomonic) for ACL tear
  • Anterior tibial translation
  • Posterior lateral femoral condyle contusion + posterolateral tibial plateau contusion = "coup" and "contrecoup"

PCL (Posterior Cruciate Ligament)​

  • Larger, stronger than ACL
  • Normal: Bow-tie shaped, uniformly dark (low T2 signal)
  • Tear: High T2 signal, discontinuity
  • Mechanism: Dashboard injury (posterior tibial force)

Meniscal Tears​

MRI grading of meniscal signal:

  • Grade 0: Normal — homogeneous dark signal
  • Grade 1: Globular high signal (not reaching articular surface) — intrasubstance degeneration
  • Grade 2: Linear high signal not reaching articular surface
  • Grade 3: High signal reaching articular surface = tear

Types of meniscal tears:

TypeDescription
HorizontalParallel to tibial plateau; often degenerative
Vertical longitudinalParallel to long axis of meniscus; acute/traumatic
Bucket-handleLarge vertical longitudinal tear; displaced fragment → medial notch
RadialPerpendicular to meniscal rim
ComplexMultiple planes

Bucket-handle tear:

  • "Double PCL sign": Displaced meniscus fragment in intercondylar notch (simulates second PCL)
  • "Absent bow-tie sign": <2 bow-tie images on sagittal MRI
  • "Flipped meniscus"

38.2 Rotator Cuff Tears​

Anatomy​

Four muscles of the rotator cuff:

  • Supraspinatus (most commonly torn)
  • Infraspinatus
  • Teres minor
  • Subscapularis

Mnemonic: SITS

Classification​

TypeDescription
Partial-thicknessDoes not extend through full thickness
Full-thickness (complete)Extends from articular to bursal surface
MassiveInvolves ≥2 tendons

MRI Features​

Normal supraspinatus: Low signal on all sequences (dark)

Tendinopathy (no tear):

  • Intermediate or high signal on T1 and PD
  • Intact tendon

Partial tear:

  • High signal extending partially through tendon
  • Articular-sided partial tears most common

Full-thickness tear:

  • High T2 signal gap through the full tendon
  • Fluid fills the defect (T2 hyperintense)
  • Tendon retraction toward musculotendinous junction
  • Secondary: Muscle atrophy and fatty infiltration

Signs of chronic/massive tear:

  • Muscle atrophy (volume loss)
  • Fatty infiltration (increased T1 signal in muscle — Goutallier classification)
  • Superior migration of humeral head (acromiohumeral distance <7 mm)

Ultrasound for Rotator Cuff​

  • Excellent for full-thickness tears (sensitivity 90-95%)
  • Dynamic imaging: assess impingement
  • Partial tears: moderate sensitivity (~70%)
  • Operator-dependent

38.3 Achilles Tendon Pathology​

Anatomy​

  • Largest tendon in the body
  • Insertion at posterior calcaneus
  • Blood supply: poor in central portion 2-6 cm proximal to insertion ("watershed zone")

Tendinopathy (Non-Insertion)​

  • Degeneration and thickening at watershed zone (2-6 cm from insertion)
  • MRI: Thickened tendon; intrasubstance high T2 signal; "fusiform swelling"
  • Ultrasound: Tendon thickening, hypoechoic changes, neovascularization on Doppler

Achilles Tendon Rupture​

Mechanism: Sudden plantarflexion; eccentric loading

Clinical: "Feeling kicked from behind"; palpable gap; positive Thompson test

MRI features:

  • Complete: Gap in Achilles tendon; fluid-filled defect on T2
  • Tendon ends may be retracted
  • Hematoma in gap

Ultrasound:

  • Real-time assessment
  • "Kager's fat pad" herniates into gap
  • Thompson test dynamic assessment

Management: Surgical repair vs. conservative (non-weight-bearing in equinus) — ongoing debate

Insertional Tendinopathy​

  • At tendon-bone junction
  • Haglund deformity: Prominent posterior superior calcaneal tuberosity → impingement
  • Retrocalcaneal bursitis: Fluid between tendon and calcaneus
  • MRI: Enthesopathy, bone edema, bursitis

38.4 Stress Fractures​

Definition​

Fracture from repetitive submaximal loading that exceeds the bone's reparative capacity; no single traumatic event.

Types​

Fatigue fractures: Normal bone subjected to abnormal repetitive loading

  • Military recruits, athletes
  • Metatarsals ("march fracture"), tibia, femoral neck, navicular

Insufficiency fractures: Abnormal bone (osteoporotic) subjected to normal loading

  • Elderly women; sacral stress fractures; pubic rami

Common Sites by Sport/Activity​

SiteSport/Activity
2nd-3rd metatarsalRunning, military training ("march fracture")
Tibia (posterior)Running
Femoral neckRunners, military
NavicularBasketball, jumping
Lumbar pars (spondylolysis)Gymnastics, cricket, batting
Pubic ramiPostmenopausal women, long-distance runners
SacrumElderly, post-radiation

Imaging​

Plain X-ray:

  • Insensitive in early stages (negative in first 2 weeks)
  • Periosteal reaction ("stress reaction") earliest X-ray sign
  • Sclerotic line through bone (late)
  • "Hot cross bun" sign: transverse sclerotic line

MRI (most sensitive):

  • T1: Linear low signal (fracture line)
  • T2/STIR: Periosteal edema and bone marrow edema (high signal)
  • Detects stress fractures within 24-72 hours
  • Classification:
    • Grade 1: Periosteal edema only
    • Grade 2: Periosteal + marrow edema
    • Grade 3: Fracture line visible on T2
    • Grade 4: Fracture line on T1 and T2

Bone scan:

  • Hot spot (focally increased uptake)
  • Sensitive but non-specific

38.5 Muscle Strains​

Grading​

GradeDescription
Grade IStrain — microtears; no macroscopic disruption
Grade IIPartial tear — palpable defect
Grade IIIComplete rupture

MRI Features​

Grade I: STIR/T2 high signal (edema) within muscle; no architectural disruption

Grade II: Partial fiber disruption with hematoma; myofascial plane involvement; architectural distortion

Grade III: Complete muscle tear; muscle retraction; hematoma; "drooping muscle" appearance

Common Sites​

  • Hamstrings: Proximal (at ischial tuberosity) — from sprinting
  • Quadriceps: Rectus femoris — from kicking
  • Gastrocnemius medial head: "Tennis leg" — sudden push-off
  • Adductors: From twisting/sudden direction change

Ultrasound for Muscle Injuries​

  • Acute: Hypoechoic edema or anechoic hematoma
  • Dynamic assessment: Contraction reveals tear
  • Guides aspiration of hematoma if indicated

Key Points Summary​

  1. Grade 3 meniscal signal (reaching articular surface) = tear; bucket-handle tear shows "double PCL sign"

  2. Segond fracture (lateral tibial rim avulsion) is pathognomonic for ACL tear

  3. Bone bruise pattern (lateral femoral condyle + posterior lateral tibial plateau) = pivot shift/ACL injury mechanism

  4. Rotator cuff: Supraspinatus most commonly torn; full-thickness tear shows fluid-filled gap on T2 MRI

  5. Stress fracture: X-ray negative in first 2 weeks; MRI detects bone marrow edema within 24-72 hours

  6. Achilles tendon rupture — gap on MRI/US; 2-6 cm proximal to insertion (watershed zone)
