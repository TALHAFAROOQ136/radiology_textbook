Chapter 39: Bone Tumors and Infections

Learning Objectives​

  • Apply a systematic approach to bone tumor analysis on plain radiographs
  • Identify classic benign bone lesions: osteochondroma, giant cell tumor
  • Recognize malignant bone tumors: osteosarcoma, Ewing's sarcoma, metastases
  • Describe osteomyelitis imaging patterns on X-ray, MRI, and bone scan
  • Differentiate tumor from infection radiographically

39.1 Systematic Approach to Bone Lesions​

Key Questions for Any Bone Lesion​

  1. Patient age: Most tumors occur in specific age groups
  2. Location: Which bone? Which part of the bone (epiphysis, metaphysis, diaphysis)?
  3. Borders: Sharp (zone of transition) or ill-defined?
  4. Matrix: Calcification pattern? Ossification?
  5. Periosteal reaction: Type and pattern
  6. Soft tissue component: Extension beyond bone?

Zone of Transition​

Zone of transition = where the lesion merges with normal bone

  • Narrow (well-defined): Slowly growing; benign
  • Wide (ill-defined): Aggressive; possibly malignant

Periosteal Reactions​

TypePatternSignificance
SolidSingle solid new bone layerBenign, slow process
Laminated ("onion skin")Multiple parallel layersAggressive (Ewing's sarcoma)
Codman's trianglePeriosteum elevated at tumor edgeAggressive, malignant
SunburstSpiculated radiating patternHighly aggressive (osteosarcoma)
Hair-on-endPerpendicular spiculesMalignant (also in bone marrow expansion)

Bone Matrix​

Calcified matrix (cartilage):

  • "Rings and arcs" — C-shaped calcifications
  • Fluffy, cloud-like — malignant cartilage tumors

Ossified matrix (bone within lesion):

  • Dense, "ivory-like" — osteosarcoma
  • Well-organized trabeculae — fibrous dysplasia

Age Groups and Tumor Type​

AgeLikely Tumors
<5 yearsNeuroblastoma metastases
10-25 yearsEwing's sarcoma, osteosarcoma, chondroblastoma
20-40 yearsGiant cell tumor, fibrous dysplasia
>40 yearsMetastases (most common), myeloma, chondrosarcoma

39.2 Benign Bone Lesions​

Osteochondroma (Exostosis)​

Most common benign bone tumor

Location: Metaphysis of long bones (distal femur, proximal tibia, proximal humerus) Age: Children and young adults

Radiographic features:

  • Bony outgrowth from cortex
  • Continuous cortex and medullary canal with parent bone (key distinguishing feature)
  • Cartilage cap at tip (may calcify)
  • Pedunculated (stalk) or sessile (broad base)
  • Points away from joint

Multiple osteochondromas: Hereditary Multiple Exostoses (HME) — autosomal dominant; higher sarcoma risk

Malignant transformation risk:

  • Solitary: <1%
  • Multiple (HME): 3-5%
  • Suspect if cartilage cap >2 cm in adult (MRI)

Giant Cell Tumor (GCT)​

Location: Epiphysis of long bones (distal femur, proximal tibia, distal radius — "around the knee") Age: 20-40 years (skeletally mature)

Radiographic features:

  • Epiphyseal location (reaches articular surface)
  • Eccentric within bone
  • "Soap bubble" appearance (internal trabeculation)
  • No matrix mineralization
  • Narrow zone of transition but no sclerotic margin
  • Cortical expansion

CT/MRI:

  • Fluid-fluid levels (secondary ABC formation)
  • Locally aggressive; may recur after curettage

Enchondroma​

  • Benign cartilage tumor within medullary cavity
  • Common in hands (phalanges)
  • Rings and arcs calcification within bone
  • Treatment: usually none; curette if painful/pathological fracture risk

Fibrous Dysplasia​

  • Fibrous tissue replacing normal bone
  • "Ground glass" matrix (hazy, lucent appearance)
  • Bowing deformity ("shepherd's crook" deformity of proximal femur)
  • Usually asymptomatic; may cause pathological fracture

Unicameral Bone Cyst (Simple Bone Cyst)​

  • Fluid-filled cyst
  • Metaphysis of humerus or femur
  • Children 5-15 years
  • "Fallen leaf sign": Pathological fracture; cortical fragment falls to dependent wall of cyst
  • Treatment: Aspiration/injection or curettage

39.3 Malignant Bone Tumors​

Osteosarcoma​

Most common primary malignant bone tumor in children/adolescents (10-25 years)

Location: Metaphysis; distal femur (50%), proximal tibia, proximal humerus ("around the knee")

Radiographic features:

  • Mixed lytic and sclerotic lesion
  • Sunburst periosteal reaction — highly specific
  • Codman's triangle — periosteal elevation at tumor margin
  • Dense "ivory-like" tumor bone within lesion
  • Soft tissue mass

Types:

  • Conventional (90%): High-grade, osteoblastic/chondroblastic/fibroblastic
  • Parosteal: Low-grade; on posterior distal femur; dense ossified mass
  • Periosteal: Intermediate grade; periosteal surface

Staging:

  • MRI: Local extent, skip lesions (within same bone)
  • CT Chest: Pulmonary metastases
  • Bone scan: Bone metastases

Ewing's Sarcoma​

Second most common malignant bone tumor in children Age: 10-20 years (adolescents); "Round cell tumor"

Location: Diaphysis of long bones (femur, tibia, fibula); flat bones (pelvis, ribs, scapula)

Radiographic features:

  • Permeative "moth-eaten" lytic lesion
  • Onion skin (laminated) periosteal reaction — characteristic
  • Large soft tissue mass (often disproportionately large)
  • Diaphyseal location

Staging: MRI (local) + CT Chest + bone scan/PET-CT

Chondrosarcoma​

Most common primary malignant bone tumor in adults (40-60 years)

Location: Pelvis, proximal femur, ribs, scapula; long bone metaphysis/diaphysis

Radiographic features:

  • Lobular, expanding mass
  • Cartilage matrix: "rings and arcs" calcification (fluffy, endosteal)
  • Endosteal scalloping of cortex (>2/3 cortical thickness = aggressive)
  • Soft tissue component in high-grade

Grade: Low (I), intermediate (II), high (III) — higher grade = worse prognosis

Bone Metastases​

Most common malignant bone tumor (by far) in adults >40 years

Primary sources ("top 5 common + 2 rare"):

  • Lung, Breast, Prostate, Kidney, Thyroid (commonly "BLT and KT")

Type:

  • Lytic (destructive): Kidney, thyroid, lung, breast, myeloma
  • Sclerotic (blastic): Prostate, breast (can be mixed), carcinoid, lymphoma
  • Mixed: Breast (most common mixed), lung

Radiographic features:

  • Multiple lesions (usually)
  • No periosteal reaction (unless pathological fracture present)
  • Spine: Vertebral body involvement most common
  • "Winking owl sign": Absent pedicle on AP X-ray (destruction of one pedicle) → suspicious for metastasis

Bone scan (scintigraphy):

  • Sensitive for osteoblastic metastases
  • May miss purely lytic (myeloma, thyroid, renal)

39.4 Osteomyelitis​

Pathogenesis​

Routes of infection:

  1. Hematogenous (most common in children): Bacteremia seeds metaphysis (most vascular)
  2. Direct inoculation: Open fracture, surgery, puncture wound
  3. Contiguous spread: Soft tissue infection spreading to bone

Most common organism: Staphylococcus aureus

Acute Osteomyelitis​

Radiographic features (X-ray):

  • Often NORMAL in first 7-14 days
  • Earliest: Periosteal reaction and soft tissue swelling
  • Later: Lytic bone destruction; permeative pattern

MRI (gold standard for early detection):

  • T1: Low signal marrow (replacement of fat by edema/pus)
  • T2/STIR: High signal in medullary cavity
  • Enhancing after contrast
  • Soft tissue involvement
  • Detects infection within 3-5 days of onset

Bone scan:

  • Hot spot (3-phase bone scan): All 3 phases increased
  • Sensitive early
  • Poor specificity

Chronic Osteomyelitis​

Radiographic features:

  • Sequestrum: Dead bone (dense, surrounded by lytic zone) within the infection
  • Involucrum: New periosteal bone surrounding the infected zone (reactive)
  • Cloaca: Opening in cortex where pus drains
  • Sclerosis and cortical thickening

MRI:

  • Sinus tracts (low T1, high T2)
  • "Burrowing" abscess
  • Adjacent soft tissue changes

Brodie's Abscess​

  • Localized form of chronic subacute osteomyelitis
  • Well-defined lytic lesion in metaphysis with sclerotic rim
  • "Penumbra sign" on MRI: Low T1 signal zone within the high signal edema = abscess granulation tissue
  • Common in tibial metaphysis

Vertebral Osteomyelitis / Discitis​

  • Disc space infection spreads to adjacent vertebral endplates
  • Earliest sign: Endplate erosion and disc space narrowing
  • MRI: T1 low signal in disc and adjacent vertebrae; T2 high signal; enhancing with contrast
  • Complication: Epidural abscess (MRI urgently if neurological signs)

39.5 Distinguishing Tumor from Infection​

FeatureTumorOsteomyelitis
LocationMetaphysis (most)Metaphysis (hematogenous)
Soft tissueMassInflammatory edema
Adjacent jointUsually sparedOften involved (septic arthritis)
Disc/joints (spine)Disc preserved in metastasesDisc destroyed in discitis
MRI enhancementPeripheral (necrotic core)Diffuse throughout
Temperature/WBCVariableUsually elevated
Sinus tractAbsentMay be present (chronic)

Biopsy: Often required for definitive diagnosis

Key Points Summary​

  1. Zone of transition: Narrow = benign; wide = aggressive/malignant

  2. Osteochondroma — continuous cortex and medulla with parent bone; points away from joint; malignant transformation rare (<1% solitary)

  3. GCT — epiphyseal, eccentric, "soap bubble"; in skeletally mature patients 20-40 years

  4. Osteosarcoma — sunburst periosteal reaction + Codman's triangle; distal femur/proximal tibia; 10-25 years

  5. Ewing's sarcoma — onion-skin periosteal reaction; diaphysis; large soft tissue mass; 10-20 years

  6. Sequestrum + Involucrum = pathognomonic of chronic osteomyelitis

  7. Metastases — most common malignant bone lesion in adults >40; prostate = sclerotic; renal/thyroid = lytic
