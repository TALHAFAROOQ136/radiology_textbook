Chapter 32: Approach to Skeletal Trauma

Learning Objectives​

  • Apply the ABCS mnemonic for systematic fracture evaluation
  • Describe a fracture using standard radiological terminology
  • Classify fractures by pattern, displacement, and angulation
  • Differentiate open from closed fractures
  • Recognize fracture healing and its complications

32.1 ABCS Mnemonic for Fracture Evaluation​

Systematic approach prevents missed injuries:

A — Alignment

  • Assess bony alignment and articular congruency
  • Look for dislocation, subluxation
  • Check joint spaces

B — Bone Density and Architecture

  • Normal bone density? (osteoporosis, metastases, Paget's)
  • Cortical integrity: cortical disruption?
  • Trabecular pattern: abnormal lucencies or sclerosis?

C — Cartilage/Joint Space

  • Joint space width (narrowed = arthritis)
  • Cartilage thickness (assessed by joint space on X-ray)
  • Effusion (joint swelling)

S — Soft Tissues

  • Soft tissue swelling
  • Subcutaneous emphysema (open injury)
  • Foreign bodies
  • Fat pad displacement (elbow, knee)
  • Periosteal reaction

32.2 Fracture Description — Standard Terminology​

Location​

Within the bone:

  • Epiphysis (joint surface)
  • Physis (growth plate — in children)
  • Metaphysis (flared region near growth plate)
  • Diaphysis (shaft)

Position:

  • Proximal, mid, or distal third
  • Specific anatomical regions (e.g., femoral neck, radial head)

Pattern​

PatternDescription
TransversePerpendicular to long axis; usually from direct force
ObliqueDiagonal line; angulating forces
SpiralHelical; from twisting/rotational force
ComminutedThree or more fragments; high-energy trauma
SegmentalTwo fractures creating an isolated segment
GreenstickOne cortex broken, one bent; children
Buckle (torus)Cortical buckling without complete break; children
ImpactedFragments driven together; axial loading
AvulsionFragment pulled off by tendon or ligament
StressFrom repetitive loading; no single traumatic event

Displacement​

Displacement = how much the distal fragment has moved relative to the proximal:

  • Described as % of shaft diameter (e.g., 50% displaced)
  • Direction: anterior, posterior, medial, lateral
  • Shortening or distraction

Angulation​

  • Angle between the axis of the two fragments
  • Described in degrees
  • Direction: valgus (lateral apex), varus (medial apex), anterior, posterior
  • "Apex" refers to direction the apex of the angle points (where fracture "bows")

Rotation​

  • Rotational deformity of the distal fragment
  • Assessed clinically (rotation of foot relative to leg, etc.)

Complete vs. Incomplete​

  • Complete: Fracture extends across the entire bone
  • Incomplete: One cortex intact (greenstick, buckle, stress fracture)

32.3 Fracture Classification Systems​

By Relationship to Skin​

Closed (simple): Skin intact over fracture

  • Low infection risk
  • Conservative or surgical management options

Open (compound): Skin broken, fracture communicates with environment

  • High infection risk
  • Requires emergency irrigation and debridement
  • Gustilo-Anderson classification (I, II, III by wound size and contamination)

General Anatomical Classifications​

Many specific fractures have named classifications:

  • Colles fracture: Distal radius; dorsal angulation
  • Smith fracture: Distal radius; volar angulation ("reverse Colles")
  • Barton fracture: Intra-articular distal radius
  • Salter-Harris: Physeal (growth plate) fractures in children

32.4 Open vs. Closed Fractures​

Open Fracture Signs on Imaging​

  • Subcutaneous emphysema (gas in soft tissues on X-ray or CT)
  • Wound communication visible clinically
  • Soft tissue swelling and disruption

Gustilo-Anderson Classification (Open Fractures)​

GradeWound SizeContaminationTissuePrognosis
I<1 cmMinimalMinimalGood
II1-10 cmModerateModerateGood
IIIa>10 cmHighExtensiveModerate
IIIb>10 cmHighPeriosteal strippingPoor
IIIcAnyHighVascular injury requiring repairWorst

32.5 Fracture Healing and Complications​

Normal Fracture Healing Stages​

StageTimeDescription
Inflammatory0-2 weeksHematoma formation; inflammation
Soft callus2-6 weeksFibrocartilage and woven bone
Hard callus6-12 weeksLamellar bone replacing woven bone
RemodelingMonths-yearsCallus remodeling to cortical bone

Radiographic Signs of Healing​

  • Periosteal new bone (callus): First visible ~10-14 days (earliest X-ray sign)
  • Bridging callus: fracture line bridged by callus
  • Disappearing fracture line
  • Cortical continuity re-established
  • Remodeling: smooth cortical contour

Complications of Fractures​

Early complications:

  • Neurovascular injury (nearby vessels and nerves)
  • Compartment syndrome (increased intracompartmental pressure → ischemia)
  • Fat embolism (especially femoral shaft fractures)

Late complications:

ComplicationRadiographic Feature
MalunionFracture healed with deformity
Non-unionFracture not healed by expected time; persistent fracture line; sclerotic margins; no bridging callus
Delayed unionSlower than expected but still healing
Avascular necrosis (AVN)Bone death; sclerosis, then collapse
OsteomyelitisBone destruction, periosteal reaction, sequestrum/involucrum
Post-traumatic arthritisIntra-articular fracture → joint space narrowing
Growth plate arrestPremature closure of physis in children

Non-Union Types​

Hypertrophic non-union:

  • Adequate blood supply but insufficient stability
  • "Elephant foot" callus at fracture ends
  • Treatment: Fixation

Atrophic non-union:

  • Inadequate blood supply
  • No callus; fracture ends osteopenic, rounded
  • Treatment: Bone graft + fixation

Key Points Summary​

  1. ABCS — systematic approach: Alignment, Bone density, Cartilage/joints, Soft tissues

  2. Fracture description: Location, pattern, complete/incomplete, displacement (%), angulation (degrees/direction), rotation

  3. Open fractures — skin disrupted; emergency treatment; Gustilo-Anderson grading

  4. First X-ray sign of healing: Periosteal callus (10-14 days)

  5. Non-union: Persistent fracture line with sclerotic margins; no bridging callus; either hypertrophic or atrophic

  6. Compartment syndrome — clinical diagnosis; extreme pain, tense compartment; requires fasciotomy
