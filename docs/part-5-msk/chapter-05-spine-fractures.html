Chapter 35: Spine Fractures and Trauma

Learning Objectives​

  • Classify cervical spine fractures by type and mechanism
  • Identify thoracolumbar fractures on plain radiographs and CT
  • Recognize compression fractures from osteoporosis
  • Describe C-spine clearance protocols
  • Understand burst fractures and their neurological implications

35.1 Cervical Spine Fractures​

Jefferson Fracture (C1 Burst Fracture)​

Mechanism: Axial compression (e.g., diving into shallow water)

Radiographic features:

  • Bilateral fractures through C1 ring (anterior and posterior arches)
  • Open-mouth view: bilateral overhang of C1 lateral masses beyond C2
  • Rule of Spence: Total lateral overhang >7 mm = ligament disruption (unstable)

Hangman's Fracture (Traumatic Spondylolisthesis of C2)​

Mechanism: Hyperextension + axial compression (judicial hanging, MVA, diving)

Radiographic features:

  • Bilateral pedicle fractures of C2 (pars interarticularis)
  • Anterior displacement of C2 body relative to C3
  • Paradoxically often STABLE (bilateral fracture allows decompression)

Odontoid (Dens) Fracture​

Types (Anderson and D'Alonzo):

TypeLocationUnion Rate
ITip of dens (oblique)Good
IIJunction of dens and bodyPoor (15-30%) — non-union risk
IIIThrough body of C2Good

Type II has highest non-union rate — consider surgical fixation

Imaging: Open-mouth view; CT multi-planar reconstruction

Facet Joint Injuries (C-Spine)​

Unilateral facet dislocation:

  • Rotation to ipsilateral side
  • Lateral view: 25% anterior subluxation
  • AP view: spinous process deviation toward distal side
  • "Bow-tie" sign on lateral

Bilateral facet dislocation:

  • 50%+ anterior subluxation
  • Usually associated with significant cord injury
  • CT essential for pre-reduction imaging (disc herniation must be excluded)

Clay Shoveler's Fracture​

  • Avulsion of spinous process (usually C6 or C7)
  • Mechanism: sudden flexion of neck
  • "Double spinous process" sign on lateral X-ray
  • Stable; conservative treatment

35.2 Thoracic and Lumbar Spine Fractures​

Denis Three-Column Classification​

Understanding stability requires assessment of three columns:

ColumnContents
AnteriorAnterior 2/3 of vertebral body, anterior disc, anterior longitudinal ligament
MiddlePosterior 1/3 of vertebral body, posterior disc, posterior longitudinal ligament
PosteriorPedicles, facets, laminae, spinous processes, posterior ligament complex

Stability:

  • 1 column disrupted: Stable
  • 2 columns disrupted: Borderline
  • 3 columns disrupted: Unstable

Compression Fractures​

Definition: Failure of anterior column only; middle and posterior columns intact

Mechanism: Axial compression + flexion; common in osteoporosis

Radiographic features:

  • Loss of anterior vertebral body height
  • Preservation of posterior wall height (key — if posterior wall involved = burst fracture)
  • End-plate irregularity
  • Wedge deformity (anterior > posterior height loss)

Typical levels: Thoracolumbar junction (T11-L2 most common)

Classification by severity (simple vs. severe):

  • <25% height loss: mild
  • 25-50%: moderate

  • 50%: severe

Burst Fracture​

Definition: Failure of anterior AND middle columns

Mechanism: Axial compression (higher energy than compression fracture)

Radiographic features:

  • Vertebral body comminution
  • Posterior wall disruption (middle column)
  • Widening of interpedicular distance on AP view
  • Retropulsed fragment into spinal canal (CT essential)

Neurological risk: Retropulsed fragment may compress spinal cord or cauda equina

Stability: Two-column failure → potentially unstable

CT: Essential for all burst fractures — quantify canal compromise

Chance Fracture (Seat Belt Fracture)​

Mechanism: Severe flexion-distraction (lap belt injury)

  • Flexion around lap belt creates distraction of posterior elements

Three types:

  • Bony (B): Through vertebral body only
  • Ligamentous (L): Through disc and ligaments
  • Combined (BL)

Radiographic features:

  • Horizontal fracture through vertebral body (bony type)
  • AP view: Horizontal fracture through pedicles, transverse processes
  • "Empty vertebral body" on AP view

High association with intestinal injuries (bowel perforation)

Fracture-Dislocation​

  • All three columns disrupted
  • Severe instability
  • High neurological injury rate
  • Emergency surgical stabilization

35.3 Compression Fractures and Osteoporosis​

Osteoporotic Compression Fractures​

Epidemiology:

  • Predominantly postmenopausal women
  • Vertebral fractures: most common manifestation of osteoporosis
  • Many are clinically silent (50% of vertebral fractures undetected)

Radiographic assessment:

  • Serial loss of vertebral height over time
  • Multiple levels often affected
  • Compare to prior images

Vertebral Fracture Assessment (VFA):

  • DEXA-based lateral spine imaging
  • Detects vertebral compression fractures
  • Genant semi-quantitative method: mild (20-25% height loss), moderate (25-40%), severe (>40%)

Differentiating Benign vs. Malignant Compression Fracture​

FeatureBenign/OsteoporoticMalignant
T1 signal (MRI)Low band (acute edema) → normalizedLow signal throughout
T2/STIR signalHigh (edema) → resolvesHigh; often persistent
Contrast enhancementDiffuseEnhancing soft tissue component
Posterior elementsIntactMay be involved
Multiple levelsCommonCommon
Associated soft tissue massAbsentEpidural or paraspinal mass
DWI (MRI)Low diffusion restrictionRestricted diffusion (high signal)

35.4 C-Spine Clearance​

Clinical Clearance (No Imaging)​

NEXUS Criteria: All 5 must be met:

  1. No posterior midline cervical tenderness
  2. No focal neurological deficit
  3. Normal alertness
  4. No intoxication
  5. No distracting injury

Imaging Approaches​

X-ray series (3 views): AP, lateral, open-mouth

  • Sensitivity: ~85-90% (inadequate for obtunded patients)
  • Must visualize all 7 cervical vertebrae

CT Cervical Spine:

  • Obtunded patients
  • High-energy mechanism
  • X-ray inadequate
  • Sensitivity >99% for fractures

MRI:

  • Neurological deficit with negative CT
  • Ligamentous injury evaluation
  • Spinal cord hemorrhage/contusion

Spinal Cord Injury​

Complete vs. Incomplete:

  • Complete: No motor or sensory function below the level of injury
  • Incomplete: Some preserved function

Spinal shock:

  • Temporary neurological dysfunction after spinal cord injury
  • May cause false-complete injury picture
  • Resolves over hours-days

ASIA Impairment Scale:

  • A: Complete — no motor or sensory at S4-S5
  • B: Sensory incomplete
  • C: Motor incomplete — most <3/5
  • D: Motor incomplete — most ≥3/5
  • E: Normal

Key Points Summary​

  1. Jefferson fracture — C1 ring burst; diving injury; open-mouth view shows bilateral C1 overhang

  2. Odontoid Type II — junction of dens and C2 body; highest non-union risk; consider surgery

  3. Burst fracture — 2-column failure (anterior + middle); retropulsed fragment into canal; CT essential

  4. Chance fracture — horizontal flexion-distraction; seat belt injury; associated with bowel perforation

  5. Denis 3-column model: 2+ column failure = unstable

  6. Malignant compression fracture — abnormal posterior elements, epidural mass, restricted diffusion on DWI (MRI)
