Chapter 47: Spinal Cord Pathology

Learning Objectives​

  • Describe MRI evaluation of spinal cord compression
  • Recognize myelitis and inflammatory cord lesions
  • Identify syringomyelia on MRI
  • Describe vascular malformations of the spinal cord
  • Explain indications for urgent spinal MRI

47.1 Spinal Cord Compression​

Causes​

Extradural (outside dura — most common):

  • Disc herniation
  • Metastatic spine disease (vertebral mets → epidural extension)
  • Epidural abscess
  • Hematoma
  • Bone fragments (trauma)

Intradural extramedullary (inside dura, outside cord):

  • Meningioma
  • Schwannoma / Neurofibroma
  • Ependymoma (of cauda equina)
  • Leptomeningeal carcinomatosis

Intramedullary (within cord itself):

  • Ependymoma (most common intramedullary cord tumor — adults)
  • Astrocytoma (children)
  • Metastasis (rare)
  • Cavernoma

MRI for Cord Compression​

Protocol:

  • Sagittal T1, T2: Anatomy, cord signal, disc levels
  • Axial T1, T2: Degree of compression, laterality
  • Post-contrast T1: Epidural abscess, tumor enhancement

Cord findings indicating chronic compression:

  • T2 bright signal (myelopathy): Chronic cord damage
  • Cord atrophy

Emergency cord compression:

  • Metastatic spinal cord compression (MSCC): Oncological emergency
  • MRI whole spine if metastatic disease suspected (may be multifocal)

47.2 Myelitis and Inflammatory Conditions​

Transverse Myelitis​

Definition: Acute/subacute inflammatory demyelinating cord lesion crossing the cord width.

Causes:

  • Multiple sclerosis (MS)
  • Neuromyelitis optica spectrum disorder (NMOSD — anti-AQP4 antibodies)
  • Idiopathic
  • Post-viral (HSV, EBV)
  • Autoimmune

MRI features:

  • T2 bright intramedullary signal
  • Cord swelling (acute)
  • Spans >1 vertebral level (usually)
  • Enhancement in active lesion

Short vs. long cord lesions:

  • Short (<3 vertebral levels): Typical for MS
  • Long (>3 vertebral levels — LETM = Longitudinally Extensive Transverse Myelitis): Typical for NMOSD; also infectious, anti-NMDAR

Multiple Sclerosis (MS) Spinal Cord​

Typical MS cord lesions:

  • Cervical cord most common
  • Short (<3 vertebral levels)
  • Peripheral (dorsal, lateral — NOT central)
  • Ovoid on sagittal view
  • T2 bright; may enhance with contrast (active lesion)

NMOSD (Neuromyelitis Optica):

  • Longitudinally extensive cord lesions (>3 vertebral levels)
  • Central cord involvement
  • Often with optic neuritis
  • Anti-AQP4 antibody positive

47.3 Syrinx (Syringomyelia)​

Definition​

A fluid-filled cavity within the spinal cord.

Communicating syrinx: Connected to central canal of cord; extension of hydromyelia

Non-communicating syrinx: Not connected to central canal; from trauma, tumor, arachnoiditis, Chiari malformation

Causes​

  • Chiari I malformation (most common cause): Cerebellar tonsils herniate through foramen magnum → impair CSF flow → syrinx
  • Post-traumatic
  • Intramedullary tumor
  • Arachnoiditis
  • Tethered cord

MRI Features​

  • Well-defined fluid-filled cavity within cord
  • T1: Low (CSF density)
  • T2: Bright (CSF signal)
  • No enhancement (unless infected or tumoral)
  • Cord may be widened
  • Associated Chiari malformation visible on brain MRI

Chiari Malformation​

Chiari I:

  • Cerebellar tonsils >5 mm below foramen magnum
  • Most common in adults; may be incidental
  • Associated syrinx in 25%
  • Symptoms: Occipital headache aggravated by Valsalva, syrinx symptoms

Chiari II:

  • Associated with myelomeningocele
  • Cerebellar vermis, pons, medulla all herniate
  • Tectal beaking; "banana" sign of cerebellum on antenatal US
  • Lemon sign (skull shape) on antenatal US

47.4 Vascular Malformations of the Spine​

Spinal Arteriovenous Malformation (AVM)​

Types:

TypeDescriptionPresentation
Intradural AVMNidus within cordHemorrhage, myelopathy
Dural AVF (most common)Fistula at dural nerve root sleeveProgressive myelopathy
Perimedullary AVFOn surface of cordProgressive or hemorrhage

Spinal Dural AVF (most common spinal vascular malformation):

  • Middle-aged to elderly men
  • Progressive ascending myelopathy (venous hypertension)
  • MRI: T2 high signal cord; "flow voids" on dorsal cord surface (engorged veins)
  • MRA/Angiography for diagnosis

Cavernoma (Cavernous Malformation)​

  • Spinal cord cavernoma: Rare
  • MRI: "Popcorn" appearance; hemosiderin rim
  • Presents with acute hemorrhage → myelopathy
  • Surgical excision if accessible

47.5 Intramedullary Tumors​

Ependymoma​

Most common intramedullary tumor in adults

Location: Cervical cord or conus medullaris (myxopapillary ependymoma)

MRI features:

  • Well-defined, centrally placed within cord
  • T2 bright; may have associated syrinx (rostral and caudal)
  • "Polar cysts" at tumor margins
  • "Cap sign": Hemosiderin caps at superior and inferior tumor poles (from previous hemorrhage) — dark on T2/GRE
  • Enhancement: Solid, heterogeneous

Astrocytoma​

Most common intramedullary tumor in children

MRI features:

  • Poorly defined margins (infiltrative)
  • Often extends over multiple levels
  • Enhancement: Variable, patchy
  • No "cap sign"
  • Associated syrinx

Differentiating Ependymoma from Astrocytoma​

FeatureEpendymomaAstrocytoma
MarginsWell-definedPoorly defined
LocationCentralEccentric
EnhancementSolid, well-definedIrregular, patchy
Hemosiderin capsPresent (cap sign)Absent
AgeAdultsChildren

47.6 When to Order Urgent Spinal MRI​

Urgent Indications (<24 hours)​

  • Acute myelopathy: Sudden onset of bilateral weakness, sensory level, bladder/bowel dysfunction
  • Suspected spinal cord compression (metastatic): Known cancer + new neurological signs
  • Cauda equina syndrome: Bilateral leg weakness + saddle anesthesia + urinary/bowel dysfunction
  • Epidural abscess: Fever + spine pain + neurological deficit

Cauda Equina Syndrome​

Clinical features:

  • Bilateral leg weakness
  • Saddle anesthesia (perineum, inner thighs)
  • Urinary retention (more specific than incontinence)
  • Fecal incontinence or retention

Imaging:

  • Emergency lumbar MRI
  • Cause: Central disc herniation (L4-L5 or L5-S1 most common), tumor, hematoma, abscess

Key Points Summary​

  1. Cord compression levels: Extradural (metastases, disc) → Intradural extramedullary (meningioma, schwannoma) → Intramedullary (ependymoma, astrocytoma)

  2. Short cord lesions (<3 levels) = MS; Long cord lesions (LETM, >3 levels) = NMOSD or infection

  3. Syrinx — most often associated with Chiari I malformation; CSF-filled cord cavity on T2 MRI

  4. Ependymoma — central cord, "cap sign" (hemosiderin poles), well-defined, adults

  5. Spinal dural AVF — progressive myelopathy; flow voids on dorsal cord; diagnose with spinal MRA/angiography

  6. Cauda equina syndrome = surgical emergency; bilateral leg weakness + saddle anesthesia + urinary retention
