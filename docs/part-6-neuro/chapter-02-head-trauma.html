Chapter 41: Head Trauma Imaging

Learning Objectives​

  • Identify skull fracture types on CT and plain radiography
  • Differentiate epidural from subdural hematoma radiographically
  • Recognize subarachnoid hemorrhage on non-contrast CT
  • Describe cerebral contusions and diffuse axonal injury
  • Assess midline shift and herniation on CT

41.1 Initial Imaging Approach​

Non-Contrast CT Head​

First-line investigation for all significant head trauma.

Advantages:

  • Rapid (completed in <5 min)
  • Excellent for detecting acute blood (hyperdense)
  • No sedation required
  • Detects fractures, hemorrhage, edema
  • Available 24/7 in most centers

CT Hounsfield Units (HU) — Key Densities:

StructureHU
Air-1000
Fat-100
CSF0
Soft tissue/brain20-60
Acute blood50-80
Subacute blood35-50 (isodense)
Bone400-1000

Acute blood appears bright (white) because hemoglobin is proteinaceous and absorbs more X-rays.

41.2 Skull Fractures​

Linear Skull Fracture​

Most common type

Radiographic features:

  • Thin dark line on skull X-ray or CT
  • Lacks sclerotic margins (unlike venous lakes/sutures)
  • Does not follow suture lines
  • May cross vascular grooves (middle meningeal artery) → epidural hematoma risk

Key concern: If fracture crosses middle meningeal groove → epidural hematoma

Depressed Skull Fracture​

  • Cortical fragment driven below inner table
  • Significant if depressed >1 cm or depth greater than skull thickness
  • Requires surgical elevation if:
    • Cosmetically significant
    • Underlying dural tear/brain compression
    • Open fracture (contaminated)

CT (3D reconstruction): Best for assessing degree of depression and surgical planning

Basilar Skull Fracture​

Location: Base of skull (temporal, sphenoid, ethmoid, occipital)

Clinical signs:

  • Raccoon eyes (periorbital ecchymosis — anterior fossa)
  • Battle's sign (mastoid ecchymosis — posterior fossa, temporal bone)
  • CSF rhinorrhea (anterior fossa fracture → middle cranial fossa)
  • CSF otorrhea
  • Hemotympanum

CT findings:

  • Fracture through skull base (temporal bone most common)
  • Pneumocephalus (air in subdural/epidural space)
  • Hemorrhage in mastoid air cells / paranasal sinuses
  • Blood in sphenoid sinus

41.3 Epidural Hematoma (EDH)​

Pathophysiology​

Arterial bleeding (usually middle meningeal artery) into potential space between skull and dura.

Key: Dura is stripped from inner table by expanding hematoma

"Lucid interval" Classic History​

  • Initial brief LOC → lucid → rapid deterioration
  • Due to: arterial bleeding → expanding hematoma → herniation
  • Classic but only in minority of cases

Radiographic Features​

CT (classic):

  • Biconvex (lenticular) hyperdense collection adjacent to skull
  • Typically temporal location (middle meningeal artery)
  • Does NOT cross sutures (dura attached at sutures)
  • May cross midline but not falx
  • May show "swirl sign": mixed density = active arterial bleeding (emergency)
  • Hyperdense (acute blood: 50-80 HU)
  • Mass effect: Midline shift, effacement of ipsilateral ventricle

Associated:

  • Skull fracture (90% of adults with EDH)
  • Note: In children, EDH can occur WITHOUT fracture (skull more elastic)

41.4 Subdural Hematoma (SDH)​

Pathophysiology​

Venous bleeding from bridging veins between cortex and dural sinuses.

Causes:

  • Trauma (acceleration-deceleration)
  • Elderly: minor trauma may suffice (cerebral atrophy → stretched bridging veins)
  • Anticoagulant therapy
  • Non-accidental injury (infants)

CT Appearance by Age​

StageTimeAppearance
Hyperacute<6 hoursIsodense (may miss)
Acute1-3 daysHyperdense (50-80 HU)
Subacute4 days-3 weeksIsodense (isoattenuating to brain — most easily missed!)
Chronic>3 weeksHypodense (CSF density, 0-30 HU)

Radiographic Features​

  • Crescent-shaped collection along inner table
  • Follows inner table of skull (unlike EDH which is biconvex)
  • Crosses sutures (blood in subdural space freely crosses sutures)
  • Does NOT cross the falx (SDH remains ipsilateral)

Midline shift calculation:

  • Midline shift: distance that septum pellucidum is displaced from midline

  • 5 mm shift → significant; surgical threshold often >1 cm

Mixed-Density SDH​

Active rebleeding into old chronic SDH:

  • Shows hyperdense (acute) within hypodense (chronic) collection
  • "Hematocrit effect": Dense layer below lighter layer

41.5 Subarachnoid Hemorrhage (SAH)​

Causes​

  • Traumatic SAH: Most common cause of subarachnoid blood
  • Spontaneous SAH: Berry (saccular) aneurysm rupture (most common non-traumatic)
  • AVM, reversible cerebral vasoconstriction syndrome

CT Findings​

Acute traumatic SAH:

  • Hyperdense blood within subarachnoid sulci (gyral surface)
  • Interpeduncular cistern, sylvian fissure, basalar cisterns
  • "Filled sulci" — normally CSF-filled sulci appear white

Aneurysmal SAH:

  • Classically fills basal cisterns (interpeduncular, prepontine)
  • Distribution depends on aneurysm location:
    • ACoA aneurysm: Interhemispheric fissure
    • MCA aneurysm: Sylvian fissure
    • ICA aneurysm: Suprasellar cistern

CT Sensitivity for SAH:

  • Within 12 hours: ~98-100%
  • 24 hours: ~95%
  • 7 days: ~50-70%
  • If CT negative but SAH suspected: LP for xanthochromia

Complications of SAH​

  • Vasospasm (days 4-14): TCD, CTA, or angiography
  • Hydrocephalus (obstructive or communicating)
  • Rebleeding (highest risk in first 24 hours before treatment)

41.6 Cerebral Contusions​

Definition​

Bruising of the brain surface (coup and contrecoup injuries).

Mechanism​

  • Coup: Injury at site of impact
  • Contrecoup: Injury opposite to impact (brain bounces against skull)

Susceptible locations: Frontal and temporal poles (irregular skull base)

CT Findings​

Acute:

  • Heterogeneous foci of hyperdensity (hemorrhage) within hypodense area (edema)
  • "Salt and pepper" appearance
  • At cortical surface
  • May enlarge over 24-48 hours ("contusion blooming")

MRI (more sensitive):

  • Detects non-hemorrhagic contusions
  • T2/FLAIR: High signal
  • GRE/SWI: Blood products as dark "blooming" foci

41.7 Diffuse Axonal Injury (DAI)​

Mechanism​

High-speed angular deceleration → shear injury to axons at gray-white matter junction

CT Findings​

  • Often normal CT despite severe clinical injury
  • When visible: Small hemorrhagic foci at:
    • Gray-white matter junction
    • Corpus callosum
    • Dorsolateral brainstem (rostral midbrain)
    • Internal capsule

MRI for DAI​

  • CT misses most non-hemorrhagic DAI
  • SWI (Susceptibility Weighted Imaging): Most sensitive; numerous dark "blooming" foci represent microhemorrhages
  • DWI: Restricted diffusion in acutely injured axons
  • FLAIR: T2 signal abnormality at gray-white junction

41.8 Midline Shift and Herniation​

Assessing Midline Shift​

  • Measured at the level of the foramen of Monro
  • Distance from septum pellucidum to midline (falx)

  • 5 mm is significant; >10 mm often requires surgical intervention

Transtentorial (Uncal) Herniation​

Most dangerous type

CT signs:

  • Effacement of ipsilateral suprasellar cistern
  • Displacement of uncus and temporal lobe medially and downward
  • Loss of normal basal cisterns
  • CN III compression (ipsilateral fixed dilated pupil)
  • Contralateral peduncle compression → ipsilateral hemiplegia

Subfalcine Herniation​

  • Cingulate gyrus herniates under falx cerebri
  • CT: Midline shift; compression of ipsilateral lateral ventricle
  • Anterior cerebral artery may be compressed → contralateral leg weakness

Tonsillar Herniation​

  • Cerebellar tonsils herniate through foramen magnum
  • Compresses brainstem
  • CT: Low-lying cerebellar tonsils; effacement of cisterna magna

Key Points Summary​

  1. Acute blood appears bright (hyperdense, 50-80 HU) on non-contrast CT; becomes isodense (subacute), then hypodense (chronic) for SDH

  2. EDH — biconvex shape; does not cross sutures; usually temporal (middle meningeal artery); 90% have skull fracture

  3. SDH — crescent shape; crosses sutures but NOT falx; subacute SDH can be isodense (most easily missed)

  4. CT sensitivity for SAH decreases rapidly after onset; LP for xanthochromia if CT negative at >6-12 hours

  5. DAI — often normal CT despite severe injury; SWI MRI most sensitive for microhemorrhages

  6. Midline shift >5 mm is significant; loss of basal cisterns indicates impending herniation
