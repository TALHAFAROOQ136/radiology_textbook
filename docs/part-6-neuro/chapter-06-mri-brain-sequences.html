Chapter 45: MRI Brain Sequences and Interpretation

Learning Objectives​

  • Describe the clinical uses of each key MRI sequence
  • Identify normal anatomy on T1-weighted MRI
  • Use T2/FLAIR to detect pathology
  • Explain DWI and ADC map interpretation
  • Recognize gradient echo findings for hemorrhage
  • Know when to order MRI vs. CT

45.1 T1-Weighted Imaging​

Characteristics​

T1-weighted images best show anatomy (gray matter vs. white matter).

Signal characteristics:

StructureT1 Signal
White matterHigh (bright) — due to myelin
Gray matterIntermediate
CSFLow (dark)
FatVery high (bright)
Acute bloodVariable → Subacute = Bright
Gadolinium enhancementBright

Clinical Uses​

  1. Anatomy: Demonstrate normal brain architecture
  2. Subacute hemorrhage: Methemoglobin is T1-bright (paramagnetic)
  3. Post-contrast enhancement: Gadolinium shortens T1 → lesions enhance (appear bright)
  4. Fat: Lipoma, dermoid — hyperintense
  5. Melanin: Melanoma metastases — T1 bright
  6. Calcification: Usually hypointense (but can vary)

Post-Contrast T1​

Pathological enhancement requires breach of blood-brain barrier (BBB):

  • Tumors (GBM, metastases, meningioma, lymphoma)
  • Infection (abscess ring, meningitis)
  • Active demyelination (MS plaque)
  • Subacute infarction (luxury perfusion, day 5-30)
  • Arteriovenous malformations

45.2 T2-Weighted and FLAIR Imaging​

T2-Weighted Imaging​

Signal characteristics:

StructureT2 Signal
CSFHigh (bright)
EdemaHigh (bright)
Gray matterIntermediate-high
White matterIntermediate-low
Cortical boneLow (dark)
AirVery low (dark)
Fibrous tissueLow

T2 is sensitive for pathology because most pathological processes increase tissue water content → increase T2 signal

Uses:

  • Cerebral edema
  • Infarction (after 6-12 hours)
  • Tumor infiltration
  • Demyelinating lesions (MS plaques)
  • ILD, inflammation, infection

FLAIR (Fluid-Attenuated Inversion Recovery)​

CSF signal is suppressed (nulled):

  • Normal CSF = dark (unlike T2 where CSF = bright)
  • This makes periventricular and cortical lesions MORE visible

Key uses:

  • Periventricular MS plaques (more visible than T2 because CSF nulled)
  • Subarachnoid hemorrhage (SAH): FLAIR-bright SAH visible when CT borderline
  • Cortical infarction
  • Meningitis (abnormal sulcal signal)
  • DWI-FLAIR mismatch for stroke timing (see Chapter 42)

FLAIR may show CSF bright if:

  • High protein CSF (meningitis, SAH)
  • Incomplete CSF nulling (artifact)
  • Hemorrhage within CSF (SAH)

45.3 Diffusion-Weighted Imaging (DWI)​

Physical Principle​

Brownian motion of water molecules:

  • Normal brain: Random water motion (unrestricted)
  • Ischemic or hypercellular tissue: Restricted water motion

DWI signal:

  • High DWI = restricted diffusion OR T2 "shine-through"
  • Must check ADC map to confirm restriction

ADC (Apparent Diffusion Coefficient) Map:

  • Low ADC = restricted diffusion (TRUE restriction)
  • High ADC = facilitated diffusion

True restricted diffusion: DWI high + ADC low

Clinical Applications​

ConditionDWI SignalADC
Acute infarctionHigh (bright)Low (dark)
Abscess (pus)High (bright)Low (dark)
Hypercellular tumor (PCNSL, GBM)HighLow
Epidermoid cystHigh (bright)Low
Chronic infarctionLowHigh
EdemaVariableHigh

T2 "Shine-Through"​

  • High T2 signal causes high DWI signal regardless of diffusion
  • If DWI high but ADC is ALSO high → NOT true restriction → T2 shine-through
  • Always check BOTH DWI and ADC

45.4 Gradient Echo (GRE) and Susceptibility Weighted Imaging (SWI)​

Principle​

Blood products (hemosiderin, deoxyhemoglobin) are paramagnetic → create local field disturbances → "blooming" artifact (appear larger than actual size)

Blood Appearance on GRE/SWI by Age​

StageBlood ProductMRI Appearance
Hyperacute (<24h)OxyHbT2 high; GRE dark
Acute (1-3 days)DeoxyHbT2 dark; GRE dark
Early subacute (4-7 days)MetHb (intracellular)T1 bright; T2 dark
Late subacute (1-4 wk)MetHb (extracellular)T1 bright; T2 bright
Chronic (>4 wk)HemosiderinT2 dark; GRE very dark (blooming)

Clinical Applications of GRE/SWI​

  1. Cerebral microbleeds: Tiny dark foci (hemosiderin); hypertension, CAA, DAI
  2. Cavernomas: "Popcorn" appearance with dark hemosiderin halo
  3. DAI: Multiple microhemorrhagic foci at gray-white junction, corpus callosum
  4. AVM: Flow voids + hemosiderin
  5. Hemorrhagic transformation of infarction

SWI is more sensitive than GRE for microbleeds.

45.5 Post-Contrast T1 Imaging​

Gadolinium Mechanisms​

  • Gadolinium shortens T1 relaxation time
  • Blood-brain barrier (BBB) intact: No contrast in brain parenchyma (stays in vessels)
  • BBB disrupted: Gadolinium leaks into tissue → enhances

Types of Enhancement​

PatternTypical Pathology
Solid homogeneousMeningioma, lymphoma
Ring-enhancingGBM, abscess, metastasis, tumefactive MS
Linear (leptomeningeal)Meningitis, carcinomatosis
SubependymalLymphoma, CMV, TB
Curvilinear (gyral)Subacute infarct (luxury perfusion)
Multiple nodularMetastases, abscesses

45.6 When to Order MRI vs. CT​

CT is Preferred​

ScenarioReason
Acute traumaFast, detects blood, fractures
Acute stroke (exclude hemorrhage)Rapid; available 24/7
Suspected subarachnoid hemorrhageFast and sensitive
Bone lesionsSuperior bone detail
Pulmonary diseaseCT better than MRI for lungs
Patient with MRI contraindicationPacemaker, certain implants

MRI is Preferred​

ScenarioReason
Brain tumorsSuperior soft tissue contrast
Spinal cordMRI required (CT inadequate)
Acute stroke (definitive)DWI most sensitive
White matter disease (MS)FLAIR periventricular lesions
Posterior fossa pathologyNo bone artifact
Posterior fossa, brainstemCT artifact; MRI superior
Soft tissue tumorsSuperior characterization
Bone marrowMRI far superior

MRI Contraindications​

Absolute:

  • Cardiac pacemaker (unless MRI-conditional)
  • Cochlear implants (unless MRI-conditional)
  • Intraocular metallic foreign bodies
  • Some cerebral aneurysm clips (depends on type)

Relative:

  • First trimester pregnancy (avoid elective MRI; acceptable for urgent indication)
  • Claustrophobia (open MRI or sedation may help)
  • Metallic implants (assess type before scanning)
  • Tattoos with metallic ink (can heat)

Key Points Summary​

  1. T1: Anatomy; fat/myelin bright; subacute blood bright; gadolinium enhancement

  2. T2/FLAIR: Pathology (high signal = edema, infarction, tumor, demyelination); FLAIR nulls CSF → better periventricular/cortical lesion detection

  3. DWI + ADC: True restricted diffusion = DWI high + ADC low → acute ischemia, abscess, hypercellular tumor

  4. GRE/SWI: Sensitive for blood products (hemosiderin "blooming"); microbleeds, DAI, cavernomas

  5. Blood-brain barrier disruption allows gadolinium enhancement → tumors, infection, active demyelination, subacute infarction

  6. CT first for: Acute trauma, hemorrhage exclusion, acute stroke; MRI for: Tumor, MS, spinal cord, posterior fossa, subacute stroke
