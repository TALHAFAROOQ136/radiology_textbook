CT of the Abdomen and Pelvis

CT of the abdomen and pelvis (CTAP) is the workhorse of abdominal imaging, providing rapid, comprehensive assessment of solid organs, bowel, vascular structures, and the peritoneum. Understanding CT technique and systematic interpretation is essential for every radiologist.

CT Technique​

Patient Preparation​

  • Fasting: 4-6 hours for elective studies (contrast reactions more likely with full stomach)
  • Oral contrast: Dilute barium or water-soluble contrast given 45-60 minutes before scan
  • IV access: Required for intravenous contrast administration

Scan Parameters​

  • Slice thickness: 3-5 mm standard; 1-1.5 mm for reconstructions
  • Field of view: Diaphragm to symphysis pubis
  • Reconstruction planes: Axial, coronal, and sagittal reformats routinely

Contrast Phases​

Non-Contrast (NECT)​

  • Indications: Renal colic, adrenal lesion characterization, haematoma detection
  • Assessment: Calcifications, haematoma density, baseline organ density

Arterial Phase (20-30 seconds post-injection)​

  • Opacification: Aorta, hepatic arteries, splenic artery brightly enhanced
  • Indications: Trauma, GI bleeding, vascular assessment, hypervascular tumors

Portal Venous Phase (60-70 seconds)​

  • Opacification: Portal vein, hepatic veins, liver parenchyma maximally enhanced
  • Best phase for: Liver metastases, bowel assessment, organ injury
  • Most commonly used routine abdominal CT phase

Delayed Phase (3-5 minutes)​

  • Indications: Renal masses (enhancement washout), biliary assessment
  • Transitional cell carcinoma: Excreted contrast outlines urothelium

Systematic Interpretation​

Windowing​

  • Soft tissue window: W400/L40 — standard abdominal assessment
  • Lung window: W1500/L-500 — bowel wall gas, pneumoperitoneum
  • Bone window: W2000/L400 — skeletal assessment
  • Liver window: W150/L75 — subtle liver lesions

Organ-by-Organ Assessment​

Liver​

  • Size: Normal craniocaudal span 15-17 cm
  • Density: Homogeneous, slightly denser than spleen on non-contrast
  • Lesion characterization: Enhancement pattern, margins, number
  • Biliary ducts: Intrahepatic ducts <2 mm, CBD <8 mm (or <10 mm post-cholecystectomy)

Gallbladder​

  • Wall thickness: Normal <3 mm
  • Gallstones: Variable density; may be hyperdense, isodense (invisible), or hypodense
  • Cholecystitis signs: Wall thickening, pericholecystic fluid, mucosal enhancement

Spleen​

  • Size: Normal <13 cm in length
  • Density: Homogeneous; tiger striping in arterial phase is normal
  • Splenic lesions: Hemangiomas, cysts, metastases

Pancreas​

  • Phases: Best seen in portal venous phase
  • Head: Overlies SMA/SMV junction
  • Duct: Normal <3 mm in body
  • Pancreatitis: Peripancreatic fat stranding, necrosis, fluid collections

Kidneys​

  • Cortex: Bright enhancement in corticomedullary phase
  • Size: 10-12 cm in length
  • Renal masses: Three-phase CT for characterization
  • Hydronephrosis: Dilated calyces, renal pelvis

Adrenal Glands​

  • Shape: Inverted V or Y shape
  • Size: Limb thickness <10 mm
  • Adenoma: Low density (<10 HU on NECT), rapid washout
  • Pheochromocytoma: Bright enhancement, may be bilateral

Aorta and Vessels​

  • Normal aorta: <3 cm diameter
  • AAA: Aneurysm >3 cm; >5.5 cm threshold for surgery
  • SMA/SMV: Assess for thrombosis, mesenteric ischemia

Bowel Assessment​

Small Bowel​

  • Normal wall thickness: <3 mm when distended
  • Enhancement: Normal mucosal enhancement
  • Ischemia signs: Wall thickening, reduced or absent enhancement, pneumatosis, portal gas

Large Bowel​

  • Normal wall thickness: <5 mm when distended
  • Diverticulosis: Outpouchings from colon wall
  • Diverticulitis: Pericolonic fat stranding, abscess formation

Appendix​

  • Normal diameter: <6 mm
  • Appendicitis: >6 mm, periappendiceal fat stranding, appendicolith, abscess

Peritoneal Assessment​

Free Fluid​

  • Ascites: Simple fluid (-10 to +10 HU), layering in dependent areas
  • Hemoperitoneum: High density fluid (40-70 HU)
  • Exudate: Intermediate density, often complex

Free Air​

  • Pneumoperitoneum: Air under diaphragm, anterior to liver, along falciform ligament
  • Retroperitoneal gas: Suggests retroperitoneal perforation

Peritoneal Deposits​

  • Peritoneal carcinomatosis: Soft tissue nodules on peritoneal surfaces, omental caking
  • Pseudomyxoma peritonei: Mucinous deposits, scalloping of liver surface

Lymph Node Assessment​

Normal Size​

  • Short axis: <10 mm is generally normal
  • Celiac/para-aortic nodes: Key stations for staging

Pathological Features​

  • Size: >10 mm short axis
  • Morphology: Round, loss of fatty hilum
  • Enhancement: Necrosis suggests infection or treated lymphoma

Common Pathologies on CTAP​

Acute Appendicitis​

  • Appendix >6 mm diameter
  • Periappendiceal fat stranding
  • Appendicolith present in ~25%
  • Abscess or perforation in advanced cases

Diverticulitis​

  • Colonic diverticula
  • Pericolonic fat stranding
  • Wall thickening of affected segment
  • Complications: Abscess, fistula, perforation

Bowel Obstruction​

  • Dilated proximal bowel with decompressed distal bowel
  • Transition point identification
  • Closed-loop obstruction: C-shaped or U-shaped loop, whirl sign

Pancreatitis (Balthazar Grade)​

  • Grade A: Normal pancreas
  • Grade B: Pancreatic enlargement only
  • Grade C: Peripancreatic fat changes
  • Grade D: Single fluid collection
  • Grade E: Multiple fluid collections or gas in pancreas

CT of the abdomen and pelvis remains the most comprehensive single examination for acute abdominal pathology, and a systematic approach ensures accurate diagnosis and appropriate clinical management.
