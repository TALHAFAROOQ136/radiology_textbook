Chapter 28: Genitourinary Imaging

Learning Objectives​

  • Describe the imaging evaluation of urinary calculi and urolithiasis
  • Recognize hydronephrosis and urinary obstruction on different modalities
  • Classify renal masses using the Bosniak classification
  • Describe bladder imaging indications and findings
  • Explain CT urography technique and applications

28.1 Kidney Stones and Urolithiasis​

Epidemiology​

  • Lifetime prevalence ~10-15% in Western populations
  • Males > females (2:1)
  • Recurrence rate: 50% within 10 years

Types of Kidney Stones​

TypeFrequencyAppearance on CTAppearance on X-ray
Calcium oxalate75-80%High density (400-600 HU)Radiopaque
Struvite (infection stones)10%High densityRadiopaque; "staghorn"
Uric acid5-8%Low density (200-400 HU)Radiolucent (invisible)
Cystine1-2%Moderate densityFaintly radiopaque
Pure calcium phosphate5%Very high densityRadiopaque

CT KUB (Non-Contrast CT of Urinary Tract)​

Gold standard for urolithiasis — sensitivity ~98%, specificity ~97%

Technique:

  • Non-contrast CT
  • Thin slices (1–3 mm)
  • Entire urinary tract from kidneys to bladder

Findings on CT:

  • Ureteric stone: bright white density within ureter
  • Soft tissue rim sign: halo of soft tissue surrounding stone (ureter wall)
  • Periureteric stranding: fat stranding around ureter at stone level (indicates obstruction/edema)
  • Hydronephrosis: dilated renal pelvis and calyces
  • Perinephric stranding: fat stranding around kidney (indicates obstructive uropathy)

Reporting elements for ureteric stones:

  • Location (upper/mid/lower ureter)
  • Size (HU attenuation and diameter)
  • Degree of hydronephrosis
  • Signs of infection/forniceal rupture

Plain Radiography (KUB)​

  • Detects only radiopaque stones (calcium stones) — approximately 90% of stones are radiopaque
  • Useful for follow-up of known calcium stones
  • Limited by: overlapping bowel gas, small stone size, uric acid stones invisible

Ultrasound in Urolithiasis​

  • Advantages: No radiation; assesses hydronephrosis; suitable in pregnancy and children
  • Limitations: Cannot reliably detect ureteric stones; misses small stones; gas interference
  • Main use: Renal calculi, hydronephrosis, monitoring in pregnancy
  • Ureteric jet on Doppler: absence suggests obstruction

Intravenous Urogram (IVU) — Historical​

  • Now largely replaced by CT urography and non-contrast CT
  • Still used in some settings
  • Iodinated contrast IV → images at 5, 15, 30 min → contrast-filled urinary tract visualized

Management by Stone Size​

Stone SizeExpected PassageManagement
<4 mm80% pass spontaneouslyMedical expulsive therapy
4–6 mm50-60% passConservative management
6–10 mm<50% passConsider intervention
>10 mmUnlikely to passESWL or ureteroscopy
>20 mm (staghorn)SurgeryPercutaneous nephrolithotomy

28.2 Hydronephrosis and Urinary Obstruction​

Definition​

Hydronephrosis = dilation of the renal pelvis and calyces due to urinary obstruction.

Grading (Society of Fetal Urology — SFU)​

GradeDescription
Grade 1Mild: renal pelvis visible only
Grade 2Moderate: few calyces visible
Grade 3Moderate-severe: all calyces visible
Grade 4Severe: parenchymal thinning begins

Causes of Hydronephrosis​

Intrinsic obstruction:

  • Ureteric stone (most common)
  • Stricture (TB, post-radiation, surgery)
  • Transitional cell carcinoma (TCC) of ureter

Extrinsic compression:

  • Retroperitoneal fibrosis
  • Pelvic malignancy (cervical, prostate, bladder cancer)
  • Gravid uterus (physiological in pregnancy)
  • Lymph node metastases

Functional/at junction:

  • Ureteropelvic junction (UPJ) obstruction
  • Vesicoureteric junction (VUJ) obstruction

Ultrasound of Hydronephrosis​

  • Dilated anechoic renal pelvis and calyces
  • Quantify degree of parenchymal thinning
  • Cannot usually identify the cause (ureteric stone not seen reliably)
  • Assess resistive index (RI) on Doppler: RI >0.70 suggests obstruction

CT Urography​

  • Three-phase study: unenhanced + nephrographic phase + excretory phase (delayed)
  • Shows entire urinary tract opacified
  • Identifies site and cause of obstruction
  • Detection of urothelial tumors, strictures, calculi

Diuresis Renography (MAG3 Nuclear Scan)​

  • Assesses differential renal function
  • Detects obstruction (slow drainage even with diuretic stimulus)
  • Used for UPJ obstruction assessment and post-treatment follow-up

28.3 Renal Masses — Bosniak Classification​

Simple Renal Cysts​

  • Common incidental finding
  • Classic US: anechoic, thin wall, posterior acoustic enhancement, no internal structure
  • Classic CT: water density (0–20 HU), smooth thin wall, no enhancement
  • Bosniak I: benign; no follow-up

Bosniak Classification​

System to classify renal cystic masses based on CT features:

CategoryDescriptionMalignancy RiskManagement
ISimple cyst: thin wall, water density, no enhancementNegligibleNo follow-up
IIMinimally complex: hairline septa, minimal calcification, high-density cysts <3 cm<5%No follow-up
IIFMore complex: multiple septa, thick calcification, high-density cysts ≥3 cm, no enhancing component~20%Follow-up CT at 6 months, then annually
IIIIndeterminate: thick irregular septa with measurable enhancement~50%Surgical excision or ablation
IVClearly malignant: solid enhancing components>90%Surgical excision

Enhancement = increase in HU by ≥15 after IV contrast

Solid Renal Masses​

Renal Cell Carcinoma (RCC):

  • Most common malignant renal tumor in adults
  • Types: Clear cell (most common, most vascular), papillary, chromophobe
  • CT: Solid, enhancing renal mass; clear cell = very avidly enhancing
  • May extend into renal vein and IVC (venous tumor thrombus)
  • Paraneoplastic syndromes: polycythemia, hypercalcemia, Stauffer syndrome

Angiomyolipoma (AML):

  • Benign; fat-containing renal mass
  • CT: Macroscopic fat (-10 to -30 HU) within renal mass = diagnostic
  • Association with tuberous sclerosis (multiple bilateral AML)
  • Risk of spontaneous hemorrhage (Wunderlich syndrome) if >4 cm

Oncocytoma:

  • Benign
  • CT: May show central stellate scar; spoke-wheel angiographic pattern
  • Cannot reliably differentiate from chromophobe RCC → surgery required

Transitional Cell Carcinoma (TCC/Urothelial Carcinoma):

  • Arises from collecting system (renal pelvis, ureter, bladder)
  • CT: Filling defect or wall thickening in renal pelvis on CT urography
  • Associated with smoking, occupational exposure, analgesic nephropathy

28.4 Bladder Imaging​

Ultrasound of the Bladder​

  • Non-invasive, no radiation
  • Assessment of bladder wall and volume
  • Detects large masses, calculi, diverticula
  • Post-void residual measurement (normal <50 mL)

CT of the Bladder​

CT Urography (excretory phase):

  • Best CT technique for bladder evaluation
  • Bladder fully distended with contrast
  • Detects TCC as filling defects within bladder lumen

MRI Pelvis (mpMRI):

  • Gold standard for bladder cancer local staging
  • Assesses depth of muscle invasion (T staging)
  • T2-weighted images: normal muscle is dark; tumor is intermediate signal
  • DWI (diffusion-weighted imaging) highlights tumor

MRI Bladder Staging​

StageDescription
T1Lamina propria only
T2aSuperficial muscle
T2bDeep muscle
T3aMicroscopic perivesical fat
T3bMacroscopic perivesical fat
T4Adjacent organs/pelvic wall

Cystography / CT Cystography​

  • Retrograde filling of bladder with contrast
  • Assessment for bladder rupture (trauma)
  • Detects fistulae

28.5 CT Urography​

Indications​

  • Hematuria (investigation for urothelial malignancy)
  • Recurrent UTIs
  • Follow-up of urothelial malignancy
  • Comprehensive evaluation of entire urinary tract

Technique​

Three phases:

  1. Unenhanced phase: Detects calculi (dense); baseline density of lesions
  2. Nephrographic phase (90 seconds): Renal parenchyma uniformly enhanced; best for solid renal masses
  3. Excretory/urographic phase (7-10 minutes): Collecting system and ureters opacified with contrast; urothelial assessment

Split-bolus technique: Reduces radiation by combining phases

CT Urography Interpretation​

  • Kidneys: Mass lesions, cortical thickness, enhancement
  • Urinary tract: Filling defects, strictures, dilatation
  • Bladder: Filling defects, wall thickening
  • Surrounding structures: Lymph nodes, adjacent organs

Key Points Summary​

  1. Non-contrast CT is the gold standard for urolithiasis (98% sensitivity); uric acid stones are invisible on plain radiography but visible on CT

  2. Bosniak classification stratifies renal cystic masses: Bosniak I–II = benign; IIF = follow-up; III = surgical excision considered; IV = malignant

  3. Macroscopic fat on CT within a renal mass = angiomyolipoma (benign); negative HU values (-10 to -30)

  4. RCC — avid contrast enhancement (especially clear cell type); check for venous tumor thrombus extending into IVC

  5. CT urography (3-phase) is the primary investigation for hematuria and urothelial malignancy evaluation

  6. Hydronephrosis — graded I-IV; ultrasound is first line; CT urography for cause identification
