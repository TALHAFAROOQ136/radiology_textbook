Acute Appendicitis

Acute appendicitis is the most common abdominal surgical emergency. Imaging plays a crucial role in diagnosis, particularly in atypical presentations and to reduce negative appendicectomy rates.

Epidemiology and Pathophysiology​

  • Lifetime risk: 7-8% (slightly higher in males)
  • Peak incidence: 10-30 years
  • Pathophysiology: Luminal obstruction (fecalith, lymphoid hyperplasia) → bacterial overgrowth → distension → ischemia → perforation
  • Perforation risk: ~20-30% if untreated; higher in very young and elderly

Clinical Presentation​

  • Classic: Periumbilical pain migrating to right iliac fossa, fever, nausea
  • Atypical: Retrocecal appendix (30-45%) — flank pain; pelvic appendix — dysuria, diarrhea
  • Alvarado Score: Combines symptoms, signs, and WBC for clinical probability

Imaging Strategy​

Algorithm​

  1. Children and pregnant women: Ultrasound first → MRI if inconclusive
  2. Adults: CT abdomen/pelvis (oral ± IV contrast)
  3. Low radiation concern: MRI (limited availability)

Ultrasound​

Technique​

  • Graded compression: Gradually displace overlying bowel gas
  • Linear high-frequency probe (7-12 MHz)
  • Identify appendix as blind-ending tubular structure in right iliac fossa

Normal Appendix​

  • Diameter <6 mm
  • Compressible
  • No periappendiceal changes

Appendicitis Findings​

  • Diameter >6 mm (measured outer wall to outer wall)
  • Non-compressibility: Rigid, tender under probe
  • Wall thickening: Echogenic submucosa, loss of wall layers
  • Appendicolith: Echogenic focus with posterior shadowing
  • Periappendiceal hyperechogenicity: Inflamed fat
  • Free fluid: Adjacent to appendix

Limitations​

  • Failed visualization: Overweight patients, retrocecal position, bowel gas
  • Sensitivity: 75-90%; Specificity: 85-95%
  • Operator-dependent

CT Scan​

Gold Standard for Diagnosis​

  • Sensitivity: 94-98%; Specificity: 95-98%
  • Protocol: IV contrast portal venous phase; oral contrast optional

CT Findings​

Primary Signs​

  • Appendix diameter >6 mm
  • Appendiceal wall thickening with enhancement
  • Periappendiceal fat stranding: Haziness/streakiness surrounding appendix
  • Appendicolith: Dense calcification in appendix (present in 25-35%)
  • Free fluid: Small amount adjacent to appendix

Secondary Signs​

  • Cecal wall thickening: "Arrowhead sign" — thickening at appendiceal orifice
  • Terminal ileal thickening: Adjacent bowel reaction
  • Lymphadenopathy: Right iliac fossa nodes

Complicated Appendicitis​

Perforation​

  • Loss of appendiceal wall integrity
  • Free air (pneumoperitoneum) — may be localized
  • Extraluminal appendicolith
  • Pericecal abscess or phlegmon
  • Diffuse peritonitis in advanced cases

Appendiceal Abscess​

  • Rim-enhancing fluid collection around perforated appendix
  • May contain gas
  • Appendix may be difficult to identify

Appendiceal Phlegmon​

  • Inflammatory mass without discrete abscess
  • No rim-enhancing collection
  • Initial non-operative management possible

MRI​

When to Use​

  • Pregnant women: First choice after inconclusive US
  • Children: Avoid radiation
  • Young women: Differentiate gynecological causes

MRI Findings​

  • Appendix >7 mm diameter
  • High T2 signal (wall edema)
  • Periappendiceal fat stranding (T2 hyperintense)
  • Appendicolith: Signal void on all sequences
  • No radiation: Key advantage

Protocol​

  • T2-weighted sequences: HASTE, TRUFI
  • DWI: Restricted diffusion in inflamed appendix
  • Fat-suppressed T2: Periappendiceal edema

Differential Diagnosis​

ConditionDistinguishing Features
Ovarian torsionEnlarged ovary, absent Doppler flow, no appendiceal dilation
Tubo-ovarian abscessAdnexal origin, sexually active female, fever
Mesenteric lymphadenitisMultiple enlarged lymph nodes, normal appendix
Crohn's diseaseTerminal ileal thickening, skip lesions, history
Meckel's diverticulitisMore medial, near terminal ileum
Cecal carcinomaMass lesion, older patient
Right ovarian cystCystic structure with Doppler flow
Psoas abscessRetroperitoneal, psoas muscle involvement

Scoring Systems​

Appendicitis Inflammatory Response (AIR) Score​

Combines CRP, WBC, neutrophils, body temperature, rebound tenderness, and guard with imaging to guide management.

Pediatric Appendicitis Score (PAS)​

Validated for children; scores >6 suggest imaging or surgical consultation.

Reporting Template​

A complete radiological report should include:

  1. Appendix visibility and size
  2. Periappendiceal changes
  3. Appendicolith presence
  4. Complications (perforation, abscess, phlegmon)
  5. Alternative diagnoses if appendix normal

Management Implications​

Imaging FindingManagement
Normal appendixDischarge if clinical probability low; admit if high
Uncomplicated appendicitisAppendicectomy (laparoscopic)
Appendiceal phlegmonAntibiotics ± interval appendicectomy
Appendiceal abscess <3 cmAntibiotics alone
Appendiceal abscess >3 cmCT-guided drainage + antibiotics
Free perforation/peritonitisEmergency surgery

Imaging is central to modern appendicitis management, reducing negative appendicectomy rates while ensuring timely surgical intervention in confirmed cases.
