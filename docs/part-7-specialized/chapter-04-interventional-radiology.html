Chapter 51: Interventional Radiology

Learning Objectives​

  • Describe common vascular interventional procedures
  • Explain image-guided biopsy and drainage principles
  • Recognize indications and contraindications for IR procedures
  • Describe basic catheter angiography principles
  • Understand embolization and ablation techniques

51.1 Principles of Interventional Radiology​

Image Guidance Modalities​

ModalityBest ForConsiderations
FluoroscopyVascular procedures; real-time guidanceRadiation; iodinated contrast
UltrasoundSuperficial biopsy/drainage; vascular accessNo radiation; real-time; limited depth
CTDeep lesions; lung biopsy; drainageExcellent anatomic detail; radiation
MRISoft tissue discrimination; prostateLimited by equipment; slower

Pre-procedure Assessment​

Coagulation:

  • INR: Typically <1.5 for major procedures
  • Platelets: Typically >50,000/μL for most procedures
  • Anticoagulation management: Hold warfarin 5 days, bridge if needed

Renal function:

  • eGFR for contrast-enhanced procedures
  • Hydration protocol for borderline renal function

Patient preparation:

  • Informed consent
  • IV access
  • NPO status for sedated procedures
  • Relevant imaging review

51.2 Vascular Access​

Central Venous Access​

Types of central venous catheters:

TypeSiteUse
PICC (Peripherally Inserted Central Catheter)Basilic/cephalic v. → SVCLong-term IV therapy
Tunneled catheter (Hickman)Internal jugular/subclavian → SVCChemotherapy; long-term
Port (implanted)Subcutaneous; tip in SVCLong-term; less infection risk
Dialysis catheterInternal jugularHemodialysis

Tip position: Distal SVC / cavoatrial junction (confirmed on CXR)

Complications:

  • Pneumothorax (subclavian approach higher risk)
  • Hemothorax, arterial puncture
  • Catheter malposition
  • Infection, venous thrombosis

Arterial Access​

Common sites:

  • Common femoral artery: Most common; compressible
  • Radial artery: Preferred for coronary access; lower bleeding
  • Brachial, axillary: When femoral not available

Closure devices: Angioseal, Perclose; vs. manual compression

51.3 Angiography and Vascular Intervention​

Diagnostic Angiography​

Principles:

  • Seldinger technique: Needle → wire → catheter
  • Contrast injection under fluoroscopy
  • Digital subtraction angiography (DSA): Subtracts background; shows vessels only

Common studies:

  • Peripheral angiography (PAD)
  • Mesenteric angiography (GI bleeding, ischemia)
  • Renal angiography (renovascular hypertension)
  • Cerebral angiography (AVM, aneurysm)

Embolization​

Principle: Occlude blood flow to:

  • Stop hemorrhage
  • Devascularize tumor (preoperative or palliative)
  • Treat AVM, fistula

Embolization agents:

AgentPropertiesUse
Coils (platinum)Permanent; large vessel occlusionPseudoaneurysm, hemorrhage
Particles (PVA, microspheres)Permanent; variable sizeTumor embolization, UAE
GelfoamTemporary (resorbable)GI hemorrhage, epistaxis
Glue (Histoacryl/NBCA)Permanent; liquidAVM, variceal
Onyx (EVOH)Permanent; liquid; non-adhesiveAVM

Common embolization procedures:

  • Uterine Fibroid Embolization (UFE): UAE with microspheres; treats menorrhagia
  • Prostate Artery Embolization (PAE): For BPH
  • Splenic embolization: Hypersplenism; trauma (splenic artery pseudoaneurysm)
  • Gastrointestinal hemorrhage: SMA/IMA branches; requires active bleeding
  • Hepatic embolization: HCC palliation; TACE (transarterial chemoembolization)

Angioplasty and Stenting​

Percutaneous Transluminal Angioplasty (PTA):

  • Balloon dilation of stenosed vessel
  • Indications: Peripheral arterial disease, renal artery stenosis, SMA stenosis

Stenting:

  • Metal stent deployed after angioplasty (for suboptimal result or recoil)
  • Covered stents: For aortic aneurysm exclusion (EVAR/TEVAR)

EVAR (Endovascular Aneurysm Repair):

  • Endograft excludes AAA from circulation
  • Less invasive than open surgery
  • Requires annual surveillance CT for endoleak

51.4 Image-Guided Biopsy​

Principles​

Tissue confirmation required for:

  • Suspected malignancy (before treatment)
  • Uncertain diagnosis (e.g., solitary pulmonary nodule)
  • Staging (lymph node sampling)

Approach selection:

  • Shortest, safest path to lesion
  • Avoid major vessels, bowel, pleura (where possible)
  • Coaxial technique: Outer guide needle + inner biopsy needle

CT-Guided Lung Biopsy​

Technique:

  • Patient prone or supine depending on lesion location
  • CT fluoroscopy or intermittent CT guidance
  • Core needle biopsy (18-gauge) — preferred; FNA where core not possible

Complications:

  • Pneumothorax: 15-20% (most clinically significant require chest drain in ~5%)
  • Hemorrhage: Usually self-limiting pulmonary hemorrhage
  • Air embolism: Rare but serious

Percutaneous Liver Biopsy​

Image-guided (ultrasound/CT):

  • Focal lesion biopsy: CT or US guided core biopsy
  • Diffuse liver disease: US-guided or blind liver biopsy

Complications:

  • Bleeding (subcapsular hematoma, hemobilia)
  • Bile duct injury (avoid targeting subcapsular lesions)

Musculoskeletal Biopsy​

Bone/soft tissue biopsy:

  • Plan approach to avoid contaminating tissue planes needed for surgery
  • Core biopsy preferred (18-20 gauge)
  • Involves surgical and orthopedic planning

51.5 Drainage Procedures​

Abscess Drainage​

Percutaneous abscess drainage (PAD):

  • Indications: Accessible collection; patient unsuitable for surgery
  • Technique: Image-guided needle → wire → drain placement
  • Catheter left in for continuous drainage; flushed regularly
  • Success rate: 80-90% for simple abscesses

Collections amenable to percutaneous drainage:

  • Intra-abdominal abscesses (diverticular, postoperative)
  • Liver abscess (pyogenic; amebic rarely needs drainage)
  • Perirenal/renal abscess
  • Pelvic abscess

Biliary Drainage​

Percutaneous Transhepatic Cholangiography (PTC):

  • Needle through liver into bile duct under US/fluoroscopy
  • Contrast injection → cholangiogram
  • Drainage catheter placement if obstruction

Indications:

  • Biliary obstruction not amenable to ERCP
  • Hilar cholangiocarcinoma
  • Post-surgical biliary complications

External vs. internal-external drainage:

  • External: Drainage to bag only
  • Internal-external: Spans obstruction; bile enters duodenum

Nephrostomy​

Percutaneous Nephrostomy (PCN):

  • Direct puncture of renal collecting system under US guidance
  • Indications: Obstructive uropathy; urinary diversion; pyonephrosis

Technique:

  • Posterior caliceal approach (avoids major vessels)
  • 18-gauge needle → wire → nephrostomy tube

51.6 Ablation​

Principles​

Thermal ablation destroys tumor in situ:

  • Heat (Radiofrequency Ablation/RFA; Microwave Ablation/MWA)
  • Cold (Cryoablation)
  • Irreversible electroporation (IRE — non-thermal)

Radiofrequency Ablation (RFA)​

Mechanism: Alternating current → ionic friction → heat → coagulation necrosis (60-100°C)

Indications:

  • Hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC): Lesions <3 cm; bridging to transplant
  • Colorectal liver metastases: Unresectable
  • Renal cell carcinoma: Small tumors (<4 cm); patient not surgical candidate
  • Lung nodules: Early-stage NSCLC; colorectal mets
  • Bone: Osteoid osteoma (near-100% cure rate); palliation of metastases

Microwave Ablation (MWA)​

Advantages over RFA:

  • Faster ablation times
  • Larger ablation zones
  • Less susceptible to "heat sink" effect from large vessels

Cryoablation​

Mechanism: Argon gas → Joule-Thomson effect → freezing → ice ball formation → cell death at <-40°C

Advantages:

  • Visible ice ball on CT/US for real-time monitoring
  • Less pain
  • Used for kidney, prostate, soft tissue

Key Points Summary​

  1. Seldinger technique: Needle → wire → catheter — basis for all vascular access

  2. EVAR: Endovascular AAA repair; less invasive; requires follow-up CT surveillance for endoleak

  3. UFE (Uterine Fibroid Embolization): Microspheres via uterine arteries; treats menorrhagia without surgery

  4. Pneumothorax is the most common complication of CT-guided lung biopsy (~15-20%)

  5. Percutaneous abscess drainage: Image-guided catheter drainage; 80-90% success rate

  6. RFA/MWA: Thermal ablation for HCC, renal tumors, osteoid osteoma; curative intent for small lesions
