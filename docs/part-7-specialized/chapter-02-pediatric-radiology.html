Chapter 49: Pediatric Radiology Essentials

Learning Objectives​

  • Apply ALARA principles in pediatric imaging
  • Recognize normal pediatric skeletal variants vs. pathology
  • Identify common pediatric chest and abdominal emergencies
  • Describe neonatal imaging findings
  • Recognize non-accidental injury (NAI) patterns

49.1 Principles of Pediatric Imaging​

ALARA and Radiation Protection​

"Image Gently" campaign principles:

  • Use the lowest dose consistent with diagnostic image quality
  • Use ultrasound and MRI preferentially where appropriate
  • Avoid CT when alternatives are adequate
  • Shield gonads and thyroid when possible

Dose reduction strategies:

  • Decrease kVp and mAs compared to adults
  • Reduce scan length to region of interest
  • Single-phase CT where possible
  • Avoid repeat imaging

Age-Appropriate Modality Selection​

Age/ConditionPreferred Modality
Neonatal bowel obstructionX-ray first, then contrast study
Intussusception diagnosisUltrasound
Intussusception reductionFluoroscopic or ultrasound-guided enema
Hip dysplasia (<4 months)Ultrasound (ossification incomplete)
Hip dysplasia (>4 months)X-ray (ossification present)
Brain injury/malformationMRI
Acute appendicitisUltrasound first; CT if inconclusive
Pyloric stenosisUltrasound

49.2 Pediatric Chest​

Normal Neonatal Chest​

Thymus:

  • Normal in neonates/infants — large, smooth, anterior mediastinum
  • "Sail sign": Thymic shadow extending laterally like a sail
  • "Notch sign": Wave notch at 4th rib due to vessels
  • Should NOT be confused with mediastinal mass
  • Thymus involutes with stress/illness (thymic shrinkage sign)

Neonatal respiratory distress:

ConditionKey Features
Respiratory Distress Syndrome (RDS/HMD)Diffuse ground glass; air bronchograms; premature
Transient Tachypnea of Newborn (TTN)Perihilar streaking; pleural fluid; clears 24-48h
Meconium Aspiration Syndrome (MAS)Hyperinflation; coarse bilateral opacity; pneumothorax risk
Congenital PneumoniaUnilateral or bilateral consolidation; group B Strep

Congenital Lung Malformations​

Congenital Pulmonary Airway Malformation (CPAM — formerly CCAM):

  • Cystic or solid pulmonary mass
  • Types 1-3 based on cyst size
  • Risk of infection; surgical resection often required
  • Prenatal US: may detect as echogenic lung mass

Bronchopulmonary Sequestration:

  • Non-functioning lung tissue receiving systemic arterial supply
  • No communication with tracheobronchial tree
  • Intralobar (within pleura) vs. extralobar (own pleura)
  • CXR: posterior basal mass (usually left lower lobe)
  • CT: solid/cystic mass; aberrant feeding artery from aorta

Congenital Diaphragmatic Hernia (CDH):

  • Bochdalek hernia: Posterolateral; left side (90%)
  • Loops of bowel, stomach in chest; mediastinal shift
  • Associated pulmonary hypoplasia
  • Diagnosis: Prenatal US or postnatal CXR

49.3 Pediatric Abdomen​

Neonatal Bowel Obstruction​

High vs. Low Obstruction:

  • High: Few dilated loops; bilious vomiting early
  • Low: Many dilated loops; delayed presentation
ConditionFeatures
Duodenal atresia"Double bubble" sign; Trisomy 21 association
Jejunal/Ileal atresiaMultiple dilated loops; absent rectal gas
Hirschsprung diseaseTransition zone; delayed rectal gas; contrast enema
Meconium ileusCF association; "soap bubble" appearance
Malrotation/VolvulusEMERGENCY; dilated duodenum; "whirlpool" on US
Necrotizing Enterocolitis (NEC)Pneumatosis intestinalis; portal gas; free air

Hypertrophic Pyloric Stenosis (HPS)​

Clinical: First-born males; 3-8 weeks; projectile non-bilious vomiting

Ultrasound (diagnostic test of choice):

  • Pyloric muscle thickness: >4 mm (abnormal)
  • Pyloric channel length: >17 mm (abnormal)
  • "Target sign": Thickened pyloric muscle around echogenic mucosa
  • No passage of fluid through pylorus

Intussusception​

Most common cause of bowel obstruction in 6 months - 3 years

Clinical: Intermittent colicky pain; "currant jelly" stool; palpable mass

Ultrasound findings:

  • "Target sign" or "donut sign" on transverse view (intussusceptum within intussuscipiens)
  • "Pseudokidney" sign on longitudinal view

Reduction:

  • Fluoroscopic air/contrast enema (success rate 80-90%)
  • Ultrasound-guided saline enema
  • Contraindications to reduction: Perforation, peritonitis

Appendicitis in Children​

Ultrasound: First-line in children; avoid radiation

  • Non-compressible appendix >6 mm diameter
  • Periappendiceal fluid; hyperemia on Doppler
  • Appendicolith (echogenic with shadowing)
  • Limited by bowel gas and body habitus

CT: If ultrasound inconclusive or perforation suspected

  • Enhanced CT; lower-dose protocols in children

49.4 Pediatric Skeletal​

Normal Variants (Must Not Confuse with Pathology)​

CRITOE ossification order (elbow):

  • Capitellum → Radial head → Internal (medial) epicondyle → Trochlea → Olecranon → External (lateral) epicondyle
  • Appears approximately every 2 years starting age 1
  • Age 1, 3, 5, 7, 9, 11 years

Common normal variants:

  • Metaphyseal spurring: Normal variant in infants; can mimic corner fractures
  • Pseudosubluxation of C2 on C3: Normal in children <8 years (anterior displacement up to 3-4 mm)
  • Ossification variants of patella, sesamoids
  • "Nutrient foramina" — diagonal lucencies in cortex

Salter-Harris Fractures​

Epiphyseal (growth plate) fractures — classified by pattern:

| Type | Description | Memory |\n|------|-------------|--------|\n| I | Through physis only; widened physis | Slipped |\n| II | Through physis + metaphysis (most common) | Above |\n| III | Through physis + epiphysis (intra-articular) | Low |\n| IV | Through all three (metaphysis + physis + epiphysis) | Through |\n| V | Crush of physis (poor prognosis; growth arrest) | Rammed |

Mnemonic: SALTER (S=Slipped, A=Above, L=Low, T=Through, E=Erasure/crush, R=Rammed)

Non-Accidental Injury (NAI / Child Abuse)​

Highly specific skeletal injuries for NAI:

  • Classic metaphyseal lesions (CML): "Corner fractures" at metaphyseal margins; HIGHLY specific
  • Posterior rib fractures: Most specific; caused by squeezing; at costovertebral junctions
  • Bilateral fractures or multiple fractures of different ages
  • Unusual locations: Scapula, sternum, spinous processes

Other NAI patterns:

  • Long bone spiral fractures in non-ambulatory children
  • Subdural hematoma (bilateral, of different ages)
  • Retinal hemorrhages on ophthalmological exam
  • "Shaken baby" pattern: Subdural + retinal hemorrhage without external trauma

Skeletal survey in suspected NAI:

  • AP and lateral skull, thorax, abdomen, pelvis, all long bones
  • Babygram (single film of whole child) is INADEQUATE

49.5 Pediatric Brain​

Neonatal Brain Imaging​

Cranial Ultrasound:

  • Used in premature neonates through anterior fontanelle
  • Screens for intraventricular hemorrhage (IVH), periventricular leukomalacia (PVL)
  • Grading of IVH (Papile system):
    • Grade I: Germinal matrix only
    • Grade II: IVH without hydrocephalus
    • Grade III: IVH + hydrocephalus
    • Grade IV: Parenchymal hemorrhage (periventricular infarction)

Periventricular Leukomalacia (PVL):

  • Premature infant white matter injury (ischemia/hypoxia)
  • Ultrasound: Periventricular echogenicity → cystic change
  • MRI: T2 bright periventricular white matter; reduced white matter volume
  • Associated with cerebral palsy

Pediatric Brain Tumors​

Most common location: Posterior fossa (infratentorial) in children

TumorLocationKey Features
Pilocytic Astrocytoma (most common)CerebellumCystic with enhancing mural nodule
MedulloblastomaCerebellar vermisMidline; fills 4th ventricle; hyperdense CT
Ependymoma4th ventricle"Plastic"; squeezes through foramina
Brainstem GliomaPonsDiffuse pontine glioma (DIPG); poor prognosis

Supratentorial in children:

  • Craniopharyngioma: Sellar/suprasellar; "motor oil" cyst; calcification; adamantinomatous type in children
  • Optic pathway glioma: NF1 association

Key Points Summary​

  1. Image Gently: Use ultrasound/MRI preferentially; reduce CT dose in children; shield appropriately

  2. Neonatal chest: Thymus is normal large — not a mass; RDS vs. TTN vs. MAS on CXR

  3. Double bubble: Duodenal atresia; association with Trisomy 21

  4. Pyloric stenosis: Ultrasound diagnostic; muscle wall >4 mm; channel >17 mm

  5. NAI: Posterior rib fractures + classic metaphyseal lesions most specific; bilateral SDH in young infant

  6. Pediatric brain tumors: Posterior fossa in children — pilocytic astrocytoma (cystic + nodule), medulloblastoma (midline solid)
