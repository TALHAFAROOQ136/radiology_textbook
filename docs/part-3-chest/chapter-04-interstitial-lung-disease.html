Chapter 17: Interstitial Lung Disease

Learning Objectives​

  • Recognize interstitial patterns on chest X-ray: reticular, nodular, reticulonodular
  • Describe the radiographic and HRCT features of idiopathic pulmonary fibrosis
  • Identify sarcoidosis imaging patterns including lymphadenopathy staging
  • Recognize hypersensitivity pneumonitis patterns
  • Explain the role of HRCT in ILD diagnosis

17.1 Interstitial Patterns​

What Is the Interstitium?​

The pulmonary interstitium is the structural framework of the lung that includes:

  • The alveolar walls (septa)
  • Peribronchovascular sheaths
  • Interlobular septa
  • Subpleural tissue

When the interstitium is abnormal, it shows characteristic patterns on imaging.

Reticular Pattern​

Definition: Network of fine lines intersecting to form a mesh

Mechanism: Thickened interlobular septa or intralobular fibrosis

Subtypes:

  • Fine reticulation: Early or mild ILD; fine mesh
  • Coarse reticulation: Advanced fibrosis; larger mesh
  • Honeycombing: End-stage fibrosis; air-containing cysts in clusters (like a honeycomb); strongly suggests UIP pattern (IPF)

Distribution:

  • Basal predominant: IPF, asbestosis
  • Upper lobe: sarcoidosis, HP (chronic), silicosis
  • Diffuse: connective tissue disease ILD

Nodular Pattern​

Definition: Discrete nodules of similar size distributed in the lung

Types by distribution:

DistributionCharacteristicsCommon Causes
CentrilobularClustered; near center of secondary lobule; spares pleuraHP, respiratory bronchiolitis
PerilymphaticAlong lymphatics (subpleural, peribronchovascular, septal)Sarcoidosis, silicosis
RandomNo pattern; uniformly distributedMiliary TB, hematogenous metastases

Reticulonodular Pattern​

Combination of reticular lines and nodules — seen in many ILDs including:

  • Sarcoidosis
  • Silicosis
  • Early IPF

Ground-Glass Opacity (GGO)​

  • Increased lung density that does NOT obscure vessels
  • Indicates partial filling of alveolar spaces or thickening of alveolar walls
  • Active (potentially reversible): inflammation, edema
  • Can indicate active alveolitis in ILD — more responsive to treatment
  • CT is essential for proper characterization

17.2 Idiopathic Pulmonary Fibrosis (IPF)​

Definition​

IPF is a chronic, progressive fibrosing ILD of unknown cause with a Usual Interstitial Pneumonia (UIP) pattern on HRCT or histology.

Clinical Features​

  • Age >60; more common in men
  • Smokers > non-smokers
  • Progressive dyspnea and non-productive cough
  • Bibasal Velcro-like crackles on auscultation
  • Clubbing (30-40%)
  • Poor prognosis: median survival 3-5 years from diagnosis

Chest X-Ray Features​

  • Bilateral basal reticular opacities
  • Peripheral distribution
  • Reduced lung volumes (fibrosis = restrictive disease)
  • Honeycombing visible in advanced cases
  • May appear normal in early disease

HRCT Features — UIP Pattern​

HRCT is the gold standard for IPF diagnosis (often avoids surgical lung biopsy):

Definite UIP pattern (diagnostic of IPF in right clinical context):

  • Honeycombing — subpleural, basal-dominant; clusters of air-containing cysts
  • Basal predominant fibrosis
  • Peripheral predominant fibrosis (subpleural)
  • Traction bronchiectasis/bronchiolectasis within fibrosis
  • Minimal or no GGO

Features suggesting alternative diagnosis (not IPF):

  • Upper or peribronchovascular predominance
  • Extensive GGO without reticulation
  • Nodules (bilateral, upper lobe)
  • Air trapping

ATS/ERS Diagnostic Criteria Summary​

Diagnosis of IPF requires:

  1. Exclusion of other known causes of ILD
  2. UIP pattern on HRCT
  3. If HRCT not definitive: surgical lung biopsy showing UIP histology

17.3 Sarcoidosis​

Overview​

Sarcoidosis is a multisystem granulomatous disease of unknown etiology. The lungs and intrathoracic lymph nodes are affected in >90% of patients.

Scadding Stages (Chest X-Ray Based)​

StageDescriptionSpontaneous Remission
Stage 0Normal chest X-ray—
Stage 1Bilateral hilar lymphadenopathy (BHL) only55-90%
Stage 2BHL + pulmonary infiltrates40-70%
Stage 3Pulmonary infiltrates only (no adenopathy)10-20%
Stage 4Advanced fibrosis<5%

Stage 1 is the most common presentation.

Chest X-Ray Features​

Bilateral hilar lymphadenopathy (BHL):

  • Symmetric, bilateral
  • "Potato nodes" — lobulated hilar masses
  • "Pawnbroker sign" — bilateral hilar enlargement with right paratracheal adenopathy (1-2-3 pattern on right: right paratracheal, right hilar, left hilar)
  • Usually bilateral (unilateral should prompt alternative diagnosis — TB, lymphoma)

Pulmonary infiltrates:

  • Reticulonodular pattern
  • Upper and middle lobe distribution
  • Perilymphatic distribution: subpleural, peribronchovascular, along fissures

HRCT Features​

  • Perilymphatic nodules (diagnostic when classic): bilateral, upper/middle lobe, subpleural, peribronchovascular, septal
  • "Galaxy sign": central mass of coalescing nodules with peripheral satellite nodules
  • Air-space consolidation (alveolar sarcoidosis)
  • Fibrosis: traction bronchiectasis, honeycombing in stage 4

Extra-Pulmonary Manifestations​

  • Skin: erythema nodosum (common, benign), lupus pernio
  • Eyes: uveitis (5-25%)
  • Cardiac: arrhythmias, heart block, sudden death
  • Neurological: cranial nerve palsies, meningitis
  • Liver, spleen: hepatosplenomegaly

17.4 Hypersensitivity Pneumonitis (HP)​

Definition​

HP (Extrinsic Allergic Alveolitis) is an immune-mediated lung disease caused by repeated inhalation of organic antigens in sensitized individuals.

Common Causative Antigens​

Antigen SourceDisease Name
Bird droppings (pigeon, budgerigar)Bird fancier's lung
Thermophilic bacteria in moldy hayFarmer's lung
Thermophilic bacteria in contaminated water/humidifiersHumidifier lung
Aspergillus fumigatusMalt worker's lung
Mycobacteria in water (hot tubs)Hot tub lung

Clinical Phases​

Acute HP (4-8 hours after exposure):

  • Fever, chills, myalgia, dyspnea
  • Resolves within days of antigen avoidance
  • Recurs on re-exposure

Subacute HP:

  • Gradually worsening dyspnea
  • Insidious onset

Chronic HP:

  • Progressive fibrosis
  • Often indistinguishable from IPF clinically
  • Antigen history crucial for diagnosis

Chest X-Ray​

  • Acute: Bilateral fine nodular or ground-glass opacities; lower/middle zone
  • Subacute: Reticulonodular pattern
  • Chronic: Fibrosis; may resemble IPF

HRCT Features​

Acute/Subacute HP:

  • Centrilobular nodules (ill-defined, GGO halo)
  • Ground-glass opacity
  • Air trapping (mosaic attenuation) on expiratory CT — bilateral, patchy
  • Upper/middle lobe predominance

Chronic HP:

  • Fibrosis: traction bronchiectasis, honeycombing
  • Subpleural distribution (but less exclusively basal than IPF)
  • Mixed with GGO and air trapping
  • Can mimic IPF or NSIP

"Head cheese" sign: Mixed areas of consolidation/GGO, normal lung, and air trapping — characteristic of HP

17.5 High-Resolution CT (HRCT) in ILD​

Why HRCT?​

HRCT provides exquisite detail of lung architecture that is not visible on chest X-ray.

Advantages over CXR:

  • Detects ILD when CXR is normal
  • Characterizes pattern (UIP, NSIP, HP, sarcoidosis)
  • Guides biopsy site selection
  • Monitors disease progression
  • Avoids surgical lung biopsy in many cases

HRCT Technique​

  • 1-mm slices (high resolution)
  • Reconstruction using high-frequency (bone) algorithm — maximizes spatial resolution
  • Prone images: helpful to distinguish true fibrosis from dependent atelectasis (positional GGO)
  • Expiratory images: detect air trapping (HP, constrictive bronchiolitis)
  • No contrast required

Key HRCT Patterns for Common ILDs​

PatternHRCT FeaturesLikely Diagnosis
UIPHoneycombing, basal-peripheral reticulation, traction bronchiectasisIPF
NSIPBilateral GGO, basal reticulation, subpleural sparingConnective tissue ILD, HP
Perilymphatic nodulesSubpleural, peribronchovascular, septal nodulesSarcoidosis
Centrilobular nodules + air trappingUpper/mid zone, mosaic attenuationHP
Crazy pavingGGO + septal thickeningAlveolar proteinosis, COVID-19

Secondary Pulmonary Lobule​

Understanding the secondary pulmonary lobule (smallest unit of lung structure bounded by interlobular septa):

  • Centrilobular: center of lobule (airways, arteries) → centrilobular nodules
  • Perilobular/Perilymphatic: edges of lobule (septa, subpleural, peribronchovascular) → sarcoidosis
  • Random: no relation to lobule structure → miliary disease

Key Points Summary​

  1. Reticular pattern = thickened interstitial lines; honeycombing = end-stage fibrosis (clusters of small air-containing cysts)

  2. IPF/UIP: bibasal, peripheral, subpleural honeycombing and reticulation; minimal GGO; traction bronchiectasis — poor prognosis

  3. Sarcoidosis: bilateral hilar lymphadenopathy (Stage 1); perilymphatic nodules; upper/middle lobe distribution — often self-limited

  4. HP: centrilobular nodules, GGO, and air trapping; upper/middle lobe predominance; antigen exposure history crucial

  5. HRCT: 1 mm slices, high-frequency algorithm; can diagnose IPF without biopsy; prone and expiratory images add diagnostic value

  6. Perilymphatic vs. centrilobular nodule distribution is the key differentiator between sarcoidosis and HP
