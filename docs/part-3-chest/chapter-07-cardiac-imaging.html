Chapter 20: Cardiac Imaging on Chest X-Ray

Learning Objectives​

  • Assess cardiac size using the cardiothoracic ratio
  • Identify chamber enlargement patterns on chest X-ray
  • Recognize radiographic features of heart failure
  • Describe pericardial effusion appearance
  • Differentiate cardiomegaly causes

20.1 Cardiac Size Assessment​

Cardiothoracic Ratio (CTR)​

Definition: Maximum transverse cardiac diameter divided by maximum internal thoracic diameter

Measurement:

  • Measured on PA (not AP) chest X-ray
  • Inner thoracic cage width at its widest point
  • Cardiac diameter = sum of maximum right cardiac border distance to midline + maximum left cardiac border distance to midline

Normal CTR: <0.5 (less than 50% of thoracic width)

Cardiomegaly: CTR ≥0.5

Limitations:

  • Only valid on PA erect CXR (not AP portable)
  • False enlargement: AP films, poor inspiration, pectus excavatum
  • CTR may be normal in acute conditions (acute myocardial infarction, constrictive pericarditis)
  • Echocardiography is gold standard for chamber assessment

Normal Cardiac Borders​

Right heart border (from top to bottom):

  1. Superior vena cava (SVC)
  2. Right atrium (lower right border)

Left heart border (from top to bottom):

  1. Aortic knob (aortic arch)
  2. Main pulmonary artery (MPA) segment
  3. Left atrial appendage (LAA)
  4. Left ventricle (dominant border)

20.2 Chamber Enlargement Patterns​

Left Ventricular Enlargement (LVE)​

Causes: Hypertension, aortic regurgitation, mitral regurgitation, dilated cardiomyopathy

CXR features:

  • Cardiac apex displaced downward and to the left
  • Elongated left heart border
  • CTR increased

CT/Echo: Dilated LV cavity; may have reduced ejection fraction

Left Atrial Enlargement (LAE)​

Causes: Mitral stenosis, mitral regurgitation, left ventricular failure

CXR features (classic signs):

  1. Double density sign: Left atrium visible through right heart border as a second curved line
  2. Splaying of carina: Widened carina angle (>70°) due to LA pushing up left mainstem bronchus
  3. Left atrial appendage: Straight or convex left heart border between pulmonary artery and LV
  4. Posterior displacement of esophagus on barium swallow (mid-esophageal indentation)
  5. Elevation of left mainstem bronchus (horizontal bronchus)

Right Ventricular Enlargement (RVE)​

Causes: Pulmonary hypertension, pulmonary stenosis, right heart failure

CXR features:

  • Cardiac apex displaced upward (boot-shaped heart — "coeur en sabot")
  • Right heart border displacement to the right
  • Filling in of retrosternal space on lateral view

Specific patterns:

  • "Boot-shaped heart" (Coeur en sabot): Classic in Tetralogy of Fallot — upturned apex + concave pulmonary artery segment

Right Atrial Enlargement (RAE)​

Causes: Tricuspid regurgitation, right heart failure, pulmonary hypertension

CXR features:

  • Right heart border prominence and displacement to the right

20.3 Heart Failure Imaging​

Pathophysiology of Radiographic Signs​

Heart failure → elevated left heart filling pressures → pulmonary venous hypertension → radiographic changes

Stages of pulmonary venous hypertension:

Stage 1: Cephalization (Redistribution)

  • Normal standing: lower lobe vessels larger than upper
  • Early failure: Upper lobe vessels equalize or become larger than lower lobe
  • "Cephalization of blood flow"
  • CXR: Upper lobe veins prominent; lower lobe relative oligemia

Stage 2: Interstitial Edema (PCWP 18-25 mmHg)

  • Fluid in pulmonary interstitium (septa, peribronchial areas)
  • Kerley B lines: Short horizontal lines perpendicular to the pleural surface at the lung bases (1-3 cm long); represent edematous interlobular septa
  • Kerley A lines: Long diagonal lines (4-10 cm) from hilum toward lung periphery; less common
  • Peribronchial cuffing (thickened bronchial walls seen end-on as "doughnut" sign)
  • Hilar haze (indistinct hilar margins)
  • Pleural effusions (usually bilateral but can be right-sided or left-sided)

Stage 3: Alveolar Edema (PCWP >25 mmHg)

  • Fluid floods alveoli
  • "Bat wing" or "butterfly" distribution: bilateral perihilar confluent opacities
  • Air bronchograms present
  • Can progress to complete "white out" of both lungs
  • Responds rapidly to treatment (resolves within hours-days of diuresis)

Key Radiographic Features of Heart Failure​

FeatureSignificance
Cardiomegaly (CTR ≥0.5)Chronic heart disease
CephalizationEarly pulmonary venous hypertension
Kerley B linesInterstitial edema
Bilateral pleural effusionsCommon in bilateral failure
Perihilar opacitiesAlveolar edema
"Bat wing" patternSevere acute pulmonary edema

Acute vs. Chronic Heart Failure on CXR​

FeatureAcuteChronic
Heart sizeMay be normalUsually enlarged
Pulmonary vesselsCongestedChronic changes
OnsetRapid opacificationGradual
Response to treatmentRapid clearingSlower

Note: In acute heart failure (e.g., acute MI with flash pulmonary edema), the heart may be normal size but lungs are flooded.

20.4 Pericardial Effusion​

Definition​

Accumulation of fluid within the pericardial sac.

Causes​

  • Viral pericarditis (most common)
  • Malignancy (metastatic, lymphoma — common in large effusions)
  • Uremia
  • Post-MI (Dressler's syndrome)
  • Hypothyroidism
  • Autoimmune disease (lupus, rheumatoid arthritis)
  • TB

Radiographic Features​

CXR:

  • Enlarged cardiac silhouette
  • "Water bottle" or "flask-shaped" heart — smooth enlargement of cardiac shadow
  • No evidence of pulmonary venous hypertension initially (distinguishes from cardiomegaly due to heart failure)
  • Normal hilar vessels (pulmonary venous congestion absent unless combined pathology)

Size on CXR:

  • Usually requires >250 mL to be visible on CXR
  • Massive effusions can hold 2 liters or more

Echocardiography: Gold standard for detection (can detect as little as 25-50 mL)

CT features:

  • Fluid density (<30 HU: transudate; >40 HU: exudate, hemopericardium, malignancy)
  • Circumferential or loculated
  • Pericardial thickening may indicate pericarditis

Cardiac Tamponade​

Definition: Pericardial effusion that compresses cardiac chambers → hemodynamic compromise

Clinical: Beck's triad — hypotension, elevated JVP, muffled heart sounds

Echocardiographic signs:

  • Right atrial collapse (systolic)
  • Right ventricular diastolic collapse
  • Plethoric IVC (no collapse with inspiration)
  • Respiratory variation in Doppler

CXR: Large heart; no specific CXR sign for tamponade → Echo is essential

20.5 Cardiomegaly Differential Diagnosis​

Common Causes of Enlarged Cardiac Silhouette​

True cardiac enlargement:

  • Dilated cardiomyopathy
  • Ischemic cardiomyopathy (post-MI)
  • Valvular heart disease (MR, AR, TR)
  • Hypertensive heart disease
  • Congenital heart disease

Pericardial effusion:

  • Flask-shaped enlargement
  • No pulmonary venous hypertension

Technical factors (false cardiomegaly):

  • AP projection (portable CXR) — cardiac magnification
  • Poor inspiration — cardiac shadow appears wider
  • Patient rotation — oblique view enlarges apparent cardiac width

Specific Cardiac Shapes​

AppearanceAssociated Condition
Flask/water bottle shapePericardial effusion
Boot shape (coeur en sabot)Tetralogy of Fallot (RV enlargement + small PA)
"Egg on side"Transposition of great arteries
"Snowman sign"Total anomalous pulmonary venous return (TAPVR)
Box-shaped heartEbstein's anomaly (massive RA)

Key Points Summary​

  1. CTR ≥0.5 on PA erect CXR = cardiomegaly (not valid on AP portable X-ray)

  2. Left atrial enlargement — double density, splayed carina, left atrial appendage convexity, mid-esophageal indentation on barium swallow

  3. Heart failure stages: Cephalization → Kerley B lines (interstitial edema) → Bat wing pattern (alveolar edema)

  4. Kerley B lines — short horizontal lines perpendicular to pleural surface at lung bases; edematous interlobular septa

  5. Pericardial effusion — flask-shaped enlargement; no pulmonary venous hypertension; echo is gold standard

  6. Acute MI may present with normal heart size but flooded lungs (cardiogenic pulmonary edema without cardiac enlargement)
