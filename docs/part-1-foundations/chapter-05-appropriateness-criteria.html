Chapter 5: Appropriateness Criteria and Evidence-Based Imaging

Learning Objectives​

By the end of this chapter, students should be able to:

  • Explain the purpose and structure of ACR Appropriateness Criteria
  • Apply evidence-based principles to select the appropriate imaging modality
  • Compare cost-effectiveness of different imaging approaches
  • Recognize imaging that exposes patients to unnecessary radiation
  • Use clinical decision support tools in imaging selection

5.1 ACR Appropriateness Criteria​

Background and Purpose​

The American College of Radiology (ACR) Appropriateness Criteria are evidence-based guidelines developed to help clinicians order the most appropriate imaging study for a given clinical condition. They were first developed in 1993 and are regularly updated based on new evidence.

Goals:

  • Improve quality of care by matching the clinical question to the best imaging modality
  • Reduce inappropriate imaging (both over- and under-utilization)
  • Reduce unnecessary radiation exposure
  • Improve cost-effectiveness in healthcare

Structure of the ACR Criteria​

Organization:

  • Divided into clinical topics (e.g., "Acute Chest Pain — Suspected Pulmonary Embolism")
  • Each topic has a panel of radiologists and clinicians who review evidence
  • Each variant (clinical scenario) is assigned a Rating for each imaging modality

Rating Scale:

ScoreInterpretation
7–9Usually Appropriate — expected benefits exceed risks substantially
4–6May Be Appropriate — benefit/risk varies; clinical judgment required
1–3Usually Not Appropriate — risk likely exceeds benefit

Radiation categories assigned to each procedure:

  • O = no radiation (ultrasound, MRI)
  • A = minimal (<1 mSv)
  • B = low (1–5 mSv)
  • C = medium (5–10 mSv)
  • D = high (>10 mSv)

How to Use the ACR Criteria​

  1. Identify the clinical condition/variant
  2. Look up the topic in ACR AC (available free at acsearch.acr.org)
  3. Review ratings for each imaging modality
  4. Select the most appropriate study considering patient-specific factors

Examples of ACR Criteria Application​

Scenario 1: Suspected Pulmonary Embolism

  • CT Pulmonary Angiography (CTPA): Usually Appropriate (Rating 9)
  • V/Q scan: Usually Appropriate in certain circumstances (Rating 8)
  • Chest X-ray: May be appropriate (helpful to exclude mimics)
  • D-dimer should guide clinical pre-test probability

Scenario 2: Low Back Pain — No Red Flags, <6 Weeks

  • X-ray lumbar spine: Usually NOT Appropriate (Rating 2) — 98% of cases resolve without imaging
  • MRI lumbar spine: Usually NOT Appropriate (Rating 2) — early MRI does not change outcomes
  • Note: Red flags (fever, weight loss, history of cancer, bladder dysfunction) change the appropriateness

Scenario 3: Right Lower Quadrant Pain — Suspected Appendicitis

  • Adult: CT Abdomen/Pelvis with contrast: Usually Appropriate (Rating 9)
  • Pediatric (under 12): US first; CT only if US equivocal — to reduce radiation
  • Pregnant: Ultrasound first, then MRI; CT only if others unavailable

Clinical Decision-Making Framework​

When selecting an imaging study, consider:

  1. Clinical question: What specific anatomical or functional information is needed?
  2. Sensitivity and specificity: Will this test reliably answer the question?
  3. Availability: Is the modality available in the clinical setting (ED vs. outpatient)?
  4. Speed: Is this an emergency requiring rapid results?
  5. Radiation: Can a non-ionizing modality answer the question equally well?
  6. Cost: Is the more expensive test justified?
  7. Patient factors: Renal function (contrast), metal implants (MRI), pregnancy, claustrophobia
  8. Pre-test probability: Does imaging change clinical management?

5.3 Cost-Effectiveness in Imaging​

Understanding Healthcare Costs​

Direct costs of imaging:

  • Equipment acquisition and maintenance
  • Personnel (radiologist, technologist, nurses)
  • Contrast materials
  • Patient time (indirect cost)

Typical cost ranges (US, approximate):

ModalityApproximate Cost
Chest X-ray$50–$200
Abdominal X-ray$100–$300
CT Head$500–$2,000
CT Chest/Abdomen/Pelvis$2,000–$5,000
MRI Brain$1,500–$4,000
MRI Spine$1,500–$4,000
Ultrasound Abdomen$200–$1,000
PET-CT$4,000–$8,000

Cost-Effectiveness Analysis​

Cost per quality-adjusted life year (QALY):

  • Standard threshold for cost-effectiveness: <$50,000–$100,000/QALY (varies by country)
  • Imaging that detects early, treatable disease can be highly cost-effective

Key cost-effectiveness principles:

  1. Use the right test the first time — ordering multiple sequential tests wastes resources
  2. Avoid tests that won't change management — imaging findings should influence treatment decisions
  3. Apply clinical pre-test probability — imaging is least cost-effective when pre-test probability is very low or very high
  4. Use algorithms — structured pathways reduce unnecessary imaging (e.g., Wells score + D-dimer before CTPA)

Economic Considerations in CT Screening​

Lung Cancer Screening CT:

  • Low-dose CT (LDCT) in high-risk patients (heavy smokers, 50–80 years)
  • Reduces lung cancer mortality by 20–24% (NLST and NELSON trials)
  • Cost-effective when appropriately targeted

Colon Cancer Screening:

  • CT Colonography (virtual colonoscopy) — cost-effective alternative to optical colonoscopy
  • Requires colonoscopy for any polyp found (additional cost if positive)

Coronary Artery Calcium (CAC) Scoring:

  • CT without contrast to detect coronary calcification
  • Risk stratification for cardiovascular disease
  • Cost-effective in intermediate-risk patients for guiding statin therapy

Downstream Costs​

Imaging can create downstream costs through:

  • Incidentalomas: Incidentally discovered findings requiring follow-up (pulmonary nodules, adrenal adenomas, thyroid nodules)
  • False positives: Unnecessary biopsies, additional imaging, patient anxiety
  • Over-diagnosis: Detecting conditions that would never have caused symptoms (e.g., indolent prostate cancer on MRI)

Structured reporting and management guidelines (e.g., Fleischner Society guidelines for pulmonary nodules, TI-RADS for thyroid nodules) help manage incidentalomas cost-effectively.

5.4 Avoiding Unnecessary Radiation Exposure​

The Problem of Inappropriate CT Use​

CT is the largest single source of medical radiation exposure in the US, accounting for ~48% of collective dose from medical radiation. Rising CT utilization has led to concerns about population-level cancer risk.

Estimates (controversial but widely cited):

  • ~1.5–2% of all cancers in the US may be attributable to CT radiation
  • One CT abdomen/pelvis ≈ 3.3 years background radiation equivalent
  • Risk is highest in children (longer life expectancy, more radiosensitive)

Identifying Inappropriate Imaging​

Red flags for potentially unnecessary imaging:

  • Repeating a study within 24–48 hours without clinical change
  • Ordering CT when ultrasound can answer the same question (particularly in young women and children)
  • Imaging low back pain without red flags within first 6 weeks
  • Ordering brain CT for uncomplicated headache without red flags
  • Follow-up CT for small pulmonary nodules in low-risk patients not following Fleischner guidelines
  • "Routine" pre-operative chest X-ray in young, healthy patients

Clinical Decision Support Integration​

Electronic Clinical Decision Support (CDS):

  • Software embedded in the Electronic Health Record (EHR)
  • Alerts clinicians when ordering "usually not appropriate" studies
  • Required by law for advanced imaging in Medicare (PAMA, US 2020)
  • Studies show 15–30% reduction in inappropriate imaging with CDS implementation

How CDS works:

  1. Clinician enters clinical indication
  2. CDS cross-references ACR Appropriateness Criteria
  3. System displays appropriateness rating for ordered study
  4. Clinician can override with documentation
  5. Data tracked for quality improvement

Radiation Reduction Strategies​

For clinicians ordering imaging:

  • Ask: "Will this test change my management?"
  • Consider: "Can US or MRI answer this question?"
  • Apply clinical decision rules before ordering (Ottawa Ankle Rules, Wells score, NEXUS criteria)
  • Avoid repeating recent imaging without clinical justification
  • Review prior imaging before ordering new studies

For radiologists:

  • Optimize protocols for minimum dose consistent with diagnostic quality
  • Use iterative reconstruction in CT (50% dose reduction vs. filtered back projection)
  • Apply dose modulation (lower dose to less dense body parts)
  • Avoid unnecessary additional phases in CT (each phase doubles dose)

5.5 Clinical Decision Support Tools​

Validated Clinical Decision Rules​

These evidence-based rules reduce unnecessary imaging:

Ottawa Ankle/Foot Rules​

Question: Does this patient need an ankle or foot X-ray?

Ankle X-ray required if ANY of:

  • Bone tenderness at posterior 6 cm of fibula or tip of lateral malleolus
  • Bone tenderness at posterior 6 cm of tibia or tip of medial malleolus
  • Inability to bear weight immediately and in the ED (4 steps)

Foot X-ray required if:

  • Bone tenderness at base of 5th metatarsal
  • Bone tenderness at navicular
  • Inability to bear weight

Performance: Sensitivity ~99% for fracture; reduces X-rays by 30–40%

Ottawa Knee Rules​

X-ray needed only if:

  • Age ≥55
  • Tenderness at fibular head
  • Isolated patella tenderness (no other bone tenderness)
  • Inability to flex to 90°
  • Inability to bear weight

NEXUS Criteria (Cervical Spine Imaging)​

C-spine imaging NOT required if ALL criteria met:

  • No posterior midline cervical tenderness
  • No focal neurological deficit
  • Normal alertness
  • No intoxication
  • No distracting injury

Canadian C-Spine Rule — more specific in certain populations

Wells Score for DVT and PE​

DVT Wells Score: Estimates pre-test probability; guides use of D-dimer and Doppler ultrasound

PE Wells Score:

  • Low probability (score <2): D-dimer; if negative, no CTPA needed
  • Moderate probability: D-dimer; if elevated, CTPA
  • High probability (score >6): Direct CTPA

HEART Score (Chest Pain in ED)​

Guides whether to image for ACS:

  • History, ECG, Age, Risk factors, Troponin
  • Low HEART score: safe discharge without advanced imaging
  • High HEART score: early cardiology referral ± coronary imaging

The "Right Test, Right Patient, Right Time" Framework​

Modern radiology stewardship aims to ensure:

  • Right test: Most appropriate modality for the clinical question
  • Right patient: Imaging in patients who will benefit (appropriate pre-test probability)
  • Right time: Timely imaging that influences management
  • Right indication: Documented clinical justification
  • Right preparation: Proper patient preparation to maximize diagnostic yield

Key Points Summary​

  1. ACR Appropriateness Criteria provide evidence-based ratings (1–9) for imaging appropriateness in specific clinical scenarios

  2. Modality selection should match the clinical question, considering radiation, cost, availability, and patient factors

  3. Pre-test probability is crucial — imaging is most valuable when pre-test probability is intermediate

  4. Clinical decision rules (Ottawa Rules, Wells Score, NEXUS) reduce unnecessary imaging while maintaining diagnostic accuracy

  5. CT is the major source of medical radiation exposure; avoiding inappropriate CT is a major quality improvement goal

  6. Clinical decision support software embedded in EHR reduces inappropriate imaging by 15–30%

  7. Incidentalomas create downstream costs; structured reporting systems (BI-RADS, LI-RADS) provide management guidance

Review Questions​

  1. A 30-year-old healthy woman presents with low back pain of 3 weeks duration without red flags. What is the ACR appropriateness rating for lumbar spine MRI at this time?

  2. A 25-year-old pregnant woman (14 weeks) has right lower quadrant pain. List the imaging studies in order of preference, according to evidence-based guidelines.

  3. What is the Wells Score and how does it guide imaging in suspected pulmonary embolism?

  4. An emergency physician wants to order a CT head for a 35-year-old with a tension headache and normal neurological exam. Using the ACR Appropriateness Criteria, is this appropriate?

  5. What is "downstream cost" in radiology, and how do structured reporting systems like Fleischner guidelines help mitigate it?
