# Gastrointestinal Pathology

This chapter covers the major gastrointestinal pathologies encountered in clinical radiology practice, including bowel obstruction, inflammatory conditions, and acute abdominal emergencies.

## Bowel Obstruction

### Small Bowel Obstruction (SBO)
- **Causes**: Adhesions (60%), hernias (15%), Crohn's disease, tumors, volvulus
- **AXR findings**: Dilated small bowel >3 cm, central location, valvulae conniventes, multiple air-fluid levels
- **CT findings**: Transition point, proximal dilation with distal decompression
- **Closed-loop obstruction**: Two points of obstruction — whirl sign, C-shape — high risk of strangulation

### Large Bowel Obstruction (LBO)
- **Causes**: Colorectal carcinoma (60%), diverticulitis, volvulus
- **AXR findings**: Peripheral dilated colon, haustral folds, distended cecum
- **Cecal dilation >9 cm**: Risk of perforation
- **Management**: Emergency if cecum critically distended

### Volvulus
- **Sigmoid volvulus**: Most common; "coffee bean" or "omega loop" sign on AXR
- **Cecal volvulus**: Coffee bean in left upper quadrant; cecum rotates anteromedially
- **Gastric volvulus**: Organoaxial or mesenteroaxial rotation

## Acute Appendicitis

### Imaging Algorithm
1. **Ultrasound** — first-line in children and young women
2. **CT** — gold standard in adults; sensitivity >95%
3. **MRI** — preferred in pregnancy

### CT Features
- Appendix diameter >6 mm
- Periappendiceal fat stranding
- Appendicolith (25-35% of cases)
- Appendiceal wall thickening and enhancement
- Periappendiceal fluid

### Complications
- **Perforation**: Loss of mural integrity, free air, abscess
- **Phlegmon**: Inflammatory mass without discrete abscess
- **Abscess**: Rim-enhancing fluid collection

### Alvarado Score
Relevant clinical scoring combined with imaging for management decisions.

## Inflammatory Bowel Disease

### Crohn's Disease
- **Distribution**: Any part of GI tract, skip lesions, transmural inflammation
- **CT findings**: Wall thickening, mural stratification, mesenteric fat stranding ("creeping fat"), comb sign (engorged vasa recta)
- **Complications**: Fistulae, abscesses, strictures, bowel obstruction
- **MRI enterography**: Preferred for small bowel Crohn's (no radiation)
- **Activity assessment**: Active inflammation vs. fibrotic stricture (treatment implications)

### Ulcerative Colitis
- **Distribution**: Continuous from rectum proximally; rectum always involved
- **CT findings**: Continuous wall thickening, loss of haustration, rectal involvement
- **Severe attack**: Toxic megacolon — colon >6 cm, wall thickening, thumbprinting
- **Long-term**: Increased colorectal cancer risk; lead pipe colon on barium

## Diverticular Disease

### Diverticulosis
- **CT**: Outpouchings from colonic wall, typically sigmoid, no surrounding inflammation
- **Complications**: Haemorrhage (most common cause of lower GI bleed in elderly), perforation, fistula

### Diverticulitis
- **CT findings**: Pericolonic fat stranding, wall thickening, diverticula
- **Hinchey Classification**:
  - Stage I: Pericolic abscess
  - Stage II: Distant abscess (pelvic, retroperitoneal)
  - Stage III: Purulent peritonitis
  - Stage IV: Fecal peritonitis

## Pancreatitis

### Acute Pancreatitis
- **CT Severity Index (Balthazar + Necrosis)**:
  - Grade A-B: No necrosis — mild
  - Grade C-D-E: With increasing necrosis — severe
- **Complications**: Pseudocyst, walled-off necrosis (WON), abscess, splenic vein thrombosis, pseudoaneurysm
- **Imaging timing**: CT at 72 hours for necrosis assessment

### Chronic Pancreatitis
- **CT findings**: Pancreatic atrophy, ductal dilation (chain of lakes), parenchymal and ductal calcifications
- **Groove pancreatitis**: Fibrous tissue in pancreaticoduodenal groove — may mimic pancreatic head carcinoma

### Pancreatic Cancer
- **CT findings**: Hypoenhancing mass in pancreatic head, double duct sign, vascular encasement, lymphadenopathy
- **Staging**: Resectable vs. borderline resectable vs. unresectable based on vascular involvement

## Ischemic Bowel

### Mesenteric Ischemia
- **Types**: Arterial (SMA occlusion), venous (SMV thrombosis), non-occlusive (low flow state)
- **CT findings**: Bowel wall thickening, reduced/absent enhancement, pneumatosis intestinalis, portal venous gas, mesenteric edema
- **Arterial occlusion**: Filling defect in SMA on CT angiography
- **Venous thrombosis**: Thrombus in SMV, bowel wall thickening, mesenteric edema

### Colonic Ischemia
- **Most common form** of intestinal ischemia
- **Watershed areas**: Splenic flexure and sigmoid (limited collateral supply)
- **CT**: Segmental wall thickening at watershed zones
- **Colonoscopy**: Confirms diagnosis

## Colorectal Cancer

### CT Staging (TNM)
- **T staging**: Wall involvement, pericolonic extension
- **N staging**: Regional lymph nodes >10 mm
- **M staging**: Liver, lung, peritoneal metastases

### CT Colonography (Virtual Colonoscopy)
- **Technique**: Colon distended with CO₂; supine and prone positions
- **Detection**: Polyps >6 mm, colorectal cancer
- **Advantage**: Non-invasive, no sedation required
- **Limitation**: Cannot biopsy; flat lesions may be missed

## Gastrointestinal Bleeding

### Upper GI Bleeding
- **CT angiography**: Active bleeding as contrast extravasation in bowel lumen
- **Nuclear medicine**: Tc-99m RBC scan detects bleeding at 0.1 mL/min
- **Angiography**: Detects bleeding >0.5 mL/min, allows embolization

### Lower GI Bleeding
- **Causes**: Diverticular (most common in elderly), AVM, colitis, cancer
- **CT angiography**: First-line for active bleeding; sensitivity ~80%

## Hernias

### External Hernias
- **Inguinal hernia**: Most common; direct (medial to inferior epigastric) vs. indirect (lateral)
- **Femoral hernia**: Below inguinal ligament; higher risk of strangulation
- **CT**: Bowel/fat in hernia sac, assessment for obstruction or strangulation

### Internal Hernias
- **Post-gastric bypass**: Petersen's hernia, mesenteric defect hernia
- **CT**: Clustered bowel loops, mesenteric swirl, venous congestion

Understanding gastrointestinal pathology enables accurate diagnosis and appropriate triage of patients presenting with acute abdominal symptoms.
