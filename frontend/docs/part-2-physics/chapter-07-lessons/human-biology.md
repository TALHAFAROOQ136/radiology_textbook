# Human Biology

A fundamental understanding of human biology — from the atomic level to the whole organism — is essential for appreciating how ionising radiation interacts with and affects the body.

## Organisation of the Human Body

The body is organised in levels of increasing complexity:

| Level | Examples |
|---|---|
| Atomic | Hydrogen, oxygen, carbon, nitrogen |
| Molecular | DNA, proteins, lipids, water |
| Cellular | Neurons, red blood cells, epithelial cells |
| Tissue | Epithelium, connective tissue, muscle, nervous tissue |
| Organ | Heart, liver, lung, kidney |
| Organ system | Cardiovascular, respiratory, digestive |
| Organism | The whole human body |

---

## Chemical Composition of the Human Body

| Element | % Body Mass | Radiologic Relevance |
|---|---|---|
| Oxygen | 65% | Major component of water; indirect radiation damage |
| Carbon | 18% | Organic molecules; basis of all tissues |
| Hydrogen | 10% | Water; MRI signal source |
| Nitrogen | 3% | Proteins, nucleic acids |
| Calcium | 1.5% | Bone (high Z → high X-ray attenuation) |
| Phosphorus | 1% | DNA backbone, ATP |
| Other | &lt;1% | Trace elements |

**Water content**: The body is ~60–70% water by mass — critical because X-ray damage occurs mainly via water radiolysis (indirect action).

## The Cell

The fundamental unit of life:

### Cell Structure
| Component | Function | Radiation Sensitivity |
|---|---|---|
| Nucleus | Contains DNA; controls cell activity | Most sensitive |
| DNA | Genetic information | Primary radiation target |
| Mitochondria | Energy production (ATP) | Moderate sensitivity |
| Cell membrane | Regulates transport | Lower sensitivity |
| Cytoplasm | Site of protein synthesis | Lower sensitivity |

### Cell Division
**Mitosis** (somatic cells — 46 chromosomes):
- G1 → S (DNA synthesis) → G2 → M (mitosis)
- Produces two identical daughter cells
- Radiosensitivity varies by phase (M and G2 most sensitive)

**Meiosis** (germ cells — gametes):
- Produces 4 cells with 23 chromosomes each
- Radiation damage can lead to heritable genetic effects

## Tissues and Their Radiation Sensitivity

### Tissue Types
| Tissue | Characteristics | Radiosensitivity |
|---|---|---|
| Epithelium | Rapidly dividing; lines surfaces | High |
| Connective tissue | Supports organs; slow turnover | Low-moderate |
| Muscle | Post-mitotic; non-dividing | Low |
| Nervous tissue | Post-mitotic; non-dividing | Low (but CNS effects at high dose) |

### Bergonié and Tribondeau Rule
Cells are most radiosensitive when:
- Actively dividing (high mitotic index)
- Undifferentiated (primitive, stem-like)
- Having a long mitotic future

---

## Organ Systems and Radiation Effects

### Haematopoietic System (Bone Marrow)
- Most radiosensitive organ system
- Stem cells rapidly divide to produce blood cells
- Radiation → aplasia → infection, haemorrhage, anaemia

### Gastrointestinal System
- Intestinal crypt cells divide rapidly
- Radiation → mucosal denudation → malabsorption, infection

### Reproductive System
- Gonads are highly radiosensitive
- Spermatogonia most sensitive male cells
- Oocytes sensitive; ovarian failure possible at moderate doses

### Lens of the Eye
- Lens epithelial cells divide slowly at equator
- Radiation → cataract (posterior subcapsular opacity)
- Threshold lowered in recent ICRP recommendations (~0.5 Gy)

### Thyroid Gland
- Particularly radiosensitive in children
- Risk of thyroid cancer after childhood head/neck irradiation
- Iodine-131 therapy exploits thyroid iodine uptake

---

## The Immune System and Radiation

- Lymphocytes are among the most radiosensitive cells
- Radiation → lymphocytopaenia (rapid drop in lymphocyte count)
- Lymphocyte count used as a biomarker of radiation exposure
- Suppression of immune function → increased infection risk

---

## Reproductive and Genetic Considerations

### Radiosensitivity by Age
| Group | Sensitivity | Reason |
|---|---|---|
| Embryo/fetus | Extremely high | Rapid cell division, organogenesis |
| Children | High | Growing tissues, long life expectancy |
| Adults | Moderate | Slower cell turnover |
| Elderly | Lower | Less remaining life; slower cell division |

### Genetic Effects
- Radiation-induced mutations in germ cells (sperm, ova)
- Heritable effects theoretically possible but difficult to demonstrate in humans
- No confirmed increase in heritable disease in children of atomic bomb survivors
