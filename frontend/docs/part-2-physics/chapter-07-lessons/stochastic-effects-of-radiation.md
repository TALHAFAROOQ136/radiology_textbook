# Stochastic Effects of Radiation

Stochastic effects are radiation-induced health effects that occur by chance, with no dose threshold — any dose, however small, carries a theoretical probability of causing harm. The probability (but not severity) of the effect increases with dose.

## Characteristics of Stochastic Effects

| Feature | Description |
|---|---|
| Threshold | None (any dose carries some risk) |
| Dose-response | Probability increases with dose |
| Severity | Not dose-dependent (all-or-nothing) |
| Examples | Cancer induction, heritable effects |
| Latency | Years to decades after exposure |

---

## Types of Stochastic Effects

### 1. Radiation-Induced Cancer (Somatic Effects)
- Most important stochastic effect in radiation protection
- Caused by mutations in somatic (body) cells
- Activates oncogenes or inactivates tumour suppressor genes
- Indistinguishable from spontaneous cancer

### 2. Heritable (Genetic) Effects
- Mutations in germ cells (sperm, ova) passed to offspring
- Theoretical risk — not confirmed in human populations
- Studies of atomic bomb survivors' children: no confirmed increase in heritable disease
- Risk is small relative to cancer risk

---

## Dose-Response Models

### Linear No-Threshold (LNT) Model
The standard model used in radiation protection:
- Risk proportional to dose at all levels
- No safe threshold — even very small doses carry finite risk
- **Basis**: Extrapolation from high-dose data (atomic bomb survivors)
- **Limitations**: Unproven at very low doses; may overestimate risk

### Alternative Models
| Model | Description | Implication |
|---|---|---|
| LNT | Linear, no threshold | Any dose risky |
| Threshold | No risk below threshold | Safe dose exists |
| Hormesis | Low doses slightly beneficial | Very low dose is protective |
| Supra-linear | Higher risk per unit dose at low doses | More conservative than LNT |

**Regulatory policy**: LNT model used because it is conservative and practical — errs on the side of caution.

## Evidence for Radiation Carcinogenesis

### Atomic Bomb Survivors (Life Span Study)
- ~120,000 survivors followed since 1950
- Most comprehensive data on radiation carcinogenesis
- Significant excess cancer at doses &gt;100–200 mSv
- Extrapolated to lower doses using LNT

### Medical Radiation Exposures
- Ankylosing spondylitis patients irradiated therapeutically → excess leukaemia
- Childhood thyroid cancer after Chernobyl fallout (iodine-131)
- Increased breast cancer in women given fluoroscopy for TB
- Increased lung cancer in underground miners (radon)

### Occupational Exposures
- Nuclear industry workers: small but detectable excess cancer risk
- Radiologists (historical): excess leukaemia before modern protection
- Uranium miners: lung cancer from radon

## Cancer Risk Estimates

### ICRP Nominal Risk Coefficients (ICRP Publication 103, 2007)
| Population | Total Cancer Risk | Fatal Cancer Risk |
|---|---|---|
| General public | ~5.5% per Sv | ~4% per Sv |
| Workers | ~4.1% per Sv | ~3% per Sv |

## Risk from Diagnostic Imaging

### Estimated Cancer Risk from Common Examinations
| Examination | Effective Dose | Approximate Lifetime Cancer Risk |
|---|---|---|
| Chest X-ray | 0.02 mSv | ~1 in 1,000,000 |
| Chest CT | 7 mSv | ~1 in 3,000 |
| Abdomen/pelvis CT | 10 mSv | ~1 in 2,000 |
| Barium enema | 7 mSv | ~1 in 3,000 |
| Annual background | 3 mSv | — |

**Context**: Baseline lifetime cancer risk ~40% (1 in 2.5 people). Diagnostic imaging adds a very small increment.

### Risk in Perspective
- Risk from a chest X-ray ≈ risk from smoking 1 cigarette
- Risk from abdominal CT ≈ risk from smoking ~700 cigarettes
- Benefit of accurate diagnosis far outweighs the small radiation risk in most clinical situations

## Modifying Factors for Stochastic Risk

### Age at Exposure
- Children are more sensitive (more remaining life; more rapidly dividing cells)
- Risk from exposure at age 1 ≈ 3–4× risk from exposure at age 50

### Sex
- Females generally have higher overall radiation risk than males
- Partly due to breast and thyroid cancer sensitivity

### Genetic Susceptibility
- Inherited DNA repair defects (ATM heterozygotes, BRCA1/2 carriers) → higher risk
- Background cancer risk already higher in these individuals

### Dose Rate
- Chronic low-dose-rate exposure: risk per unit dose may be lower than acute exposure
- Dose and dose-rate effectiveness factor (DDREF) = 2 (ICRP) applied to high-dose data

## Radiation Protection Against Stochastic Effects

Since there is no threshold, protection aims to minimise dose:

### Three Principles (ICRP System)
1. **Justification**: Every exposure must produce net benefit
2. **Optimisation (ALARA)**: Doses as low as reasonably achievable
3. **Dose limitation**: Annual dose limits for workers and public

### Dose Limits (ICRP 103)
| Group | Effective Dose Limit |
|---|---|
| Radiation workers | 20 mSv/year (averaged over 5 years); 50 mSv in any single year |
| General public | 1 mSv/year |
| Eye lens (workers) | 20 mSv/year |
| Skin (workers) | 500 mSv/year |

*Note: Dose limits do not apply to patients receiving medical exposures.*

## Summary

| Feature | Stochastic Effects |
|---|---|
| Threshold | None |
| Main effect | Cancer (somatic); hereditary effects (germ cells) |
| Dose-response | Probability linear with dose (LNT model) |
| Severity | Independent of dose |
| Latency | 5–40+ years |
| Modifying factors | Age, sex, tissue type, genetic susceptibility |
| Protection | Justification, optimisation (ALARA), dose limits |
