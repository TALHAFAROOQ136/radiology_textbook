# Molecular Radiobiology

Molecular radiobiology examines radiation effects at the level of molecules — particularly DNA, proteins, and lipids. Understanding these molecular events explains why radiation causes the biological effects observed at the cellular, tissue, and whole-body levels.

## Target Molecules

### DNA — The Critical Target
DNA is uniquely important because:
- It cannot be replaced if permanently damaged (unlike proteins)
- A single critical mutation can transform a normal cell to malignant
- Loss of reproductive integrity eliminates the cell lineage

**DNA structure**:
- Double helix of nucleotide chains
- Bases: adenine (A), thymine (T), guanine (G), cytosine (C)
- A-T and G-C base pairing
- Packaged as chromatin in chromosomes

### Other Molecular Targets
| Molecule | Damage | Consequence |
|---|---|---|
| Proteins (enzymes) | Denaturation, cross-linking | Loss of catalytic function |
| Lipids (membranes) | Peroxidation | Membrane disruption |
| RNA | Strand breaks | Temporary loss of gene expression |

## Mechanisms of DNA Damage

### Direct Action
- Radiation quantum directly ionises a DNA atom
- Electron ejected from DNA molecule
- Results in radical cation on DNA
- More important for high-LET radiation

### Indirect Action
- Radiation ionises water → hydroxyl radicals (OH·)
- OH· diffuses to DNA and abstracts hydrogen atoms
- Creates DNA radicals → strand breaks, base damage
- Accounts for ~60–70% of X-ray-induced DNA damage

## Types of DNA Lesions

### Single-Strand Breaks (SSB)
- One phosphodiester bond broken in sugar-phosphate backbone
- ~1,000 SSBs per cell per Gray
- Repaired rapidly and accurately using opposite strand as template
- Rarely lethal if isolated

### Double-Strand Breaks (DSB)
- Both strands broken within ~10 base pairs
- ~40 DSBs per cell per Gray
- Most critical radiation-induced lesion
- Misrepair leads to chromosomal aberrations, mutations, or cell death

**DSB detection**:
- γ-H2AX foci form at DSB sites (phosphorylation of histone H2AX)
- Used as a biomarker of radiation exposure and DSB repair

### Base Damage
- Oxidation, alkylation, or ring opening of DNA bases
- ~1,000–2,000 base lesions per cell per Gray
- Usually repaired by base excision repair (BER)

### DNA-DNA and DNA-Protein Cross-links
- Covalent bonds between DNA strands or DNA-protein
- Interfere with replication and transcription
- Repaired by nucleotide excision repair (NER)

## DNA Repair Pathways

### Base Excision Repair (BER)
- Repairs oxidised, alkylated, or deaminated bases
- Key enzyme: DNA glycosylase removes damaged base
- Gap filled by DNA polymerase β
- Most common repair pathway

### Nucleotide Excision Repair (NER)
- Repairs bulky DNA lesions, cross-links
- 12–24 nucleotide segment excised around damage
- Gap filled by DNA polymerase δ/ε

### Homologous Recombination (HR)
- Repairs DSBs accurately using sister chromatid as template
- Active in S and G2 phases (when sister chromatid available)
- Slow but accurate — minimal error rate

### Non-Homologous End Joining (NHEJ)
- Repairs DSBs by directly ligating broken ends
- Active in all cell cycle phases
- Faster than HR but error-prone
- Major pathway for DSB repair in G1 phase

## Consequences of Unrepaired or Misrepaired DNA

### Chromosomal Aberrations
Visible cytogenetically (microscopy after metaphase arrest):

| Aberration | Cause | Consequence |
|---|---|---|
| Deletion | Loss of chromosome segment | Loss of gene function |
| Translocation | Exchange between chromosomes | Gene fusion (e.g., leukaemia) |
| Inversion | Segment reversed | Altered gene regulation |
| Dicentric chromosome | Two centromeres joined | Cell death at mitosis |
| Ring chromosome | Chromosome ends joined | Unstable; often lethal |

**Dicentric chromosomes**: Most sensitive biological dosimeter — frequency proportional to radiation dose.

### Point Mutations
- Single base changes in DNA sequence
- May activate oncogenes or inactivate tumour suppressor genes
- Foundation of radiation carcinogenesis

### Genomic Instability
- Radiation induces instability that persists for many generations after exposure
- Progeny of irradiated cells show elevated mutation rate
- Contributes to delayed radiation effects

## Cell Signalling After Radiation

### DNA Damage Response (DDR)
Radiation activates a complex signalling network:

1. **Sensor proteins** (MRN complex, PARP) detect DSBs
2. **Transducer kinases** (ATM, ATR, DNA-PKcs) amplify signal
3. **Effector proteins** activate:
   - Cell cycle checkpoints (arrest at G1/S, S, G2/M)
   - DNA repair pathways
   - Apoptosis (if damage too severe)

### p53 — The Guardian of the Genome
- Tumour suppressor protein activated by radiation
- Triggers G1 arrest (time for repair) or apoptosis
- Mutated in ~50% of human cancers → impaired radiation response
- p53 wild-type cells: more apoptosis after radiation
- p53 mutant cells: reduced apoptosis; may contribute to radioresistance

### Bystander Effect
- Irradiated cells signal to neighbouring non-irradiated cells
- Unirradiated cells show DNA damage, mutations, apoptosis
- Mediated by gap junctions and secreted factors (cytokines, ROS)
- Complicates low-dose risk extrapolation

## Molecular Basis of Radiosensitivity

Cells with defects in DNA repair are hypersensitive to radiation:

| Syndrome | Defective Gene | Radiation Sensitivity |
|---|---|---|
| Ataxia-telangiectasia | ATM | Extreme (100× normal) |
| Nijmegen breakage syndrome | NBS1 | Extreme |
| Fanconi anaemia | FANC genes | High |
| BRCA1/2 mutations | BRCA1, BRCA2 | Moderate increase |

These syndromes demonstrate that intact DSB repair is essential for normal radiation tolerance.
