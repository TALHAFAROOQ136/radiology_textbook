## 1. Introduction

A **radiographic artefact** is any density, pattern, or structural alteration visible on a radiographic image that does not correspond to anatomical structures or pathological changes within the patient. Artefacts may simulate disease (false positive), obscure genuine pathology (false negative), or degrade overall image quality to the point where diagnostic adequacy cannot be assured.

In screen-film radiography, artefacts arise from a complex system involving:
- **Photographic film** — the image receptor and permanent record
- **Intensifying screens** — the phosphor coating that converts X-ray energy to visible light
- **Cassette** — the light-tight housing that maintains screen-film contact
- **Darkroom and film handling** — all processes prior to development
- **Automatic film processor** — the chemical and mechanical development system
- **Exposure and technique factors** — conditions during image acquisition

Each component of this system has characteristic failure modes that produce recognisable artefact patterns. The ability to identify an artefact, determine its source, and implement appropriate corrective action is a core competency in radiographic quality assurance.

:::info Historical Relevance
Although digital radiography has replaced screen-film systems in most modern departments, understanding screen-film artefacts remains clinically important for:
- Departments transitioning from or still using screen-film systems
- Reviewing historical radiographic records stored on film
- Examination and professional knowledge assessments
- Understanding the baseline principles of image quality management that apply across all modalities
:::

## 2. Film-Related Artefacts

### 2.1 Fogging

**Fogging** refers to an unwanted increase in film base density caused by exposure to agents other than the primary X-ray beam. It reduces image contrast and may render subtle findings invisible.

#### Types of Fog and Their Origins

**Radiation Fog**

Unexposed film is sensitive to all ionising radiation, not only the primary beam. Sources of radiation fog include:
- **Scatter radiation** from adjacent examinations if cassettes are stored near the X-ray room
- **Background radiation** from cosmic rays or naturally occurring radioactive material (NORM) in storage walls
- **Secondary emission** from materials near the X-ray tube during exposure

Radiation fog produces a **uniform grey veil** over the entire image, reducing contrast throughout. In high-scatter environments (e.g., fluoroscopy suites), even briefly adjacent unexposed cassettes may accumulate fog.

**Light Fog**

Any white light reaching the film before processing produces fog. Sources include:
- **Cassette light leaks** — damaged hinges, worn felt seals, or cracked cassette bodies
- **Darkroom light leaks** — gaps around doors, ventilation openings, pass-through boxes
- **Safelight failure** — incorrect safelight filter (wrong colour), cracked filter, excessive safelight wattage, or safelight positioned too close to the work surface
- **Accidental white light exposure** — loading/unloading film in non-darkroom conditions

Light fog is characterised by:
- **Uniform distribution**: If the cassette was light-tight and fogged after opening, density is uniform
- **Gradient distribution**: If a leak is along one edge of the cassette, fog density tapers from the leak source
- **Safelight fog**: Typically affects the entire film surface exposed to the safelight; may be asymmetric if film was partially shielded

**Safelight Testing:**

The standard safelight test (coin test / sensitometric test) verifies safelight safety:

1. Place an unexposed film on the darkroom work surface
2. Cover half the film with an opaque card
3. Expose the uncovered half to the safelight for 2 minutes from normal working distance
4. Process the film
5. Compare density of exposed and unexposed halves — acceptable if difference is less than 0.05 OD units

**Heat and Humidity Fog**

Excess temperature and moisture accelerate chemical deterioration of the film emulsion, producing:
- Elevated base fog density (measured by sensitometry)
- Reduced speed and contrast
- Granular or mottled appearance in severe cases

Film storage requirements:
- Temperature: 10–21°C (50–70°F)
- Relative humidity: 30–50%
- Store vertically in original packaging
- Avoid storage near heat sources (processors, steam pipes)

**Age Fog (Outdated Film)**

Radiographic film carries a manufacturer's expiry date. Beyond this date, spontaneous emulsion deterioration produces increasing base fog. Film should be used in rotation (first in, first out) and never used beyond expiry.

#### Fog Recognition and Impact

| Fog Type | Distribution | Density Change | Contrast Effect |
|---|---|---|---|
| Radiation fog | Uniform (whole film) | Uniform increase | Reduced throughout |
| Cassette light leak | Gradient from edge/hinge | Higher at leak source | Reduced at affected area |
| Safelight fog | Uniform or patchy | Mild uniform increase | Mild reduction |
| Heat/humidity | Uniform | Moderate increase | Moderate reduction |
| Age fog | Uniform | Variable; progressive | Progressive reduction |

### 2.2 Static Electricity Artefacts

Static artefacts are produced by electrostatic discharge (ESD) within the cassette during film loading, unloading, or during the examination. The discharge exposes the film at the point of discharge, producing characteristic dark (black) branching patterns.

#### Physics of Static Generation

When film is slid across a surface (screen, cassette interior, or packaging material), triboelectric charging occurs — charge separation at the interface generates a static potential. When the potential exceeds the dielectric breakdown threshold of air, discharge occurs and the film emulsion is exposed at the discharge point.

Contributing factors:
- **Low relative humidity** — dry air has low conductivity; charge builds up rather than dissipating
- **Rapid film handling** — faster sliding generates greater charge
- **Synthetic materials** — synthetic screen coatings and cassette linings accumulate more charge than natural materials
- **Cold temperatures** — cold environments typically have low absolute humidity

#### Types of Static Artefact

| Type | Appearance | Cause |
|---|---|---|
| **Crown static** | Multiple radiating dark lines from a central point, resembling a crown or starburst | Single discharge point with radial current spread |
| **Tree static** | Branching dark lines resembling a tree or lightning bolt | Multiple sequential discharges along a path |
| **Smudge static** | Irregular dark smudge or cloud | Diffuse low-level discharge over an area |
| **Track static** | Parallel dark lines | Film sliding across screen surface |

All static artefacts appear **dark (increased density)** because the discharge exposes the film, creating a developable latent image. They cannot be confused with true anatomy because they do not conform to any anatomical distribution.

#### Prevention of Static Artefacts

- Maintain darkroom relative humidity between **40–60%**
- Handle film slowly and deliberately; avoid rapid sliding
- Use **anti-static sprays** on screens (manufacturer-approved products only)
- Ensure screens are clean — dust and debris increase surface resistance
- Ground metal cassette components
- Use humidifiers in winter months when indoor air is particularly dry

### 2.3 Pressure Artefacts

Mechanical pressure applied to the film before development can alter the latent image by creating localised stress changes in the emulsion crystal structure.

#### Crescent (Fingernail) Marks

- **Appearance**: Dark crescentic (half-moon) marks, typically 5–10 mm in size
- **Cause**: Film bent sharply at a point, creating a localised stress artefact in the emulsion
- The bending causes crystal lattice deformation that produces a developable latent image
- **Prevention**: Never bend film sharply; hold film at edges; avoid point loading

#### Pressure Marks

- **Appearance**: Dark or light spots at pressure points
- **Cause**: Objects placed on top of loaded cassettes; stacking of cassettes under weight
- **Prevention**: Store cassettes flat and not stacked under heavy objects

#### Screen-Film Contact Pressure Artefacts

- **Appearance**: Areas of uneven density and blurring
- **Cause**: Non-uniform pressure from warped cassette back or worn foam padding
- This is covered in detail under screen-film contact artefacts (Section 3.2)

### 2.4 Handling Artefacts

These artefacts result from improper physical contact with the film surface before or after processing.

#### Fingerprint Marks

- **Appearance**: Faint or dark fingerprint impressions on the processed film
- **Cause**: Skin oils and sweat contain compounds that affect emulsion chemistry; contact before processing creates a latent image; contact after processing may produce chemical staining
- **Appearance pre-processing**: Dark fingerprint pattern
- **Appearance post-processing**: Greasy smudge; may be cleaned with photographic cleaning solution
- **Prevention**: Handle film only at edges; use lint-free gloves in darkroom

#### Scratches

- **Appearance**: Fine linear marks, white (light) if emulsion is removed, dark if emulsion is disturbed
- **Cause**: Sharp objects (rings, fingernails, cassette edge burrs) contacting the emulsion surface
- **Emulsion removal**: appears white (no silver deposited in scratched area)
- **Emulsion disturbance**: appears dark (mechanical stress creates developable latent image)
- **Prevention**: Remove jewellery before film handling; inspect cassette edges for rough surfaces

#### Cinch Marks

- **Appearance**: Parallel curved dark lines across the film, typically near one end
- **Cause**: Film pulled too tightly from the packaging or a stack, causing tensile stress artefact in the emulsion
- **Prevention**: Remove film carefully from packaging without tension

## 3. Intensifying Screen Artefacts

### 3.1 The Role of Intensifying Screens

Intensifying screens convert X-ray energy into visible light, which exposes the film on either side in a double-screen/double-emulsion cassette system. The phosphor coating must be uniform, clean, and in close contact with the film to produce a sharp, artefact-free image.

The phosphor layer is fragile — it can be scratched, contaminated, or develop artefacts that produce permanent white or dark patterns on every subsequent image made in that cassette.

### 3.2 Screen Damage Artefacts

#### Scratches

- **Appearance**: Fine white (unexposed) lines in the same position on every image made with that cassette
- **Cause**: Physical damage to the phosphor coating by sharp objects; abrasive cleaning materials; cassette edge contact
- The scratch removes phosphor from the screen surface; the film in contact with the damaged area receives less light and less X-ray exposure
- **Result**: White line on the processed film (reduced density at the scratch)
- **Key feature**: Reproducible — appears in identical position on every subsequent image
- **Action**: Replace the screen; damaged screens cannot be repaired

#### Dirt and Debris Contamination

- **Appearance**: White (unexposed) spots or blotches of variable size
- **Cause**: Dust, lint, skin cells, or other particles on the screen surface
- Particles block light emission from the phosphor beneath them
- **Key feature**: May change position between examinations (if loose) or be fixed (if adherent)
- **Action**: Clean screens with manufacturer-recommended antistatic cleaning solution and lint-free cloth; test after cleaning
- **Prevention**: Regular cleaning schedule; store cassettes vertically in protective sleeves; keep darkroom clean

#### Screen Stains

- **Appearance**: Dark (increased density) areas of variable shape
- **Cause**: Chemical contamination of screen surface — processing chemicals, developer, or fixer splashed on open cassette
- Chemical contamination may cause localised increase in phosphor emission
- **Action**: Gentle cleaning; replace screen if staining is permanent

#### Screen Fog (Phosphorescent Afterglow)

- **Cause**: Some phosphor materials exhibit **afterglow** — continued light emission after the X-ray exposure has ended
- **Appearance**: Increased density on the subsequent image in the distribution of the previous image (ghost image)
- More significant with older calcium tungstate screens; modern rare-earth screens have much shorter afterglow
- **Prevention**: Allow adequate time between exposures; erase screens with intense white light exposure before reuse in high-throughput settings

### 3.3 Screen-Film Contact Artefacts

#### Mechanism and Significance

**Screen-film contact** refers to the uniformity and completeness of physical contact between the screen phosphor surface and the adjacent film emulsion. Poor contact introduces a light-diffusion layer between the screen and film, causing blurring at the point of separation.

The physics: light emitted from a screen phosphor crystal travels in all directions. When the screen is in direct contact with the film, only the directly adjacent film area is exposed. When a gap exists, the light spreads laterally before reaching the film — the larger the gap, the larger the spread, and the greater the loss of spatial resolution.

**Clinical consequence:** Blurred areas on the image may obscure:
- Fine fracture lines
- Small pneumothorax edges
- Trabecular bone detail
- Pulmonary vessel margins

#### Causes of Poor Screen-Film Contact

| Cause | Description |
|---|---|
| Warped cassette back | Structural deformation of the rigid cassette backing |
| Worn or compressed foam padding | The foam layer behind the back screen loses springback |
| Trapped air | Air bubble between screen and film, particularly after rapid loading |
| Foreign material | Debris between screen and film |
| Damaged cassette latch | Allows cassette to bow open slightly |
| Manufacturing defect | Screen not uniformly bonded to backing |

#### Wire Mesh Contact Test

The standard method for detecting poor screen-film contact uses a **wire mesh (contact test tool)**:

**Procedure:**
1. Place the wire mesh on top of the closed cassette
2. Expose at a low kVp (typically 50–60 kVp) and appropriate mAs
3. Process the film
4. View the processed film at 2–3 metres distance in a dimly lit room (not on a bright illuminator)

**Interpretation:**
- **Uniform sharpness of the mesh throughout** → Good screen-film contact
- **Blurred area(s)** → Poor contact at the blurred region
- **Peripheral blurring** → Cassette back warp or worn peripheral foam
- **Central blurring** → Central air trapping or central foam compression

**Frequency:** Monthly for all cassettes in clinical use; immediately when blurring artefact is identified on a clinical image.

**Action:** Cassettes with significant contact failure must be removed from clinical use. Minor contact issues may be temporarily managed by allowing cassettes to stand vertically (air escapes), but underlying structural causes should be corrected.

## 4. Film Processing Artefacts

The automatic film processor is the most mechanically complex component of the screen-film system and generates the broadest range of artefacts. Processing artefacts can be categorised as **chemical** (arising from solution composition, concentration, contamination, or temperature) or **mechanical** (arising from the transport roller system).

### 4.1 Processor Chemistry and the Development Process

Understanding the development process is prerequisite to understanding chemical artefacts:

1. **Developer solution** — reduces exposed silver halide crystals to metallic silver (black); selectivity for exposed vs. unexposed crystals produces image contrast
2. **Fixer solution** — dissolves unexposed silver halide; clears film base; hardens emulsion
3. **Wash water** — removes residual fixer to prevent subsequent film degradation
4. **Dryer** — removes moisture; prepares film for viewing

Each stage has specific temperature and time requirements. Deviation produces characteristic artefacts.

### 4.2 Chemical Artefacts

#### Developer Cutoff

- **Appearance**: Sharp horizontal line across the film, above which density is normal and below which density abruptly falls (underdeveloped)
- **Cause**: Developer solution level too low — film partially bypasses developer immersion; the film-developer interface corresponds to the solution surface level
- **Key feature**: The cutoff line is exactly horizontal and extremely sharp — unlike any pathological density change
- **Action**: Check and replenish developer level; verify replenishment rate; inspect for solution leaks

#### Chemical Fog

- **Appearance**: Uniform increased density across entire film; reduced contrast
- **Cause**: Developer contamination; developer exhaustion causing loss of selectivity (develops unexposed as well as exposed crystals); developer pH too high
- **Distinguished from radiation fog**: Chemical fog typically affects all films processed in the affected batch; radiation fog may be selective to particular cassettes
- **Action**: Drain, clean, and replace developer; check replenishment system

#### Dichroic Fog

- **Appearance**: Film appears **green** in transmitted light and **pink** in reflected light — a characteristic two-colour (dichroic) appearance
- **Cause**: Contamination of developer with fixer, or inadequate fixation, creates a mixed silver-silver sulphide deposit that has different optical absorption characteristics in transmission vs. reflection
- **Clinical significance**: Severely affected films are difficult to interpret; archived films with fixer retention develop dichroic fog over years
- **Action**: Drain and clean all solutions; replace contaminated chemistry; check developer-fixer sequence

#### Fixer Retention and Yellow-Brown Staining

- **Appearance**: Yellow-brown staining, typically developing weeks to months after processing
- **Cause**: Inadequate wash removes insufficient fixer; residual thiosulphate reacts with atmospheric oxygen over time to produce coloured degradation products
- **Immediate appearance**: Film appears normal after processing; staining is delayed
- **Prevention**: Adequate wash time and temperature; regular wash water flow rate checks; hypo estimation testing (residual fixer testing)
- **Test**: Apply a drop of hypo estimator solution to the film — colour change indicates residual fixer level

#### Reticulation

- **Appearance**: Fine network (reticulated) pattern across the film surface, resembling cracked mud
- **Cause**: Sudden temperature change between solutions (e.g., warm developer to cold water wash) causes rapid thermal contraction of the gelatin emulsion, cracking it
- **Prevention**: Maintain all solution temperatures within specification; ensure wash water is adequately temperature-controlled

#### Emulsion Lifting and Frilling

- **Appearance**: Areas of wrinkled or detached emulsion on the film surface
- **Cause**: Prolonged soak in water; excessive wash temperature; damage to hardened emulsion
- **Prevention**: Maintain wash water temperature; ensure adequate fixer hardener concentration; replace exhausted fixer

### 4.3 Mechanical (Roller Transport) Artefacts

The automatic processor transports film through the solution tanks and dryer via a series of **rollers**. These rollers are precisely sized and spaced; any contamination, damage, or misalignment produces characteristic periodic artefacts.

#### Pi Lines (π Lines)

The most diagnostically important and distinctive processor artefact:

- **Appearance**: Fine dark lines running **across the film** (perpendicular to the direction of travel), spaced at **intervals equal to π (3.14) times the diameter** of the causative roller
- **Cause**: A contaminated or damaged roller that contacts the film at the same point on each rotation deposits developer or physical pressure at regular intervals
- **Direction**: Always perpendicular to the direction of film travel through the processor
- **Spacing formula**: Line spacing = π × roller diameter (approximately 3.14 × diameter in cm)
- **Diagnostic use**: Measuring the spacing between pi lines identifies which specific roller in the processor is responsible

**Example calculation:**
- If pi lines are spaced 9.4 cm apart: roller diameter = 9.4 ÷ 3.14 = 3.0 cm
- Cross-reference with processor service manual to identify which roller has this diameter

**Causes of roller contamination:**
- Accumulated silver deposits (from developer reaction)
- Chemical crystallisation on roller surface
- Biological contamination (bacterial slime)
- Physical damage (chips, scratches) on roller surface

**Prevention and correction:**
- Daily cleaning of feed tray and accessible rollers
- Weekly crossover roller cleaning
- Regular complete processor cleaning (monthly or per manufacturer schedule)
- Sensitometric monitoring to detect early artefact development

#### Hesitation Marks (Stub Lines)

- **Appearance**: Dark bands running **parallel to the leading edge** of the film; typically one or more bands near the leading end
- **Cause**: Film hesitates (stops momentarily) in the processor — the film edge receives prolonged developer contact, producing a dark band at the hesitation point
- **Causes of hesitation**: Worn or misaligned transport rollers; roller drive failure; film jam clearing
- **Key feature**: Parallel to the leading film edge, not perpendicular (unlike pi lines)
- **Distinction from pi lines**: Orientation — hesitation marks are parallel to leading edge; pi lines are perpendicular to direction of travel

#### Roller Marks (Periodic Bands)

- **Appearance**: Regular dark or light bands across the film, perpendicular to travel direction
- **Cause**: Dirty or unevenly loaded transport rollers that periodically apply uneven pressure or deposit solution
- **Distinction from pi lines**: Roller marks are typically wider and less defined than pi lines; pi lines are specifically caused by a point of contamination on one roller
- **Action**: Clean and inspect all rollers; replace damaged rollers

#### Chatter Marks

- **Appearance**: Fine, regular, closely spaced lines across the entire film
- **Cause**: Mechanical vibration in the roller transport system — loose rollers, worn roller bearings, or mechanical resonance in the transport mechanism
- **Action**: Inspect roller bearings; tighten or replace loose components; service drive mechanism

#### Guide Shoe Marks

- **Appearance**: Fine scratches parallel to the direction of film travel (longitudinal scratches)
- **Cause**: Guide shoes (curved metal plates that redirect film around the roller rack) have burrs or misalignment that scratch the film surface as it passes
- **Key feature**: Parallel to direction of travel (unlike pi lines and roller marks which are perpendicular)
- **Action**: Inspect and smooth guide shoes; replace damaged guide shoes

#### Pressure Artefacts from Rollers

- **Appearance**: Localised density changes at roller contact points
- **Cause**: Excessive roller pressure from spring tension set too high; damaged roller surface creating point pressure
- **Action**: Check and adjust roller tension; replace damaged rollers

### 4.4 Temperature-Related Processing Artefacts

#### Overdevelopment

- **Appearance**: Dark, dense image with reduced contrast
- **Cause**: Developer temperature too high; development time too long; developer concentration too high
- **Effect on sensitometry**: Elevated speed; elevated Dmax; elevated fog; altered gradient
- **Action**: Check and correct developer temperature; verify replenishment and solution concentration

#### Underdevelopment

- **Appearance**: Light, low-density image with reduced contrast; appears similar to underexposure
- **Cause**: Developer temperature too low; developer exhaustion; insufficient replenishment
- **Distinction from underexposure**: Underdevelopment affects all films processed simultaneously; underexposure affects only the specific examination

**Key clinical rule:**
> If multiple consecutive films appear identically light or dark without change in technique, suspect a **processing fault**. If only one film is affected, suspect an **exposure error**.

#### Uneven Temperature

- **Appearance**: Patchy, non-uniform density variation across the film
- **Cause**: Inconsistent developer temperature due to recirculation pump failure; temperature sensor failure; heater element failure
- **Action**: Service processor heating and recirculation system; verify temperature uniformity across tank

## 5. Exposure and Geometry Artefacts

### 5.1 Double Exposure

- **Appearance**: Two superimposed images on the same film, typically with one at a different density
- **Cause**: Same cassette used for two exposures without removing and replacing the film
- **Clinical risk**: May mimic complex pathology or conceal findings under superimposed anatomy
- **Prevention**: Mark cassettes immediately after exposure; implement systematic cassette workflow; use single-use film identification systems

### 5.2 Underexposure and Overexposure

| Parameter | Underexposure | Overexposure |
|---|---|---|
| Appearance | Light, pale image; increased noise/graininess | Dark, dense image |
| Contrast | Reduced (operating below film shoulder) | Reduced (operating above film shoulder) |
| Detail visibility | Fine detail obscured by noise | Fine detail obscured by density |
| Cause | Insufficient mAs or kVp; AEC failure | Excessive mAs or kVp; AEC failure |
| Corrective action | Increase mAs (and/or kVp if needed) | Reduce mAs |

Both represent exposure errors, not processing or handling errors. They are not true artefacts in the strictest sense but are included in this classification because they degrade diagnostic quality in ways that may be confused with processing problems.

### 5.3 Grid Artefacts

#### Grid Lines (Stationary Grid)

- **Appearance**: Fine, regularly spaced white lines across the image, perpendicular to the lead strips
- **Cause**: Grid used without Bucky (oscillating) mechanism; stationary grid with fine grid frequency
- **Grid frequency effect**: High-frequency grids (more than 40 lines/cm) produce less visible lines; low-frequency grids may produce very obvious lines
- **Prevention**: Always use Bucky mechanism for table and wall examinations; use high-frequency grids if stationary grid is required

#### Grid Cut-Off

Grid cut-off occurs when the primary beam enters the grid at an angle outside its designed focal range, causing the lead strips to absorb primary beam photons:

| Type | Appearance | Cause |
|---|---|---|
| **Peripheral cut-off** | Bilateral density loss at image edges | Incorrect SID (too short or too long for focused grid) |
| **Unilateral cut-off** | Density loss on one side only | Lateral tube angulation with focused grid; off-centre positioning |
| **Central cut-off** | Density loss in image centre | Grid inverted (upside down) |
| **Complete cut-off** | Severe density loss throughout | Extreme mis-alignment; grid inverted and off-centre |

:::warning Grid Inversion
Grid inversion (placing the focused grid upside down) produces a characteristic pattern with maximum density in the centre and severe density loss bilaterally. This pattern is sometimes confused with patient anatomy — always check grid orientation before exposure.
:::

#### Moiré Pattern

- **Appearance**: Broad dark and light banding pattern across the image, resembling interference fringes
- **Cause**: In digital systems, the grid frequency interacts with the detector pixel frequency to produce a beat frequency — visible as broad banding (Moiré effect)
- **Note**: This artefact is more relevant to digital systems than screen-film, but may occur when a digital detector is used with a grid designed for screen-film frequency matching

### 5.4 Collimation Artefacts

#### Collimator Cutoff

- **Appearance**: Straight white border(s) cutting across the expected image area
- **Cause**: Collimator blades positioned within the image field; field size too small for the anatomy
- **Significance**: May obscure peripheral anatomy; indicates failure to include all required structures

#### Collimator Misalignment

- **Appearance**: The primary beam field is not centred on the cassette; anatomy is eccentric within the image
- **Cause**: Poor centering of tube to image receptor; tube and Bucky alignment error
- **Action**: Verify tube-Bucky alignment; recalibrate if persistent

### 5.5 Patient Motion Artefacts

- **Appearance**: Blurred, indistinct image margins; "ghost" double edges on high-contrast structures
- **Cause**: Patient movement during exposure; cardiac motion (heart border); respiratory motion (lung fields)
- **Prevention**: Patient instruction; immobilisation; shortest possible exposure time; high mA station
- **Identification**: Motion blur is most obvious at high-contrast interfaces (bone-soft tissue, lung-pleura) and has a characteristic directional blur component

## 6. Artefact Identification: A Systematic Approach

When a suspect artefact is identified on a clinical image, a systematic approach determines its source and appropriate management:

### 6.1 Step-by-Step Diagnostic Framework

**Step 1 — Is it an artefact or true anatomy?**
- Does the density/pattern follow anatomical distribution?
- Is it present in other projections of the same anatomy?
- Does it respect tissue planes?
- Artefacts typically do not follow anatomical distributions

**Step 2 — Where does it appear on the film?**
- **Whole film uniformly affected** → likely fogging (radiation, chemical) or processing chemistry error
- **Edge/margin only** → likely light fog from cassette seal; grid cut-off; collimation
- **Fixed position, reproduces on all images from this cassette** → screen artefact (scratch, debris)
- **Varies between images** → loose screen debris; static (variable); handling artefact

**Step 3 — What is its character?**

| Pattern | Likely Source |
|---|---|
| Branching dark lines | Static electricity |
| Crescentic dark mark | Fingernail pressure mark |
| Fine lines perpendicular to travel | Pi lines (processor roller) |
| Fine lines parallel to travel | Guide shoe scratches |
| Lines parallel to leading edge | Hesitation marks |
| White lines, same position every time | Screen scratch |
| White spots, may vary | Screen debris |
| Uniform density increase | Fog (light, radiation, chemical, age) |
| Sharp horizontal density step | Developer cutoff |
| Two-colour appearance | Dichroic fog |
| Yellow-brown stain | Fixer retention |

**Step 4 — Does it affect the diagnostic area?**
- **Outside anatomy of interest**: Document; do not repeat
- **At edge of anatomy**: Determine if diagnostic question can still be answered
- **Within anatomy of interest**: Repeat exposure if artefact cannot be excluded as pathology

**Step 5 — Identify and correct the source**
- Log the artefact and its identification in the QC record
- Take corrective action before the cassette or processor is returned to clinical use
- Verify correction by repeating the QC test

### 6.2 Distinguishing Reversible from Irreversible Artefacts

| Artefact | Reversible? | Action |
|---|---|---|
| Screen debris (loose) | Yes | Clean screen; re-image |
| Screen scratch | No | Replace screen |
| Cassette light leak (fog) | Yes | Repair cassette seal; re-expose |
| Static marks | N/A | Already developed; prevent recurrence |
| Pressure/fingernail marks | No | Already developed; prevent recurrence |
| Developer cutoff | No | Already processed; correct processor; re-expose |
| Pi lines | No | Already processed; clean roller; re-expose |
| Double exposure | No | Re-expose |
| Poor contact blur | Yes (partial) | Service cassette; re-image |

## 7. Quality Assurance Programme for Artefact Prevention

### 7.1 Daily Checks

| Check | Method | Action Threshold |
|---|---|---|
| Processor sensitometry | Expose sensitometric strip; measure Dmax, Dmin, speed, contrast | Any parameter outside ±0.15 OD of control |
| Processor cleanliness | Visual inspection of feed tray and rollers | Visible contamination |
| Darkroom light integrity | Visual inspection; door-gap check | Any visible light leak |
| Safelight integrity | Visual inspection of filter condition | Any cracked, faded, or incorrect filter |

### 7.2 Weekly Checks

| Check | Method | Action |
|---|---|---|
| Crossover roller cleaning | Remove and clean crossover rollers | As per manufacturer protocol |
| Screen cleaning | Clean all cassettes with approved cleaner | Remove all debris |
| Fixer retention test | Hypo estimator test on processed film | Residual fixer above 5 micrograms/cm² |
| Processor temperature check | Measure developer and fixer temperature | Outside ±0.5°C of setpoint |

### 7.3 Monthly Checks

| Check | Method | Action |
|---|---|---|
| Screen-film contact test | Wire mesh test on all cassettes | Blurring on any cassette — remove from service |
| Cassette integrity check | Light seal test; visual inspection | Any light leakage — repair or replace |
| Safelight test | Coin/sensitometric test | Fog greater than 0.05 OD — investigate safelight |
| Developer replenishment rate | Measure volume added per film processed | Deviate more than 10% from specification |

### 7.4 Annual Checks

| Check | Method | Action |
|---|---|---|
| Full processor preventive maintenance | Engineer service; replace worn rollers/bearings | Per manufacturer schedule |
| Screen replacement assessment | Evaluate scratches, stains, contact | Replace screens older than 5 years or with defects |
| Film storage audit | Check storage conditions; rotate stock | Out-of-specification storage — review film inventory |
| Darkroom full inspection | Professional light-leak survey | Any leak — repair before clinical use |

### Key Points

- **Radiographic artefacts** are non-anatomical densities or patterns that may simulate pathology, obscure genuine findings, or degrade image quality
- **Film artefacts** (fogging, static, pressure, handling) arise from improper storage, handling, or darkroom conditions; most are preventable by environmental control and careful technique
- **Screen artefacts** (scratches, debris, poor contact) are often reproducible across multiple images and identified by their fixed position; scratches require screen replacement
- **Screen-film contact failure** causes localised blurring detected by the wire mesh test; warped cassettes and worn foam padding are the principal causes
- **Processing artefacts** are either chemical (fog, cutoff, dichroic fog, fixer retention) or mechanical (pi lines, hesitation marks, guide shoe marks); chemical artefacts typically affect all concurrently processed films
- **Pi lines** are uniquely diagnostic — their spacing equals π times the roller diameter, allowing identification of the specific causative roller
- **The key rule for processing vs. exposure errors**: If multiple consecutive films are identically affected, suspect a processing fault; if only one film is affected, suspect an exposure error
- **Grid artefacts** include visible grid lines (stationary grid), cut-off (misalignment), and central cut-off (grid inversion); always verify grid orientation before exposure
- **A systematic artefact identification framework** (whole film vs. localised; fixed vs. variable; character of pattern) enables rapid source determination and appropriate corrective action
- **A structured QA programme** with daily sensitometry, weekly screen cleaning, monthly contact testing, and annual preventive maintenance is the foundation of artefact-free screen-film practice

</div>
