# Concepts of Radiographic Image Quality

Radiographic image quality determines how accurately an image represents the anatomy being examined. High-quality images allow confident diagnosis while minimising the need for repeat exposures and additional patient dose.

## Four Primary Image Quality Factors

### 1. Density (Brightness)
The overall degree of darkening on a radiographic image.

- **Film radiography**: Optical density (OD) — logarithm of light opacity
- **Digital radiography**: Brightness/exposure index
- **Controlled by**: mAs (primary), kVp (secondary), SID, patient thickness

**Optimal density**: Sufficient to visualise all relevant anatomy without being too light or too dark.

### 2. Contrast
The difference in density between adjacent areas of the image.

**Types of contrast**:

| Type | Definition |
|---|---|
| Subject contrast | Difference in X-ray transmission through different tissues |
| Film contrast | Response of film emulsion to different exposures |
| Image contrast | Overall visible difference on the final image |

**Factors affecting contrast**:
- **kVp**: Lower kVp → higher contrast (more photoelectric effect)
- **Scatter radiation**: Reduces contrast (fogs the image)
- **Collimation**: Reducing field size decreases scatter → improves contrast
- **Contrast media**: Iodine and barium increase subject contrast

### 3. Spatial Resolution (Detail/Sharpness)
The ability to record fine detail as separate, distinct structures.

**Measured by**: Line pairs per millimeter (lp/mm)

**Factors affecting resolution**:

| Factor | Effect |
|---|---|
| Focal spot size | Smaller → sharper (less geometric blur) |
| OID (object-image distance) | Smaller → sharper |
| SID (source-image distance) | Larger → sharper |
| Motion | Movement → blur |
| Receptor resolution | Higher resolution → more detail |

### 4. Noise
Random fluctuations that reduce image quality and mimic pathology.

**Sources of noise**:
- **Quantum mottle**: Statistical variation in photon numbers (dominant at low mAs)
- **Film graininess**: Silver halide crystal distribution
- **Electronic noise**: In digital systems

**Reducing noise**: Increase mAs (more photons), use higher-speed receptor systems carefully.

## Geometric Factors

### Magnification
All radiographic images are magnified to some degree:

**Magnification factor (MF) = SID / SOD**

- Minimise by: decreasing OID, increasing SID

### Distortion
Misrepresentation of the true size or shape of a structure:
- **Size distortion**: Magnification (always enlargement in radiography)
- **Shape distortion**: Foreshortening or elongation due to beam/object/receptor misalignment

## Receptor Characteristics

### Speed (Sensitivity)
- Higher speed → less radiation required → lower dose
- Trade-off: higher speed → more noise (quantum mottle)

### Dynamic Range
- Range of exposures over which the receptor responds
- Digital systems have much wider dynamic range than film

## Quality Assurance

Regular testing maintains consistent image quality:
- Sensitometry (film systems)
- Exposure index monitoring (digital systems)
- Spatial resolution test tools (bar patterns)
- Contrast-detail phantoms
