# Screen-Film Radiographic Technique

Radiographic technique refers to the selection of exposure factors to produce a diagnostic quality image with minimum patient dose. In screen-film radiography, technique selection is critical because the system has limited exposure latitude.

## Exposure Factors

### kVp (Kilovoltage Peak)
Controls the **quality** (penetrating power) of the X-ray beam:

- Higher kVp → more penetration → lower contrast → wider latitude
- Lower kVp → less penetration → higher contrast → narrower latitude
- **15% rule**: Increasing kVp by 15% is equivalent to doubling mAs (halving mAs compensates)

**Optimal kVp ranges**:

| Body Part | Approximate kVp |
|---|---|
| Extremities (fingers, toes) | 50–60 kVp |
| Chest | 110–125 kVp |
| Abdomen / pelvis | 70–80 kVp |
| Lumbar spine | 75–85 kVp |
| Skull | 70–80 kVp |

### mAs (Milliampere-Seconds)
Controls the **quantity** of X-rays (number of photons):

**mAs = mA × time (seconds)**

- Directly controls film density
- Doubling mAs doubles film density
- **Reciprocity law**: Same mAs produces same density regardless of mA/time combination (within limits)

### Exposure Time
- Short times reduce motion blur
- Minimum time recommended for uncooperative patients and chest imaging
- Limited by tube heat capacity at high mA settings

## Technique Charts

Standardised exposure settings for each projection and body part:

**Types**:
- **Fixed kVp chart**: kVp fixed per body region; mAs adjusted for patient thickness
- **Variable kVp chart**: Both kVp and mAs vary with patient thickness (less common)

**Advantages of fixed kVp**:
- Consistent contrast
- Wider exposure latitude
- Lower patient dose

## Patient Thickness and Technique Adjustment

For every 4–5 cm increase in part thickness, double the mAs (or increase kVp by 15%).

| Part Thickness Change | mAs Adjustment |
|---|---|
| +4 cm | ×2 mAs |
| -4 cm | ÷2 mAs |
| +8 cm | ×4 mAs |

## Source-to-Image Distance (SID) Effect

Changing SID affects density; compensate using the **inverse square law**:

**New mAs = Old mAs × (New SID)² / (Old SID)²**

Example: Moving from 100 cm to 150 cm SID requires 2.25× more mAs.

## Automatic Exposure Control (AEC)

AEC (phototimer) automatically terminates the exposure when the detector receives sufficient radiation:

- Detector cells positioned behind patient at the image receptor
- Exposure continues until preset density is reached
- Radiographer selects: kVp, mA, detector cells, and density adjustment
- Ensures consistent density regardless of patient size variation

**AEC cells**:
- Typically 3 cells (left, centre, right)
- Select cell(s) under the area of clinical interest
- Avoid positioning bowel gas or air over selected cell

## Pathology and Technique Modification

Certain pathological conditions require technique adjustments:

| Condition | Effect | Technique Change |
|---|---|---|
| Pneumonia / consolidation | Increased density | Increase mAs or kVp |
| Emphysema / osteoporosis | Decreased density | Decrease mAs or kVp |
| Ascites / oedema | Increased density | Increase mAs or kVp |
| Bowel obstruction (gas) | Decreased density | Decrease mAs |
| Pleural effusion | Increased density | Increase kVp |

## Common Exposure Errors and Remedies

| Error | Cause | Remedy |
|---|---|---|
| Film too dark (overexposed) | Excessive mAs or kVp | Reduce mAs by 30–50% |
| Film too light (underexposed) | Insufficient mAs or kVp | Double mAs |
| Loss of contrast | kVp too high, excessive scatter | Reduce kVp, add grid/collimate |
| Motion blur | Long exposure time | Increase mA, reduce time |
| Poor detail | Large focal spot, large OID | Use small focal spot, reduce OID |
