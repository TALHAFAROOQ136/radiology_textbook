# Screen-Film Radiography

Screen-film radiography was the dominant imaging technology for most of the 20th century. Understanding its principles remains important for radiologic science, as many concepts directly apply to modern digital systems.

## The Screen-Film System

A screen-film system consists of two components working together:

### 1. Intensifying Screens
Phosphor-coated sheets that convert X-ray photons into visible light:

**How screens work**:
1. X-ray photon absorbed by phosphor crystal
2. Phosphor emits multiple visible light photons (luminescence)
3. Light exposes the adjacent film emulsion
4. ~95% of film exposure comes from screen light, not direct X-rays

**Phosphor materials**:

| Material | Emission Colour | Era |
|---|---|---|
| Calcium tungstate (CaWO₄) | Blue | Traditional |
| Rare earth (Gd₂O₂S, La₂O₂S) | Green or blue | Modern (higher efficiency) |

**Screen speed**:
- Determined by phosphor type, crystal size, and layer thickness
- Higher speed → less radiation needed → more quantum mottle
- Lower speed → more radiation needed → less noise, better detail

### 2. Radiographic Film
Light-sensitive material that records the image:

**Film structure**:
- **Base**: Polyester (blue-tinted) — provides support
- **Subbing layer**: Adhesive between base and emulsion
- **Emulsion**: Silver halide crystals (AgBr) in gelatin — light-sensitive layer (both sides for screen film)
- **Supercoating**: Protective gelatin layer

**Film response**:
- Light (from screens) reduces silver ions to metallic silver (latent image)
- Chemical processing makes this image visible and permanent

## Film Processing

### Automatic Processing Steps:
1. **Developer**: Converts exposed silver halide to black metallic silver
2. **Fixer**: Removes unexposed silver halide, clears the film
3. **Wash**: Removes processing chemicals
4. **Drier**: Removes water

**Processing temperature**: ~35°C; total time ~90 seconds (extended cycle) or ~45 seconds (rapid)

## The Characteristic Curve (H&D Curve)

Describes the relationship between exposure and film density:

**Regions of the curve**:
- **Toe**: Underexposure region — low density, little contrast
- **Straight-line portion**: Optimal exposure region — maximum contrast
- **Shoulder**: Overexposure region — density plateaus

**Key parameters**:
- **Base + fog density**: Minimum density (~0.2 OD)
- **Speed point**: Exposure required to reach OD of 1.0 above base+fog
- **Gamma/average gradient**: Slope of straight-line portion (film contrast)
- **Latitude**: Range of exposures producing diagnostically useful densities

## Cassettes

Film and screens are housed in a **cassette**:
- Light-tight rigid case
- Ensures close screen-film contact (poor contact → blurring)
- Front face: low attenuation material (carbon fibre or plastic)
- Back: lead foil to absorb backscatter

## Advantages and Limitations

| Aspect | Screen-Film |
|---|---|
| Resolution | High (up to 10 lp/mm with detail screens) |
| Dynamic range | Narrow (limited latitude) |
| Processing | Chemical processing required |
| Storage | Physical film storage |
| Retakes | Common due to exposure errors |
| Cost | Lower equipment cost |

## Legacy and Relevance

Although largely replaced by digital systems, screen-film principles underlie:
- Understanding of image density and contrast
- Concept of system speed and noise trade-offs
- Film viewing on lightboxes (still used in some settings)
- Historical context for ACR/RSNA standards
