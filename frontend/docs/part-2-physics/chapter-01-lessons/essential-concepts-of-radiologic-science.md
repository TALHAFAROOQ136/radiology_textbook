# Essential Concepts of Radiologic Science

Radiologic science is the discipline that applies the principles of physics, biology, and technology to the production and interpretation of medical images. Understanding its foundational concepts is essential before advancing to specific imaging modalities.

## What is Radiologic Science?

Radiologic science encompasses:
- **Diagnostic radiology**: Using imaging to diagnose disease
- **Radiation therapy**: Using radiation to treat disease
- **Nuclear medicine**: Using radioactive tracers for diagnosis and treatment
- **Radiation protection**: Minimizing unnecessary exposure to patients and staff

## Key Concepts

### Ionizing vs. Non-Ionizing Radiation
| Type | Energy | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| Ionizing | High (can remove electrons) | X-rays, gamma rays, alpha, beta |
| Non-ionizing | Low | Radiofrequency (MRI), ultrasound, visible light |

### Units of Measurement
| Quantity | SI Unit | Definition |
|---|---|---|
| Absorbed dose | Gray (Gy) | 1 J of energy per kg of tissue |
| Equivalent dose | Sievert (Sv) | Dose × radiation weighting factor |
| Effective dose | Sievert (Sv) | Accounts for tissue sensitivity |
| Activity | Becquerel (Bq) | 1 radioactive disintegration per second |

### The Radiologic Science Triad
Three core areas form the foundation:
1. **Physics** — How radiation is produced and interacts with matter
2. **Biology** — How living tissue responds to radiation
3. **Technology** — Equipment, detectors, and image processing

## Scientific Notation and Units

Radiologic science uses very small and very large numbers:
- 1 keV = 1,000 eV = 1.6 × 10⁻¹⁶ J
- 1 MeV = 1,000,000 eV
- 1 nm = 10⁻⁹ m (nanometer)
- 1 pm = 10⁻¹² m (picometer, scale of X-ray wavelengths)

## ALARA Principle

**As Low As Reasonably Achievable** — the guiding principle of radiation protection:
- Use the minimum dose necessary to obtain diagnostic-quality images
- Balance image quality against radiation risk
- Apply to patients, staff, and the public
