# The Structure of Matter

Understanding the structure of matter at the atomic and subatomic level is fundamental to understanding how radiation interacts with tissue and how medical images are formed.

## The Atom

All matter is composed of atoms. An atom consists of:

### Nucleus
- Contains **protons** (positive charge) and **neutrons** (no charge)
- Protons determine the element (atomic number Z)
- Protons + neutrons = mass number (A)
- Diameter: ~10⁻¹⁵ m (femtometers)

### Electron Shells
- **Electrons** (negative charge) orbit the nucleus in discrete energy shells
- Shells designated: K, L, M, N, O, P (innermost to outermost)
- Maximum electrons per shell: 2n² (K=2, L=8, M=18...)
- **Binding energy**: Energy required to remove an electron from its shell

| Shell | Binding Energy (tungsten) |
|---|---|
| K | ~70 keV |
| L | ~11 keV |
| M | ~2.5 keV |

## Key Atomic Properties

### Atomic Number (Z)
- Number of protons in the nucleus
- Determines the chemical element
- Higher Z → greater X-ray attenuation (photoelectric effect ∝ Z³)

### Mass Number (A)
- Total number of protons + neutrons
- Approximately equal to atomic mass in amu

### Isotopes
- Atoms of the same element with different numbers of neutrons
- Same Z, different A
- Some isotopes are unstable (**radioisotopes**) and undergo radioactive decay
- Examples used in nuclear medicine: ⁹⁹ᵐTc, ¹⁸F, ¹³¹I

## States of Matter

| State | Properties | Radiologic Relevance |
|---|---|---|
| Solid | Fixed shape, high density | Bone, contrast media |
| Liquid | Fixed volume, flows | Blood, soft tissue (~water) |
| Gas | Expands to fill space | Lung air, bowel gas |
| Plasma | Ionized gas | X-ray tube (electron stream) |

## Molecules and Compounds

- **Molecule**: Two or more atoms bonded together
- **Water (H₂O)**: Most abundant molecule in the human body (~60% by mass)
- Tissue density and atomic composition determine attenuation of X-rays

## Nuclear Forces and Stability

The nucleus is held together by the **strong nuclear force**, which overcomes electrostatic repulsion between protons. When the balance of neutrons to protons is disrupted, the nucleus becomes unstable and undergoes **radioactive decay**:

| Decay Type | Emission | Change |
|---|---|---|
| Alpha (α) | ⁴He nucleus | Z−2, A−4 |
| Beta⁻ (β⁻) | Electron + antineutrino | Z+1, A unchanged |
| Beta⁺ (β⁺) | Positron + neutrino | Z−1, A unchanged |
| Gamma (γ) | High-energy photon | No change in Z or A |
| Electron capture | Orbital electron absorbed | Z−1, A unchanged |
