# The Structure of Matter

All matter — from the lead in a protective apron to the hydrogen in soft tissue — is composed of atoms. Understanding the internal architecture of the atom, the forces that govern nuclear stability, and the behaviour of electrons is not merely academic: it is the mechanistic foundation for every interaction between radiation and the human body. This chapter builds that foundation systematically, from the subatomic particle to the molecule and state of matter.

## The Atom — An Overview

The **atom** is the smallest unit of an element that retains the chemical properties of that element. All matter in the universe is composed of atoms, and approximately **118 elements** have been identified — 94 occurring naturally and the remainder synthesised artificially.

The atom consists of two fundamental regions:

1. **The nucleus** — a dense central core containing protons and neutrons (collectively called **nucleons**)
2. **The electron cloud** — a diffuse region surrounding the nucleus in which electrons occupy discrete energy levels

<div className="book-definition">

**An atom** is the smallest structural unit of an element, comprising a positively charged nucleus (containing protons and neutrons) surrounded by negatively charged electrons arranged in discrete orbital shells. The atom is electrically neutral in its ground state because the number of electrons equals the number of protons.

</div>

### Scale of the Atom

The extreme smallness of atomic structures is difficult to appreciate without reference points:

| Component | Approximate Diameter | Relative Scale |
|---|---|---|
| Atom | ~10⁻¹⁰ m (1 Å = 0.1 nm) | Reference |
| Nucleus | ~10⁻¹⁵ m (1 fm) | 100,000× smaller than atom |
| Proton / Neutron | ~10⁻¹⁵ m | Similar to nucleus |
| Electron | &lt;10⁻¹⁸ m (point-like) | &lt;1/1000 of nucleus |

If the nucleus were enlarged to the size of a marble (1 cm), the atom would be approximately **100 metres** in diameter — the rest is essentially empty space through which the electrons move.

## The Nucleus

### Protons

The **proton** is a positively charged particle found in the nucleus:

- Charge: **+1** (equal and opposite to the electron)
- Rest mass: **1.6726 × 10⁻²⁷ kg** ≈ 1 atomic mass unit (amu)
- The number of protons in the nucleus is the **atomic number (Z)**, which uniquely defines the element

### Neutrons

The **neutron** is an electrically neutral particle found in the nucleus:

- Charge: **0**
- Rest mass: **1.6749 × 10⁻²⁷ kg** ≈ 1 amu (slightly heavier than the proton)
- Neutrons provide nuclear stability by diluting proton–proton electrostatic repulsion
- Outside the nucleus, free neutrons are unstable, with a half-life of approximately **10.3 minutes**, decaying into a proton, electron, and antineutrino

### Nuclear Notation

The standard nuclear notation for any nuclide is:

**ᴬ_Z X** &nbsp;(mass number A, atomic number Z, element symbol X)

Where:
- **X** = chemical symbol of the element
- **Z** = atomic number (number of protons)
- **A** = mass number (total nucleons = protons + neutrons)
- **N** = neutron number = A − Z

**Examples:**

| Notation | Element | Z | A | N | Clinical Relevance |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| ¹H | Hydrogen | 1 | 1 | 0 | Basis of MRI signal |
| ¹²C | Carbon | 6 | 12 | 6 | Major body constituent |
| ⁴⁰Ca | Calcium | 20 | 40 | 20 | Bone mineralisation |
| ⁷⁴W (tungsten) | Tungsten | 74 | 184 | 110 | X-ray tube anode |
| ⁵⁶Ba | Barium | 56 | 137 | 81 | Oral contrast agent |
| ¹²⁷I | Iodine | 53 | 127 | 74 | IV contrast; thyroid imaging |

### Nuclear Forces

Two opposing forces govern nuclear stability:

**1. Electrostatic (Coulomb) repulsion**
- Acts between positively charged protons
- Long-range force; tends to destabilise the nucleus
- Becomes dominant as Z increases (more protons → greater repulsion)

**2. Strong nuclear force**
- Acts between all nucleons (proton–proton, proton–neutron, neutron–neutron)
- Approximately **100× stronger** than electrostatic repulsion at nuclear distances
- Very short range (~10⁻¹⁵ m); does not extend beyond the nucleus
- Holds the nucleus together despite proton repulsion

<div className="book-keypoint">

The stability of the nucleus depends on the **neutron-to-proton (N/Z) ratio**. For light elements (Z ≤ 20), stability requires N ≈ Z (ratio ~1:1). For heavier elements, more neutrons are needed to counteract increasing proton repulsion — in bismuth (Z = 83), N/Z ≈ 1.52. When the N/Z ratio deviates from the stable range, the nucleus undergoes **radioactive decay** to regain stability — the physical basis of nuclear medicine and radiation oncology.

</div>

### Nuclear Binding Energy

The mass of a nucleus is always **less than** the sum of its constituent protons and neutrons — the difference is called the **mass defect (Δm)**. This mass is converted into energy (the nuclear **binding energy**) according to Einstein's equation:

**E = Δm × c²**

- c = speed of light = 3 × 10⁸ m/s
- Higher binding energy per nucleon = more stable nucleus
- **Iron-56 (⁵⁶Fe)** has the highest binding energy per nucleon (~8.8 MeV) and is the most stable naturally occurring nucleus

## Atomic Number, Mass Number, and Atomic Mass

### Atomic Number (Z)

- **Definition**: The number of protons in the nucleus
- **Significance**: Determines the chemical element — each element has a unique Z
- **Radiologic importance**: The photoelectric effect is proportional to **Z³** — elements with high Z (iodine Z=53, barium Z=56, lead Z=82) are therefore excellent X-ray attenuators and contrast agents

### Mass Number (A)

- **Definition**: The total number of nucleons (protons + neutrons)
- Always a **whole number**
- Approximately equal to atomic mass in amu

### Atomic Mass Unit (amu)

- 1 amu = 1/12 the mass of a carbon-12 atom = **1.6605 × 10⁻²⁷ kg**
- Proton mass ≈ 1.007 amu
- Neutron mass ≈ 1.009 amu
- Electron mass ≈ 0.000549 amu (~1/1836 of proton mass)

### Atomic Weight (Relative Atomic Mass)

The **atomic weight** is the weighted average mass of all naturally occurring isotopes of an element, expressed in amu. For example:

- Chlorine has two stable isotopes: ³⁵Cl (75.77%) and ³⁷Cl (24.23%)
- Atomic weight of Cl = (35 × 0.7577) + (37 × 0.2423) = **35.45 amu**

## Electron Configuration and Orbital Shells

### Electron Properties

- **Charge**: −1 (equal and opposite to the proton)
- **Mass**: 9.109 × 10⁻³¹ kg (approximately 1/1836 the mass of a proton)
- The number of electrons in a neutral atom equals the number of protons (Z)

### Orbital Shells and Quantum Numbers

Electrons do not orbit the nucleus in fixed circular paths (as Bohr initially proposed), but occupy **discrete quantum energy levels** called shells or orbitals. These shells are designated by letters and principal quantum numbers:

| Shell | Principal Quantum Number (n) | Maximum Electrons (2n²) |
|---|---|---|
| K | 1 | 2 |
| L | 2 | 8 |
| M | 3 | 18 |
| N | 4 | 32 |
| O | 5 | 50 |
| P | 6 | 72 |

**Key rules:**
- The **K shell** is closest to the nucleus and has the **highest binding energy**
- Electrons fill from the innermost shell outward (lowest energy first)
- The **outermost occupied shell** is the **valence shell** — determines chemical bonding
- A full outermost shell (noble gas configuration) confers maximum stability

**Example — Tungsten (W, Z = 74):**

| Shell | Electrons |
|---|---|
| K | 2 |
| L | 8 |
| M | 18 |
| N | 32 |
| O | 12 |
| P | 2 |

### Electron Binding Energy

**Binding energy (E_b)** is the energy required to completely remove an electron from its shell to infinity. It represents the strength with which the electron is held by the nucleus.

Key relationships:
- **Innermost shells have the highest binding energy** (closest to nucleus, strongest attraction)
- Binding energy **increases with atomic number Z** (more protons = stronger attraction)
- Binding energy values are unique to each element — they serve as an **atomic fingerprint**

**Binding energies for clinically important elements:**

| Element (Z) | K-shell (keV) | L-shell (keV) | M-shell (keV) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Carbon (6) | 0.28 | — | — |
| Calcium (20) | 4.0 | 0.44 | 0.04 |
| Iodine (53) | 33.2 | 5.2 | 0.62 |
| Barium (56) | 37.4 | 5.99 | 0.80 |
| Tungsten (74) | 69.5 | 11.5 | 2.52 |
| Lead (82) | 88.0 | 15.9 | 3.85 |

<div className="book-keypoint">

The **K-edge** of iodine is 33.2 keV — the energy at which a K-shell electron can just be ejected. At photon energies just above the K-edge, the **photoelectric absorption probability rises sharply**, making iodine an extremely effective contrast agent for X-ray and CT. This is why iodinated contrast agents produce such marked enhancement: the 70–120 kVp X-ray beams used in CT are ideally matched to iodine's atomic absorption characteristics.

</div>

## Isotopes, Isobars, Isotones, and Nuclear Isomers

These terms describe specific relationships between different nuclides and are essential in nuclear medicine:

### Isotopes

**Same Z, different A** — same element, different number of neutrons

- Share identical **chemical properties** (same electron configuration)
- Have different **physical properties** (different mass, different stability)
- Unstable isotopes are called **radioisotopes** or **radionuclides**

| Element | Stable Isotopes | Clinically Used Radioisotopes |
|---|---|---|
| Hydrogen | ¹H, ²H (deuterium) | ³H (tritium — research) |
| Carbon | ¹²C, ¹³C | ¹¹C (PET imaging) |
| Iodine | ¹²⁷I | ¹²³I (thyroid scan), ¹³¹I (thyroid therapy) |
| Technetium | None (all radioactive) | ⁹⁹ᵐTc (most widely used in nuclear medicine) |
| Fluorine | ¹⁹F | ¹⁸F (FDG-PET) |

### Isobars

**Same A, different Z** — same mass number, different elements

- Different chemical and physical properties
- Example: ¹³¹I (Z=53) and ¹³¹Xe (Z=54) — iodine-131 decays to xenon-131

### Isotones

**Same N, different Z** — same neutron number, different elements

- Less clinically relevant than isotopes or isobars
- Example: ¹⁴C (Z=6, N=8) and ¹⁵N (Z=7, N=8)

### Nuclear Isomers (Metastable States)

**Same Z, same A** — but the nucleus exists in a **higher energy (excited) state**

- Denoted by the superscript **m** (metastable)
- Decay to the ground state by emitting a **gamma ray** (isomeric transition)
- **⁹⁹ᵐTc** (technetium-99m) is the most important nuclear isomer in medicine:
  - Produced from molybdenum-99 in a radionuclide generator
  - Decays to ⁹⁹Tc by emitting a **140 keV gamma ray** — ideally suited to gamma camera detection
  - Physical half-life: **6.02 hours** — short enough to limit patient dose, long enough for imaging
  - Used in >80% of all nuclear medicine procedures worldwide

<div className="book-pearl">

**Technetium-99m** was first produced in 1938 by Carlo Perrier and Emilio Segrè — making it the first artificially produced element (its name derives from the Greek *technetos*, meaning "artificial"). Today, global nuclear medicine imaging depends on a continuous supply chain of ⁹⁸Mo (produced in nuclear reactors), which is shipped to radiopharmacies to generate ⁹⁹ᵐTc on demand. A reactor shutdown anywhere in this network can disrupt cardiac and bone scans worldwide within days.

</div>

## Ionisation and Excitation

When radiation interacts with an atom, it transfers energy to the orbital electrons. Two fundamental outcomes are possible:

### Ionisation

Ionisation occurs when **sufficient energy is transferred to completely eject an electron** from its shell, overcoming the binding energy:

**Process:**
1. Incident radiation deposits energy ≥ electron binding energy
2. Electron is ejected from the atom → **photoelectron** or **Compton electron**
3. Atom becomes a **positive ion** (lost an electron)
4. Ejected electron may be captured by a neutral atom → **negative ion**
5. The positive and negative ions together constitute an **ion pair**

**Threshold:** Ionisation requires energy **above** the binding energy of the targeted shell. For the K shell of soft tissue (primarily carbon, oxygen, hydrogen), binding energies are very low (< 0.5 keV), so even low-energy X-rays can ionise soft tissue atoms efficiently.

**Biological consequence:** Ion pairs are chemically reactive. In biological tissue, ionisation initiates a cascade:
- Direct damage to DNA and critical molecules
- Radiolysis of water → free radical production → indirect DNA damage

### Excitation

Excitation occurs when radiation transfers energy to an electron **insufficient to eject it**, raising it to a **higher energy shell**:

**Process:**
1. Incident photon deposits energy < electron binding energy of the outer shell
2. Electron jumps to a higher, temporarily vacant energy level
3. Atom enters an **excited state**
4. Electron returns to ground state, releasing energy as a **characteristic photon** (fluorescence)
5. Atom returns to normal — no permanent structural change

**Radiologic application:** Excitation is exploited in:
- **Fluorescent screens** in radiography
- **Scintillator crystals** in gamma cameras and PET scanners (e.g., NaI, BGO, LYSO)
- **Photostimulable phosphor (PSP) plates** in computed radiography (CR)

<div className="book-definition">

**Ionisation** is the process of completely removing an electron from an atom, producing an ion pair. **Excitation** is the process of elevating an electron to a higher energy level without ejection. Ionisation requires more energy and causes direct atomic structural change; excitation is transient and reversible. Both processes are mechanisms by which radiation deposits energy in matter.

</div>

## Radioactive Decay

When the neutron-to-proton ratio falls outside the stability range, the nucleus is **radioactive** — it will spontaneously transform (decay) to achieve a more stable configuration. This transformation is always accompanied by the emission of radiation.

### Modes of Radioactive Decay

#### 1. Alpha (α) Decay

**What is emitted:** An alpha particle = ²He nucleus (2 protons + 2 neutrons)

**Nuclear change:** Z decreases by 2; A decreases by 4

**ᴬ_Z X → ᴬ⁻⁴_(Z−2) Y + ⁴_₂α**

**Properties of alpha particles:**
- Charge: **+2**
- Mass: **4 amu** — heaviest decay product
- Kinetic energy: typically 4–9 MeV (discrete, monoenergetic)
- Range in air: **2–10 cm**; in tissue: **20–100 μm** (stopped by epidermis)
- Cannot penetrate a sheet of paper

**Occurs in:** Heavy nuclei (Z > 82), including radium-226, radon-222, uranium-238, polonium-210

**Medical relevance:**
- External hazard: minimal (stopped by clothing/skin)
- **Internal hazard: extremely high** — if inhaled or ingested, alpha emitters deposit enormous energy in a very small tissue volume (high linear energy transfer, LET ~100 keV/μm)
- **Radon gas** (alpha emitter) is the second leading cause of lung cancer after smoking when inhaled
- **Targeted alpha therapy (TAT)**: Emerging cancer treatment using alpha-emitting radionuclides (e.g., ²²³Ra for bone metastases) — the short range confines cytotoxic dose to the tumour

#### 2. Beta-minus (β⁻) Decay

**What is emitted:** An electron (β⁻ particle) and an antineutrino (v̄)

**Nuclear change:** A neutron converts to a proton: Z increases by 1; A unchanged

**ᴬ_Z X → ᴬ_(Z+1) Y + β⁻ + v̄**

**Properties of beta-minus particles:**
- Charge: **−1**
- Mass: **0.000549 amu**
- Energy: **continuous spectrum** from 0 to maximum (E_max) — shared with antineutrino
- Range in tissue: **up to several mm** depending on energy
- Can be stopped by **plastic, glass, or a few mm of aluminium**

**Occurs when:** Neutron excess → N/Z ratio too high (nucleus too neutron-rich)

**Clinical examples:**

| Nuclide | E_max | Half-life | Medical Use |
|---|---|---|---|
| ¹³¹I | 0.61 MeV | 8.02 days | Thyroid ablation, differentiated thyroid cancer therapy |
| ¹⁸⁹Re | 2.12 MeV | 16.9 h | Radionuclide therapy |
| ⁸⁹Sr | 1.49 MeV | 50.5 days | Bone pain palliation |
| ¹⁴C | 0.16 MeV | 5,730 years | Carbon dating; metabolic research |

#### 3. Beta-plus (β⁺) Decay / Positron Emission

**What is emitted:** A positron (β⁺ particle) and a neutrino (ν)

**Nuclear change:** A proton converts to a neutron: Z decreases by 1; A unchanged

**ᴬ_Z X → ᴬ_(Z−1) Y + β⁺ + ν**

**Annihilation radiation:**
- The positron travels a short distance in tissue (< 1–2 mm)
- It rapidly encounters an electron → **annihilation**
- Both particles are converted to **two 511 keV gamma rays** emitted at **180°** to each other
- These paired photons are the basis of **PET (Positron Emission Tomography)**

**Clinical PET radionuclides:**

| Nuclide | Half-life | Production | Common Tracer | Application |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| ¹⁸F | 109.8 min | Cyclotron | ¹⁸F-FDG | Oncology, neurology, cardiology |
| ¹¹C | 20.4 min | Cyclotron | ¹¹C-PIB | Amyloid imaging (Alzheimer's) |
| ¹³N | 10.0 min | Cyclotron | ¹³N-ammonia | Myocardial perfusion |
| ¹⁵O | 2.0 min | Cyclotron | ¹⁵O-water | Cerebral blood flow |
| ⁶⁸Ga | 68.0 min | Generator | ⁶⁸Ga-PSMA | Prostate cancer staging |
| ⁸²Rb | 75.0 s | Generator | ⁸²Rb-chloride | Myocardial perfusion |

<div className="book-keypoint">

The 511 keV **annihilation photons** produced by positron decay are clinically exploited in PET imaging. Because both photons travel in exactly opposite directions (180°), PET cameras detect them in **coincidence** — both photons must arrive at opposing detectors within a narrow time window (~5 ns). This coincidence detection eliminates the need for a lead collimator, allowing PET scanners to achieve much higher sensitivity than conventional gamma cameras, but also explains why PET requires a separate ring detector design.

</div>

#### 4. Electron Capture (EC)

**What happens:** The nucleus captures an **inner orbital electron** (usually K-shell), which combines with a proton to form a neutron and a neutrino

**Nuclear change:** Z decreases by 1; A unchanged

**p⁺ + e⁻ → n + ν**

**Radiation emitted:**
- **Characteristic X-rays** — produced when outer electrons fill the vacancy left by the captured K-electron (K-capture → K X-rays emitted)
- **Auger electrons** — alternatively, the excitation energy is transferred to another orbital electron, which is ejected
- No directly emitted beta particles

**Occurs in:** Proton-rich nuclides where positron emission is energetically prohibited (requires > 1.022 MeV) or when proton excess is modest

**Clinical example:** ¹²⁵I (iodine-125) undergoes electron capture, emitting low-energy (~27–35 keV) X-rays and gamma rays — used in **prostate brachytherapy seeds** and laboratory immunoassays

#### 5. Gamma (γ) Emission / Isomeric Transition

**What is emitted:** A high-energy electromagnetic photon (gamma ray)

**Nuclear change:** No change in Z or A — the nucleus transitions from an **excited state** to the **ground state**

- Gamma decay typically **follows** alpha or beta decay (which may leave the daughter in an excited state)
- Pure gamma emitters used in nuclear medicine are metastable isomers (e.g., ⁹⁹ᵐTc)
- Gamma rays are identical in nature to X-rays — both are electromagnetic radiation; they differ only in origin

### Summary of Decay Modes

| Decay Type | Emission | Z Change | A Change | Penetrating Power | Example |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Alpha (α) | ⁴He nucleus | −2 | −4 | Very low (cm in air) | ²²⁶Ra |
| Beta-minus (β⁻) | Electron + v̄ | +1 | 0 | Low–moderate (mm in tissue) | ¹³¹I |
| Beta-plus (β⁺) | Positron + ν | −1 | 0 | Low + 511 keV annihilation | ¹⁸F |
| Electron capture | Neutrino + characteristic X-rays | −1 | 0 | Very low (characteristic X-rays) | ¹²⁵I |
| Gamma (γ) | Photon | 0 | 0 | High (cm of lead) | ⁹⁹ᵐTc |

## Radioactive Decay — Quantitative Principles

### The Decay Constant (λ)

Radioactive decay is a **stochastic** (random) process — it is impossible to predict when any individual nucleus will decay. However, for large numbers of atoms, the **rate of decay** is predictable:

**dN/dt = −λN**

Where:
- N = number of radioactive atoms
- λ = **decay constant** (fraction of atoms decaying per unit time; s⁻¹)
- The negative sign indicates N decreases with time

### Activity (A)

**Activity** is the number of nuclear disintegrations per unit time:

**A = λN**

**SI unit:** **Becquerel (Bq)** = 1 disintegration per second
**Old unit:** Curie (Ci) = 3.7 × 10¹⁰ Bq (activity of 1 gram of radium-226)

Clinically administered activities:
- Bone scan (⁹⁹ᵐTc-MDP): ~700 MBq
- FDG-PET: ~300–370 MBq
- ¹³¹I thyroid ablation: 1,100–3,700 MBq (30–100 mCi)

### Physical Half-Life (T½)

The **physical half-life** is the time required for the activity of a radionuclide to decrease to **exactly half** its initial value:

**T½ = 0.693 / λ**

Exponential decay equation:

**A_t = A₀ × (½)^(t/T½) = A₀ × e^(−λt)**

**Half-life values for key radionuclides:**

| Nuclide | Physical T½ | Use |
|---|---|---|
| ¹⁵O | 2.0 minutes | PET cerebral blood flow |
| ¹³N | 10.0 minutes | PET myocardial perfusion |
| ¹¹C | 20.4 minutes | PET amyloid/dopamine |
| ¹⁸F | 109.8 minutes (1.83 h) | FDG-PET |
| ⁶⁸Ga | 68.0 minutes | PSMA-PET |
| ⁹⁹ᵐTc | 6.02 hours | Bone, cardiac, renal, thyroid scans |
| ¹²³I | 13.2 hours | Thyroid function/MIBG |
| ⁶⁷Ga | 3.26 days | Infection/tumour imaging |
| ¹³¹I | 8.02 days | Thyroid therapy |
| ¹¹¹In | 2.80 days | WBC scan, octreotide |
| ²²³Ra | 11.4 days | Bone metastasis therapy |
| ⁸⁹Sr | 50.5 days | Bone pain palliation |
| ¹²⁵I | 59.4 days | Brachytherapy seeds |
| ¹⁴C | 5,730 years | Carbon dating |
| ²³⁸U | 4.5 × 10⁹ years | Uranium ore |

### Effective Half-Life

In the body, a radionuclide is eliminated by two processes simultaneously:
1. **Physical decay** (T½_phys)
2. **Biological clearance** (T½_biol)

The **effective half-life** is always shorter than either component alone:

**1/T½_eff = 1/T½_phys + 1/T½_biol**

**Example — ⁹⁹ᵐTc-DTPA (renal scan):**
- T½_phys = 6.02 h
- T½_biol (renal clearance) ≈ 1.5 h
- T½_eff ≈ 1.2 h

<div className="book-remember">

**Half-life calculation shortcut:**

After **n** half-lives, the activity remaining is: **A = A₀ × (1/2)ⁿ**

| Half-lives elapsed | Fraction remaining | % remaining |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | 1/2 | 50% |
| 2 | 1/4 | 25% |
| 3 | 1/8 | 12.5% |
| 4 | 1/16 | 6.25% |
| 7 | 1/128 | ~0.78% |
| 10 | 1/1024 | ~0.1% |

A practical rule: after **10 half-lives**, activity has fallen to less than **0.1%** of the starting value — the basis for radioactive waste storage guidelines and patient isolation decisions.

</div>

## Elements, Molecules, and Compounds

### Elements

A **pure element** contains atoms of a single atomic number. Of the 118 known elements, the human body is composed primarily of four:

| Element | Symbol | Z | % Body Mass | Primary Location |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Oxygen | O | 8 | ~65% | Water, proteins, DNA |
| Carbon | C | 6 | ~18% | Organic molecules |
| Hydrogen | H | 1 | ~10% | Water, organic molecules |
| Nitrogen | N | 7 | ~3% | Proteins, nucleic acids |
| Calcium | Ca | 20 | ~1.5% | Bone, teeth |
| Phosphorus | P | 15 | ~1% | Bone, DNA, ATP |
| Other | — | — | ~1.5% | Trace elements |

### Molecules and Chemical Bonds

**Molecules** form when two or more atoms share or transfer electrons:

- **Covalent bond**: Electrons shared between atoms (e.g., H₂O, CO₂, glucose)
- **Ionic bond**: Electron transferred from one atom to another (e.g., NaCl → Na⁺ + Cl⁻)
- **Hydrogen bond**: Weak attraction between polar molecules (e.g., between water molecules; stabilises DNA double helix)

### Water — The Dominant Molecule in Radiologic Physics

Water (H₂O) has a central role in radiologic science:

- Constitutes **~60% of body mass** (~42 L in a 70 kg adult)
- **Attenuation coefficient** of soft tissue approximates that of water — hence Hounsfield unit of water = 0 HU in CT
- **Radiolysis of water** is responsible for ~70% of radiation-induced DNA damage:

**H₂O + radiation → H₂O⁺ + e⁻ → H· + OH· (hydroxyl radical)**

- The hydroxyl radical (OH·) is the primary mediator of indirect radiation injury to DNA
- Oxygen enhances free radical toxicity → **oxygen enhancement ratio (OER)** of ~2.5–3 for sparsely ionising radiation

## States of Matter

Matter exists in four states, all of which are relevant to radiologic science:

### Solid

- **Structure**: Fixed, ordered lattice; atoms/molecules vibrate in place
- **Properties**: Fixed shape, high density, relatively incompressible
- **Radiologic examples**: Bone (calcium phosphate crystals), calcifications, contrast agents (barium sulphate suspension), lead shielding

### Liquid

- **Structure**: Close proximity but disordered; atoms/molecules flow freely
- **Properties**: Fixed volume, no fixed shape, moderate density
- **Radiologic examples**: Blood (~1.05 g/cm³), soft tissue (~water density), iodinated contrast media, mercury (historical thermometers)

### Gas

- **Structure**: Wide separation; random motion; fills any container
- **Properties**: No fixed shape or volume, very low density
- **Radiologic examples**:
  - Lung air (density ~0.001 g/cm³) — appears black on chest X-ray
  - Bowel gas — normal appearance; abnormal distribution in obstruction or perforation
  - **Xenon (¹³³Xe)**: radioactive gas used in **ventilation scintigraphy** for V/Q scanning
  - Air gaps in pneumothorax, pneumoperitoneum — key diagnostic findings

### Plasma

- **Structure**: Ionised gas — electrons separated from nuclei
- **Properties**: Electrically conductive; responds to magnetic fields
- **Radiologic examples**:
  - **X-ray tube electron stream**: electrons emitted from heated cathode filament exist briefly as a plasma current accelerated toward the anode
  - Not encountered as tissue in imaging, but fundamental to X-ray production

### Summary of States and Their Radiologic Significance

| State | Density (approx.) | X-ray Attenuation | Radiologic Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| Solid (metal/bone) | 1.5–20 g/cm³ | Very high (white) | Cortical bone, barium, lead |
| Solid (soft tissue) | 1.0–1.1 g/cm³ | Moderate (grey) | Liver, spleen, muscle |
| Solid (fat) | 0.9 g/cm³ | Low (dark grey) | Subcutaneous fat, fatty liver |
| Liquid | ~1.0 g/cm³ | Moderate | Pleural effusion, ascites, contrast |
| Gas | ~0.001 g/cm³ | Very low (black) | Lung, bowel, pneumothorax |

<div className="book-important">

The four-compartment Hounsfield scale in CT directly reflects the states of matter:
- **Gas**: approximately −1000 HU (lung, free air)
- **Fat**: approximately −100 HU (adipose tissue)
- **Water/soft tissue**: 0–80 HU
- **Bone/calcium**: +400 to +1000 HU
- **Metal**: +1000 to +3000 HU (and beyond — causes streak artefacts)

This physical density difference — grounded in atomic composition and state of matter — is what gives CT its unparalleled soft tissue and bone contrast characterisation capability.

</div>

## Atomic Composition and Radiologic Tissue Properties

The X-ray attenuation of any tissue is determined by three factors, all rooted in atomic structure:

1. **Physical density (ρ)** — more atoms per cm³ = greater attenuation
2. **Atomic number (Z)** — higher Z = greater photoelectric absorption (∝ Z³)
3. **Electron density** — number of electrons per unit mass

| Tissue | Composition | Effective Z | Relative Attenuation | HU Range |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Air | N₂, O₂ | 7.6 | Very low | ~−1000 |
| Fat | C, H, O (lipid) | 5.9 | Low | −50 to −100 |
| Water | H₂O | 7.4 | Reference | 0 |
| Soft tissue | H, C, N, O | 7.4–7.6 | Moderate | 20–80 |
| Bone | Ca₃(PO₄)₂ + collagen | 13.8 | High | 200–1000 |
| Iodine contrast | Iodine-based molecule | 53 | Very high | +200 to +500 |
| Lead | Pb | 82 | Extremely high | Attenuates beam |

<div className="book-pearl">

The reason bone is so visible on X-rays is not simply density — it is the **high atomic number of calcium (Z=20)** and phosphorus (Z=15). At the photon energies used in diagnostic radiology (20–120 keV), the **photoelectric effect** dominates in high-Z materials, with absorption probability ∝ Z³. Bone (effective Z ≈ 13.8) absorbs far more photons than soft tissue (effective Z ≈ 7.4) — the ratio is (13.8/7.4)³ ≈ **6.5 times greater**. This is why even a thin cortex produces a distinct white line against the soft tissue background, and why iodinated and barium contrast agents — with even higher Z — produce such dramatic enhancement.

</div>

<div className="book-summary">

- All matter is composed of **atoms** — a dense nucleus (protons + neutrons) surrounded by electrons in discrete orbital shells. The atom is electrically neutral when the number of electrons equals the number of protons (Z).
- **Atomic number (Z)** determines the element; **mass number (A)** is the total nucleon count. The **neutron number N = A − Z** governs nuclear stability.
- **Electron binding energy** is highest in the innermost (K) shell and increases with Z. The K-edge energy of iodine (33.2 keV) explains the dramatic X-ray attenuation of iodinated contrast agents.
- **Isotopes** share the same Z but differ in A (neutron number). Radioisotopes are unstable isotopes that undergo spontaneous decay — they are the basis of nuclear medicine.
- **⁹⁹ᵐTc** (technetium-99m), a metastable nuclear isomer, is the most widely used radionuclide in nuclear medicine, emitting a pure 140 keV gamma ray with a 6-hour half-life.
- The five modes of radioactive decay are: **alpha (Z−2, A−4), beta-minus (Z+1), beta-plus/positron (Z−1), electron capture (Z−1),** and **gamma emission (no change)**. Each has distinct emissions, penetrating power, and clinical applications.
- **Physical half-life (T½)** is the time for activity to fall by 50%. After **10 half-lives**, activity is < 0.1% of its original value. **Effective half-life** (shorter than physical T½) accounts for biological clearance.
- **Ionisation** (electron ejection) is the primary mechanism by which radiation damages biological molecules. **Excitation** (electron elevation without ejection) is reversible and exploited in detector systems.
- X-ray attenuation depends on **physical density, atomic number (Z),** and **electron density**. High-Z materials (bone, iodine, barium, lead) attenuate far more than soft tissue — the physical basis of radiographic contrast.
- The four states of matter — solid, liquid, gas, and plasma — all feature in radiologic science, from bone and soft tissue imaging to gas-filled lung, Xenon ventilation studies, and X-ray tube electron physics.

</div>
