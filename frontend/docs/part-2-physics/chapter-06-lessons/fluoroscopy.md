# Fluoroscopy

Fluoroscopy is a real-time, continuous X-ray imaging technique that allows dynamic visualisation of moving structures and provides live guidance for diagnostic and interventional procedures.

## Principles of Fluoroscopy

Unlike static radiography, fluoroscopy produces a continuous or rapidly sequential series of images displayed in real time:
- X-ray tube produces continuous or pulsed beam
- Transmitted X-rays detected and converted to a visible image
- Image displayed on monitor at video frame rates (typically 25–30 frames/sec)
- Radiologist or clinician observes dynamic processes or guides instruments

## Components of a Fluoroscopic System

### X-ray Tube
- Continuous or pulsed output
- Lower mA than radiography (1–5 mA typical)
- Rotating anode with high heat capacity (long exposure times)
- Small focal spot for acceptable resolution

### Image Intensifier (II)

The traditional image detector, now largely replaced by FPDs:

**Components**:
1. **Input phosphor** (CsI): Converts X-rays to light
2. **Photocathode**: Converts light to electrons
3. **Electrostatic lenses**: Accelerate and focus electrons
4. **Output phosphor**: Converts electrons back to visible light image
5. **Optical system / video camera**: Captures and transmits image to monitor

**Minification gain**: Input screen area / output screen area (~100×)
**Flux gain**: Electron acceleration (~50×)
**Total brightness gain**: ~5,000–10,000×

### Image Intensifier Sizes
- Standard: 23 cm, 33 cm, 40 cm input diameter
- Smaller field of view (electronic magnification) → higher resolution, higher dose

### TV Camera and Monitor
- CCD or CMOS camera captures output phosphor image
- Displayed on high-resolution monitors
- Frame rate: 25 fps (PAL) or 30 fps (NTSC)

## Fluoroscopic Modes

| Mode | Description | Dose |
|---|---|---|
| Continuous fluoroscopy | Constant beam; real-time image | Highest |
| Pulsed fluoroscopy | Intermittent pulses (e.g., 7.5, 15, 30 fps) | Lower (50–75% reduction) |
| Last image hold | Displays last acquired frame | No additional dose |
| Fluorography (spot image) | High-quality still image acquired | Higher than fluoro; lower than radiograph |
| Cine acquisition | Rapid sequence of high-quality images | Highest (used in cardiac cath) |

## Radiation Dose in Fluoroscopy

Fluoroscopy delivers significantly higher doses than static radiography:

| Examination | Typical Effective Dose |
|---|---|
| Barium swallow | ~1.5 mSv |
| Barium meal | ~3 mSv |
| Barium enema | ~7 mSv |
| IVU (intravenous urography) | ~2.5 mSv |
| Cardiac catheterisation | ~7–15 mSv |
| ERCP | ~4 mSv |

**Dose area product (DAP)**: Measured in Gy·cm²; recorded for every procedure.

## Dose Reduction Strategies

- Use **pulsed fluoroscopy** (lowest pulse rate acceptable for the procedure)
- Use **last image hold** instead of continuous screening
- **Collimate** to area of interest
- Minimise **fluoroscopy time** — use pedal carefully
- Maintain maximum **SSD** (source-to-skin distance) — minimum 30 cm (mobile), 38 cm (fixed)
- Keep patient as **close to detector** as possible (reduce OID)
- Use **lower frame rates** for slow-moving structures
- Avoid **electronic magnification** unless necessary

## Fluoroscopy Time Alarm

- Mandatory 5-minute audible alarm on all fluoroscopic equipment
- Reminds operator to assess cumulative fluoroscopy time
- Does not automatically terminate the exposure

## Common Fluoroscopic Examinations

| Examination | Structure Evaluated |
|---|---|
| Barium swallow | Pharynx, oesophagus |
| Barium meal | Stomach, duodenum |
| Barium follow-through | Small bowel |
| Barium enema | Large bowel |
| Cystourethrogram (MCUG) | Bladder, urethra |
| Hysterosalpingogram (HSG) | Uterus, fallopian tubes |
| Arthrogram | Joint space |
| Myelogram | Spinal canal |

## Image Quality Factors

- **Contrast**: Affected by kVp, scatter (reduced by collimation and grid)
- **Resolution**: Limited by image intensifier/detector, focal spot, and monitor
- **Noise**: Quantum noise prominent at low dose rates
- **Lag**: Persistence of previous image (ghosting) — more pronounced in older II systems
