# Interventional Radiography

Interventional radiology (IR) uses image guidance — primarily fluoroscopy, but also ultrasound, CT, and MRI — to perform minimally invasive diagnostic and therapeutic procedures. It combines radiologic expertise with procedural skills to treat conditions that previously required open surgery.

## Principles of Image-Guided Intervention

All IR procedures rely on:
1. **Real-time imaging**: Fluoroscopy for vascular/catheter work; US/CT for needle guidance
2. **Access**: Percutaneous (through skin) or endoluminal (through body orifices)
3. **Devices**: Needles, wires, catheters, sheaths, balloons, stents, embolic agents
4. **Contrast media**: Iodinated contrast for vascular opacification

## The Seldinger Technique

Foundational technique for vascular access:
1. Puncture vessel with hollow needle
2. Pass flexible guidewire through needle into vessel
3. Remove needle over guidewire
4. Advance catheter/sheath over guidewire
5. Remove guidewire; catheter in position

## Vascular Interventional Procedures

### Angioplasty and Stenting
- **Balloon angioplasty (PTA)**: Inflatable balloon catheter dilates stenosed vessel
- **Stenting**: Metallic mesh stent deployed to maintain vessel patency
- **Applications**: Peripheral arterial disease, renal artery stenosis, iliac occlusion

### Endovascular Aneurysm Repair (EVAR)
- Stent-graft deployed inside aortic aneurysm via femoral access
- Excludes aneurysm from arterial pressure
- Less invasive than open surgical repair

### Thrombolysis and Thrombectomy
- **Catheter-directed thrombolysis**: Thrombolytic drug infused directly into clot
- **Mechanical thrombectomy**: Device mechanically removes clot
- Applications: Acute stroke, DVT, PE, arterial occlusion

### Embolisation
Deliberate occlusion of a vessel using embolic agents:

| Agent | Type | Use |
|---|---|---|
| Coils | Mechanical | Aneurysm, AVM, haemorrhage |
| Particles (PVA, microspheres) | Particulate | Tumour embolisation (UAE, TACE) |
| Liquid agents (Onyx, glue) | Liquid | AVM, fistula |
| Gelfoam | Temporary | Trauma haemorrhage |

### Transjugular Intrahepatic Portosystemic Shunt (TIPS)
- Creates shunt between portal and hepatic veins within liver
- Treats portal hypertension (variceal bleeding, refractory ascites)

## Non-Vascular Interventional Procedures

### Percutaneous Biopsy
- Image-guided (US, CT, fluoroscopy) needle biopsy
- Liver, lung, kidney, lymph node, bone
- Replaces surgical biopsy in many cases

### Percutaneous Drainage
- CT or US guided catheter placement into abscess or fluid collection
- Avoids general anaesthesia and surgery

### Biliary Intervention
- **Percutaneous transhepatic cholangiography (PTC)**: Opacify biliary tree
- **Biliary drainage (PTBD)**: Decompress obstructed biliary system
- **Biliary stenting**: Restore bile flow through malignant stricture

### Nephrostomy
- Percutaneous drainage of obstructed renal collecting system
- Placed under US/fluoroscopic guidance

### Tumour Ablation
| Technique | Mechanism |
|---|---|
| Radiofrequency ablation (RFA) | Heat via radiofrequency current |
| Microwave ablation (MWA) | Heat via microwave energy |
| Cryoablation | Freezing with argon gas |
| Irreversible electroporation (IRE) | Cell membrane disruption |

**Applications**: Liver, kidney, lung, bone metastases

## Radiation Safety in IR

IR procedures deliver the **highest radiation doses** in diagnostic radiology:

| Procedure | Typical DAP (Gy·cm²) | Effective Dose |
|---|---|---|
| Coronary angiogram | 20–50 | ~5 mSv |
| PTCA (angioplasty) | 50–100 | ~15 mSv |
| TIPS | 100–300 | ~30–70 mSv |
| Uterine artery embolisation | 50–200 | ~40–70 mSv |

### Radiation Protection in IR
- **Distance**: Operator stands as far as practical from patient
- **Shielding**: Lead apron (0.35–0.5 mm Pb), thyroid collar, lead glasses
- **Technique**: Minimise fluoroscopy time, use pulsed fluoro, last image hold
- **Positioning**: Keep FPD close to patient; maximise SSD
- **Monitoring**: Personal dosimeters (badge + ring dosimeter)

## Contrast Media in IR

### Iodinated Contrast
- Essential for vessel opacification in angiography and DSA
- Low-osmolar or iso-osmolar non-ionic agents preferred
- Risk: nephrotoxicity, anaphylaxis (rare)

### CO₂ as Contrast Agent
- Used in patients with renal impairment or iodine allergy
- Injected into vessels; CO₂ displaces blood, appears black on DSA
- Not suitable above diaphragm (risk of air embolism to cerebral/coronary vessels)
