# X-ray Production

X-rays are produced when high-speed electrons are suddenly decelerated upon striking a metal target. Two distinct mechanisms are responsible for X-ray production in a diagnostic X-ray tube.

## Prerequisites for X-ray Production

Three conditions must be met:
1. **A source of electrons** — tungsten filament (thermionic emission)
2. **High voltage** — to accelerate electrons (kVp)
3. **A target material** — tungsten anode to stop electrons

## Mechanism 1: Bremsstrahlung Radiation

**"Braking radiation"** — produced when electrons are deflected and decelerated by the nuclear electric field of target atoms.

### Process:
1. High-speed electron passes close to a tungsten nucleus
2. Coulomb attraction deflects the electron from its path
3. Kinetic energy lost during deceleration is emitted as an X-ray photon
4. Photon energy = energy lost by the electron (variable)

### Characteristics:
- Produces a **continuous spectrum** of energies
- Photon energies range from near zero up to **maximum = eV (kVp)**
- Most probable energy ≈ one-third of peak energy
- Accounts for **~80–90%** of X-ray output

## Mechanism 2: Characteristic Radiation

Produced when an incident electron ejects an inner-shell electron from a target atom, and an outer-shell electron drops down to fill the vacancy.

### Process:
1. Incident electron collides with and ejects a K-shell electron (requires energy &gt; K-shell binding energy ~70 keV for tungsten)
2. Vacancy in K-shell filled by an electron from L, M, or N shell
3. Energy difference emitted as a **characteristic X-ray photon**

### Characteristics:
- Produces **discrete (line) spectrum** — specific energies
- Energies characteristic of the target material (tungsten):
  - K-alpha: ~59 keV (L→K transition)
  - K-beta: ~67 keV (M→K transition)
- Accounts for **~10–20%** of output
- Only occurs above threshold kVp (~70 kVp for tungsten K-shell)

## The X-ray Spectrum

The combined output forms the **X-ray emission spectrum**:

| Feature | Bremsstrahlung | Characteristic |
|---|---|---|
| Spectrum type | Continuous | Discrete lines |
| Energy range | 0 to max kVp | Fixed values |
| Proportion | 80–90% | 10–20% |
| kVp dependence | Shifts with kVp | Fixed (material dependent) |

## Factors Affecting the X-ray Spectrum

### Effect of Increasing kVp
- Shifts spectrum to higher energies
- Increases maximum photon energy
- Increases total output (more and higher-energy photons)
- Reduces contrast (more Compton scatter relative to photoelectric)

### Effect of Increasing mAs
- Increases number of photons at all energies
- Does **not** change shape or peak energy of spectrum
- Proportionally increases radiation output

### Effect of Filtration
- Removes low-energy photons (beam hardening)
- Shifts effective (mean) energy higher
- Reduces patient skin dose
- Slightly reduces total output

### Effect of Target Material (Z)
- Higher Z → more efficient bremsstrahlung production
- Shifts characteristic lines to higher energies
- Tungsten (Z=74) optimal for general radiography
- Molybdenum (Z=42) used in mammography (lower energy characteristic lines)

## Efficiency of X-ray Production

X-ray production efficiency is very low:

**Efficiency ≈ 9 × 10⁻¹⁰ × Z × kVp**

- At 80 kVp with tungsten: ~0.5–1% efficiency
- Remainder (~99%) converted to **heat**
- This is why anode heat management is critical
