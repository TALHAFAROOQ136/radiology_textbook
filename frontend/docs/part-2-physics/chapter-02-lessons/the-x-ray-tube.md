# The X-ray Tube

The X-ray tube is the component that generates X-rays. It is a vacuum-sealed device that converts electrical energy into X-ray photons through the interaction of accelerated electrons with a metal target.

## Construction

### Glass or Metal Envelope
- Maintains vacuum (~10⁻⁶ mmHg) inside the tube
- Prevents electron scattering by air molecules
- Modern tubes use metal/ceramic envelopes for better heat dissipation

### Cathode Assembly
The negative electrode; source of electrons:

- **Filament**: Coiled tungsten wire heated by a low-voltage current
  - Thermionic emission: heating liberates electrons from the filament surface
  - Filament current (mA) controls electron emission rate
- **Focusing cup**: Negatively charged molybdenum cup that focuses the electron beam onto a small area of the anode (focal spot)
- **Dual filament**: Most tubes have two filaments — large and small focal spots

### Anode Assembly
The positive electrode; target for electrons:

| Feature | Stationary Anode | Rotating Anode |
|---|---|---|
| Use | Dental, portable units | General radiography, CT |
| Heat capacity | Low | High |
| Focal spot | Fixed area | Distributed over ring |
| Rotation speed | — | 3,000–10,000 rpm |

**Anode material**: Tungsten (W, Z=74)
- High melting point (3,422°C)
- High atomic number → efficient X-ray production
- Good thermal conductivity

**Rhenium-tungsten alloy**: Used in rotating anodes to prevent surface roughening under thermal stress.

## Focal Spot

The area on the anode where electrons strike and X-rays are produced:

- **Actual focal spot**: Physical area of electron bombardment
- **Effective focal spot**: Projected size seen from the image receptor (smaller due to anode angle)
- **Line focus principle**: Angling the anode face reduces effective focal spot size while maintaining a larger actual area for heat distribution

| Focal Spot Size | Application |
|---|---|
| 0.1–0.3 mm (fine) | Magnification radiography, mammography |
| 0.6–1.0 mm (small) | General radiography |
| 1.2–2.0 mm (large) | High-output procedures |

## Heat Management

Only ~1% of electron energy becomes X-rays; 99% becomes heat:

- **Heat units (HU)**: kVp × mA × time × generator factor
- **Anode heat capacity**: Maximum HU the anode can store
- **Cooling**: Radiation cooling (anode to oil to housing), some tubes have direct oil cooling
- **Tube housing**: Lead-lined to absorb leakage radiation; oil bath for cooling

## Tube Rating Charts
Before making an exposure, verify:
1. Single exposure rating — within limits for selected kVp/mA/time
2. Anode cooling chart — adequate time between exposures
3. Housing cooling chart — for high-volume work

## Tube Failure
Common causes:
- Vaporised tungsten deposits on glass envelope (arcing)
- Bearing failure in rotating anode
- Thermal cracking of anode disc
- Filament burnout (from excessive mA)
