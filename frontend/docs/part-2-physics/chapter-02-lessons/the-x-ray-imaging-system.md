# The X-ray Imaging System

The X-ray imaging system is an integrated assembly of components that work together to produce diagnostic radiographic images. Understanding each component and its function is essential for optimizing image quality and ensuring patient safety.

## Major Components

### 1. X-ray Generator
Controls the electrical supply to the X-ray tube:
- Converts AC mains supply to high-voltage DC
- Controls kVp, mA, and exposure time
- Types: single-phase, three-phase, high-frequency (most modern)

**High-frequency generators** are preferred because they:
- Produce nearly constant voltage (less ripple)
- Are compact and energy-efficient
- Allow precise exposure control

### 2. X-ray Tube
Produces X-rays from the interaction of electrons with the anode target (see Chapter: The X-ray Tube).

### 3. Collimator (Beam Restrictor)
Limits the X-ray beam to the area of clinical interest:
- Reduces patient dose by limiting field size
- Reduces scatter radiation, improving image contrast
- Types: cones, cylinders, adjustable light-beam diaphragms

### 4. Patient Support (Table)
- Carbon fibre tabletop — low attenuation, high strength
- Floating top allows easy patient positioning
- Bucky tray holds the image receptor beneath the table

### 5. Anti-scatter Grid
Placed between patient and detector to absorb scattered radiation:
- Improves image contrast
- Requires increased mAs to compensate
- Characterised by grid ratio (typically 8:1 to 12:1)

### 6. Image Receptor
Captures the transmitted X-ray pattern:

| Type | Technology |
|---|---|
| Film-screen | Silver halide film + intensifying screens |
| Computed radiography (CR) | Photostimulable phosphor plates |
| Digital radiography (DR) | Flat-panel detectors (direct or indirect) |

### 7. Image Display and Archiving
- PACS (Picture Archiving and Communication System) stores and distributes images
- DICOM format — universal standard for medical images
- Workstations with high-resolution monitors for image review

## System Geometry

### Source-to-Image Distance (SID)
- Standard SID: 100 cm (40 inches) for most projections
- 180 cm for chest radiography (reduces magnification)
- Longer SID → less magnification, sharper image

### Object-to-Image Distance (OID)
- Minimise OID to reduce magnification and blur
- Place area of interest closest to the detector

### Magnification Factor
**Magnification = SID / SOD**
(SOD = source-to-object distance)

## Exposure Factors Summary

| Factor | Primary Effect |
|---|---|
| kVp | Beam quality, penetration, contrast |
| mAs | Beam quantity, image density/noise |
| SID | Magnification, intensity (inverse square law) |
| Field size | Scatter, dose |
| Filtration | Beam hardening, skin dose reduction |
